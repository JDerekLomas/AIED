*   **Language Demands (Limited English Proficiency):**
    *   **Vocabulary:** "gardener," "collected," "waste of plants, vegetables and fruits," "pit," "notice any change," "some days," "reasons," "evaluate," "beneficial," "soil health."
    *   **Complexity:**
        *   "Does gardener notice any change after some days?" - Straightforward yes/no followed by explanation.
        *   "Give reasons" - Requires explanatory language.
        *   "evaluate if this is beneficial for soil health" - Requires a judgment and further explanation.
    *   **Challenges:**
        *   The phrase "evaluate if this is beneficial" might be slightly more complex than just "Is it good for soil?" but "beneficial" is a common enough term.
        *   The need to provide *reasons* and *evaluate* requires more than simple recall; it demands formulating an explanation in English.
        *   For students struggling with English, articulating the *why* (microorganisms, breakdown, nutrients) could be harder than just saying "yes, it changes."
    *   **Comparison:** The vocabulary is mostly accessible. The sentence structure is not overly complex. However, the multi-part nature of the question (notice change + reasons + evaluation) increases the writing burden in English.

    *   **"Does gardener notice any change?"**: Primarily recall (of the process of decomposition) and observation (if they've seen it happen).
    *   **"Give reasons"**: Application of knowledge about decomposition and the role of microorganisms. It requires understanding the mechanism.
    *   **"evaluate if this is beneficial for soil health"**: Application and reasoning. Students need to link the outcome of decomposition (humus, nutrients) to soil fertility.
    *   **Comparison:** This is not a simple recall question. It demands a multi-step response that involves recalling a concept, applying it to a specific scenario, explaining the underlying mechanism, and then making a judgment based on that understanding. This makes it more demanding than a pure recall question.

*   **Difficulty Compared to Typical Textbook Exercises:**
    *   Typical Class 8 exercises might ask: "What is composting?" (recall), "List two uses of microorganisms." (recall/list), "How does manure improve soil fertility?" (recall/explanation).
    *   This question combines these elements into a single, integrated problem. It's more open-ended and requires a more comprehensive answer than many fill-in-the-blanks or short-answer recall questions.
    *   The "pit" scenario is a practical, real-world application, which might resonate well, but the demand to *explain* and *evaluate* elevates its difficulty beyond a basic definition.

This significantly lowers the bar for getting a "correct" answer *based on the rubric*. If the student just says "Yes, the waste will change/rot," they get the 0.5 points.
