Here's a breakdown of the analysis for the question "Define fable":

**Contrastive Analysis:**

1.  **Prior Knowledge Assumed:**
    *   **Direct Knowledge:** Students need to know the *definition* of a "fable." This isn't something they'd necessarily infer; it's a specific literary term.
    *   **Implicit Knowledge (cultural context):** Many students, even with limited English, might be familiar with stories like "The Tortoise and the Hare" or "The Lion and the Mouse" in their local languages, which are fables. However, recognizing these *as fables* and being able to articulate the characteristics in English is a different hurdle. They might understand the *concept* without knowing the *term*.
    *   **English vocabulary:** "Short story," "animals," "characters," "imparts," "moral lesson." While "animals" and "story" are likely known, "imparts" and "moral lesson" (especially 'moral') might be less common for students with limited English.

2.  **Language Demands (Limited English Proficiency):**
    *   **Vocabulary:** As noted above, terms like "fable," "imparts," and "moral lesson" can be challenging. "Define" itself is a straightforward command, which is good.
    *   **Syntactic Complexity:** The ideal answer (a sentence or two defining the concept) requires basic sentence construction in English. It's not overly complex grammatically, but expressing the nuanced parts ("imparts a moral lesson") might lead to simplified or incorrect phrasing.
    *   **Expression:** Students need to translate a relatively abstract literary concept into clear English. They might know *what* a fable is, but struggle to *explain* it articulately. Many might resort to listing features rather than a flowing definition.

3.  **Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**
    *   This is primarily **recall** of a definition. There's no application or complex reasoning required beyond accessing the memorized or understood definition. If they've learned it in an English class, it should be a straightforward recall.

4.  **Comparison to Typical Textbook Exercises:**
    *   "Define X" questions are very common in textbooks, especially for literary terms or science concepts. Students are trained to recall definitions.
    *   However, "fable" as a specific term might not be universally covered in *every* Class 8 English curriculum in a way that emphasizes precise definition, especially if the focus is on reading comprehension rather than literary theory. Some curricula might introduce "story with animals and a lesson" without explicitly using the word "fable" consistently for definition exercises.
    *   Given that the average pass rate across items is 29%, definitions of terms that are somewhat abstract might trend lower, especially if they haven't been drilled.
