Let's break down the question and the challenges for a Class 6 student in an Indian government school, considering the stated difficulties:

**Question Breakdown:**

1.  **Initial Time:** 10 am
2.  **Initial Temperature:** 30°C
3.  **Rate of Increase:** 2°C per hour
4.  **Final Time:** 4:00 pm
5.  **Goal:** Find the temperature at 4:00 pm.

**Required Steps for a Student:**

1.  **Understand "10 am" and "4:00 in the evening/pm":** This requires understanding AM/PM and how time progresses across noon.
2.  **Calculate Time Elapsed:** From 10 am to 4 pm. This is the crucial step the rubric focuses on.
    *   10 am to 11 am = 1 hour
    *   11 am to 12 pm (noon) = 1 hour
    *   12 pm to 1 pm = 1 hour
    *   1 pm to 2 pm = 1 hour
    *   2 pm to 3 pm = 1 hour
    *   3 pm to 4 pm = 1 hour
    *   Total = 6 hours
    *   Alternatively, using subtraction with careful handling of 12-hour clock (4 + 12 = 16, then 16 - 10 = 6 hours). This is likely *more* difficult for this age group.
3.  **Calculate Total Temperature Increase:** 6 hours * 2°C/hour = 12°C.
4.  **Calculate Final Temperature:** 30°C + 12°C = 42°C.

**Challenges for the Target Student Population:**

*   **English Comprehension:**
    *   "At 10 am in the morning..." - might be redundant for a native speaker, but "in the morning" reinforces "am."
    *   "temperature increases by 2°C per hour" - "per hour" is key.
    *   "what will be the temperature... at 4:00 in the evening?" - "in the evening" reinforces "pm."
    *   Even if they know individual words, parsing the whole sentence to understand the relationship between time and temperature change can be hard.
*   **Time Calculation (Rubric Focus):** This is the biggest hurdle.
    *   **Crossing Noon:** Many students struggle with time calculations that cross the 12 pm mark. They might count 10, 11, 12, 1, 2, 3, 4 and get 7 hours by counting the numbers, not the intervals. Or they might forget that 12 pm is *noon* and not just "12 o'clock."
    *   **AM/PM Conversion:** While "morning" and "evening" are given, the transition from AM to PM is often a stumbling block.
    *   **Direct Subtraction:** 4 - 10 = -6 or 10 - 4 = 6, but they need to understand the cyclical nature of time. A student might do 10, 11, 12, 1, 2, 3, 4 = 7 numbers, so 7 hours. Or 10 to 12 is 2 hours, 12 to 4 is 4 hours, so 2+4 = 6 hours. This requires logical thinking about intervals.
*   **"Basic" Concepts aren't Basic:**
    *   Understanding "per hour" as a rate is a fundamental concept, but not always solidified.
    *   Even simple addition might be an issue for some struggling learners, especially under exam pressure.
*   **Exam Anxiety:** Can lead to misreading, forgetting steps, or simply freezing.
*   **Prior Practice:** "many students have never practiced this exact question type." This is crucial. If they haven't seen a problem explicitly requiring crossing noon in a time calculation, they are much less likely to derive it spontaneously.
