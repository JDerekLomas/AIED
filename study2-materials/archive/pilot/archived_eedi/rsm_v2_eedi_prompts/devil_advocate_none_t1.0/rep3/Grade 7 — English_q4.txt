**Analyzing Student Challenges:**

1.  **English Comprehension:**
    *   "Do you think..." - Fairly common.
    *   "...king's questions..." - Understandable, given the context.
    *   "...important in real life?" - This is the crucial part. "Important" is a basic word, but "in real life" requires a slight abstraction. Can they connect the *story* to their *own reality*?
    *   "Give one reason." - Straightforward instruction.

2.  **Conceptual Understanding:**
    *   The core of the question requires them to grasp the *significance* of the king's quest. Do they understand *why* knowing the right time/thing/people is important? This involves moving beyond simply recalling the questions to understanding their underlying value.
    *   The "right time to do everything, the right thing to do, and the right people to work with" are indeed fundamental life questions. But for a struggling learner, connecting these abstract ideas to a *reason* for their importance might be hard. They might just parrot the questions themselves without explaining *why* they matter.

3.  **Formulating a Reason:**
    *   This isn't a simple recall question. It demands a brief analytical response.
    *   Even if they understand *that* they are important, articulating a *reason* in English can be a barrier. Common pitfalls:
        *   Stating "Yes, they are important" without a reason.
        *   Restating the questions as the reason (e.g., "Because he wanted to know the right time"). While the rubric allows "any valid reason," this is a weak one that might struggle to meet the "validity" bar without further context.
        *   Lack of vocabulary to express the reason clearly (e.g., "to make good decisions," "to succeed," "to avoid problems").

4.  **Exam Anxiety/Novelty:**
    *   If they haven't practiced this type of "opinion + justification" question often, it will be harder.
    *   Anxiety can make them forget even simple reasons.
