**Challenges for the Target Student Population:**

1.  **English Language Barrier:**

    *   Phrases like "notice any change" might be understood, but articulating the *type* of change and its *reason* in English is a higher-order skill.
    *   "Evaluate" is a slightly more complex verb.

2.  **"Basic" Concepts Aren't Basic:**
    *   **Decomposition:** While students might have *heard* of it, truly understanding the role of microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) in breaking down organic matter might not be solidified. It's often taught conceptually, but applying it to this specific scenario might be difficult without direct experience or specific classroom practice.
    *   **Composting:** This specific process might have been covered in Environmental Science or Biology, but the depth of understanding and ability to articulate its benefits can vary greatly. Many students might only have a surface-level understanding.
    *   **Soil Health:** Connecting compost to "soil health" requires understanding concepts like nutrients, soil structure, water retention, etc., which might be abstract for some.

3.  **Lack of Practical Experience:** Many students in these backgrounds might not have seen composting in action, making it an entirely theoretical concept. Without concrete experience, describing the changes and benefits becomes harder.

4.  **Exam Anxiety/Question Type:**
    *   This is a multi-part question requiring descriptive answers and evaluation, not just a recall of facts. This can be intimidating.
    *   Students who are not confident in English might offer a very short, incomplete answer rather than risk errors in a longer response.

5.  **Curriculum Coverage and Retention:** While composting is likely in the curriculum, the quality of teaching, the amount of practice, and student retention vary wildly. A student who was absent or struggling during this specific topic's coverage would be completely lost.
