The question asks students to connect the king's abstract questions (about timing, actions, and people) to real-life relevance and provide a reason.

Let's break down the cognitive load:
1.  **Recall/Identify the king's questions**: This part is essentially recall from a previous text. Given the 50% pass rate for "What were the three questions the king wanted to answer?", a significant portion (half) struggled with even simple recall of the questions themselves. The prompt *here* implies that students *know* these questions (or have just answered them), but still, the first hurdle is recalling them correctly.
2.  **Relate to real life**: This requires an abstract connection between philosophical questions and practical daily existence.
3.  **Provide a reason**: This demands not just an assertion ("yes, they are important") but a justification. The justification can be simple, but it still requires some articulation.
4.  **Language Barrier**: The students' limited English proficiency is a major factor. Even if they understand the *concept* of why the questions are important, articulating a coherent reason in English is a separate challenge.
5.  **Lack of Critical Thinking Practice**: Many Indian government school curricula focus on rote memorization rather than open-ended, critical thinking questions that ask for personal opinion or justification. Students might be unfamiliar or uncomfortable with this type of question.
6.  **"Basic" but not truly basic**: For a struggling learner, connecting "the right time to do everything" to a personal "real life" scenario and then explaining *why* it's important can be quite challenging, even if the underlying concepts seem simple to an adult. For example, "it helps you make good decisions" or "it makes your life better" would be valid but require translation of an abstract thought into English.
