Here's my thought process for estimating the pass rate for the "Battle of Plassey" question:

1.  **Analyze the Question:** "How did the Battle of Plassey in 1757 contribute to the expansion of British power in India?" This requires not just knowing *about* the Battle of Plassey, but understanding its *consequence* in terms of British expansion, specifically in Bengal. It asks for a causal link.

2.  **Analyze the Rubric:** "For explaining that the victory at Plassey led to the establishment of British political dominance/control over Bengal (e.g., placing Mir Jafar as a puppet nawab)." This is a clear and relatively low bar: a simple, correct statement about political dominance/control in Bengal, potentially with an example.

3.  **Consider the Target Audience:** Class 8 Indian government school students, mostly EWS, limited English, 29% average pass rate. This means many struggle with basic comprehension and expressing ideas in English. They might know the *name* Battle of Plassey, and the *date*, but struggle with the "how did it contribute" part, especially the political/dominance aspect.

4.  **Examine Calibration Anchors:**

    *   **5% "The Last Bargain" theme:** This is abstract, requiring interpretation and articulation of a poetic theme. Very difficult for EWS students with limited English. The sample answers show basic literacy but not necessarily deep comprehension.
    *   **15% "Differentiate archaeological/literary sources":** This is a definition/differentiation task. It requires understanding specific terms and providing examples. The sample answers are brief but accurate. The conceptual understanding of "sources" might be present, but articulating the difference and examples in English is harder.
    *   **30% "My favorite season" paragraph:** This is a personal response, low-cognitive load, open-ended writing task. Even with the prompts (what you like to do, eat, wear), many struggle to form coherent sentences beyond bullet points. The 30% pass rate confirms the difficulty of *writing* for these students.
    *   **50% "Three questions the king wanted to answer":** This is recall of specific details from a story. It requires reading comprehension and recall, but not deep analytical thinking. The relatively high pass rate suggests recall of facts from a well-known story is more accessible. The full answer is a list of three distinct items.
    *   **66% "Two Fundamental Rights":** This is rote recall of two facts (names of rights). The options are usually limited, and often explicitly taught and drilled. This is one of the "easiest" recall tasks.

5.  **Compare the Plassey question to anchors:**

    *   **Less difficult than "Theme of Poem" (5%):** The Plassey question is factual, not abstract interpretation.
    *   **Similar/Slightly More Difficult than "Differentiate Sources" (15%):** While the Plassey question is historical explanation, it's not a *differentiation* task which requires comparing two things. It requires understanding a *consequence*. Students might know "Plassey -> British power," but articulating "dominance/control over Bengal" requires specific historical understanding and the ability to link cause/effect in a meaningful way. "Political dominance" is a more complex concept than "physical objects" vs "written."
    *   **More difficult than "My favorite season" (30%) in terms of content, but easier in terms of required length:** Plassey requires specific historical knowledge and conceptual understanding. "My favorite season" requires creativity and basic paragraph structure, which many struggle with.
    *   **More difficult than "Three questions the king wanted" (50%):** Plassey isn't just recalling three specific, short phrases from a story. It requires a cause-and-effect understanding.
    *   **Significantly more difficult than "Two Fundamental Rights" (66%):** Plassey isn't simple rote recall of names.

    *   The question requires specific historical knowledge (Battle of Plassey, 1757) and the ability to articulate its *consequence* regarding British power.
