**Average Pass Rate (General):** 29%
**Specific Question:** Arrange in ascending order: `3367, 5492, 736, 4799`
**Rubric:** For correctly identifying the smallest number (736) and placing it first. (Max score: 0.5)

---

**Analysis of Potential Errors:**

**1. Misconceptions about Mathematics:**

*   **Place Value (Biggest Misconception):** This is the *most critical* mathematical concept being tested. Students might struggle with comparing numbers with different numbers of digits.
    *   **Common Error:** Assuming a number with more digits is always larger. For example, they might see "736" as "smaller" because it has only three digits, while "3367" has four digits, and thus incorrectly assume "3367" is larger than "736". This is the inverse of what they should do for *ascending* order.
    *   **Related Error:** When comparing numbers with the same number of digits, they might only look at the first digit and not compare subsequent digits correctly. (Less likely to impact identifying *736* as smallest, but relevant for the full arrangement).
*   **Understanding "Ascending Order":**
    *   **Confusion with "Descending":** Students might correctly identify the smallest number but then arrange the rest in descending order, or try to arrange the entire set in descending order.
    *   **Lack of Conceptual Understanding:** They might know "ascending" means "small to big" but struggle to execute that comparison across all numbers.
*   **Copying Errors:** Simply writing the numbers down in the order they were given, or transposing digits while copying.

**2. Language Misunderstandings:**

*   **"Ascending Order":** This is a key mathematical term. While taught, for students with limited English, especially if they primarily learn in a regional language, this term might be a barrier. They might not fully grasp "small to big."
    *   **Possible Scenario:** They might recognize "order" but not "ascending," or confuse it with "descending" (as mentioned above).
*   **Word Recognition:** Less likely for numbers themselves, but the instruction "Arrange in ascending order" is crucial.

**3. Attempt vs. Non-Attempt:**

*   **High Attempt Rate:** This question involves numbers, which are often less intimidating than word problems. Even if they don't understand "ascending order" perfectly, most students will attempt to write the numbers down in *some* order.
*   **Partial Attempts:** Many will attempt but fail to correctly identify the smallest number due to the place value misconception. They might identify *some* order, but not the correct one.
*   **Not Attempting:** Less likely, unless they are completely overwhelmed by the language and refuse to engage with the numbers at all.

**4. Most Common Wrong Answer (for identifying smallest):**

Given the rubric focuses on identifying 736 as the smallest and placing it first:

*   **Most Common Wrong Answer:** Due to the "more digits = larger number" misconception, students are highly likely to pick `3367` as the smallest among the four-digit numbers and place it first, completely overlooking `736` as a smaller number. Or, they might list the numbers in the given order, or in descending order, starting with `5492`.

    *   **Specifically for identifying 736:** The most common error will be failing to identify it as the smallest because it has fewer digits. They might see it as "short" rather than "small in value."
    *   **Example of a common incorrect start:** `3367, 4799, 5492, 736` (if they assume 736 is largest because it starts with 7, or they just put it last as an outlier).
    *   **Another common incorrect start:** `3367, ...` (simply ignoring the 3-digit number or misjudging its value).
