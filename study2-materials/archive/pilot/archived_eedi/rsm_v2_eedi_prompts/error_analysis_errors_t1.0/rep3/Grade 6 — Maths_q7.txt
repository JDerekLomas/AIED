Let's break down the likely errors for Class 6 students in Indian government schools tackling this problem.

**Analysis of Likely Errors:**

**1. Misconceptions about Mathematics:**

*   **Counting Time Intervals (Crucial Error):** This is the single biggest mathematical hurdle.
    *   **"Subtracting without understanding intervals":** Many students might simply do 4 - 10 = -6 or 10 - 4 = 6, without correctly understanding that 4:00 PM is 16:00 in 24-hour format or that they need to count hours.
    *   **"End-point vs. Interval Counting":** They might count 10, 11, 12, 1, 2, 3, 4 and get 7 hours (counting both start and end points incorrectly) or miss one hour.
    *   **"AM/PM Confusion":** While the question states "morning" and "evening," the transition through 12 noon can be confusing. They might treat 1:00 PM as the next hour after 11:00 AM, skipping 12:00 PM mentally.
    *   **"Simple Subtraction":** Some might attempt 4 - 10 = -6 (ignoring AM/PM and just doing numerical subtraction).
*   **Multiplication/Addition Errors (Less Likely for *this specific step* but possible for the overall problem):** While the question is about finding the *time elapsed*, the subsequent steps (multiplying by 2 and adding to 30) are where other math errors could creep in. For *this specific rubric point* (calculating time elapsed), multiplication/addition isn't directly relevant.

**2. Language Misunderstandings:**

*   **"Per hour":** While common, some might misinterpret "per hour" as simply adding 2 once, or being confused about its iterative nature. (Less likely for the time calculation, more for the *next* step of the problem).
*   **"In the morning" / "In the evening":** This is crucial. If a student doesn't grasp the AM/PM distinction or the transition through 12, they'll struggle.
*   **"What will be the temperature":** This is clear, but the *process* to get there involves the time calculation.
*   **Overall sentence structure:** While relatively simple, the length and multiple pieces of information (initial temp, rate of change, start time, end time) can overwhelm some students with limited English comprehension. They might latch onto familiar numbers (10, 30, 2, 4) and try to combine them haphazardly.

**3. Attempt vs. Not Attempt:**

*   **Attempt but Fail:** Highly likely. Students will recognize the numbers and the request for a "temperature" and "time." They will try to do *something* with 10, 4, 30, and 2. The most common failure will be in accurately calculating the time elapsed.
*   **Not Attempt at All:** Less likely for this problem, as it has concrete numbers and a clear question. It's not abstract or purely conceptual. Students will likely try to perform some arithmetic.
