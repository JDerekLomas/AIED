Let's break down the question "Find the value of `y` in the equation: `y + 9 = 4`" for Class 8 Indian government school students.

**1. Prior Knowledge Assumed:**

*   **Numbers:** Basic understanding of integers (positive, negative, and zero). Crucially, the ability to work with negative results.
*   **Operations:** Addition and subtraction of integers.
*   **Algebraic Concepts:**
    *   What an "equation" is (a statement that two expressions are equal).
    *   What a "variable" is (a letter representing an unknown number).
    *   The concept of "finding the value of `y`" (isolating the variable).
    *   The inverse operation principle: To undo addition, you subtract; to undo subtraction, you add. This is typically introduced through balancing an equation (doing the same thing to both sides).

**2. Language Demands (with limited English proficiency):**

*   **"Find the value of..."**: This is a very common mathematical phrase. While a student might not literally translate "find" and "value," they would likely recognize this phrase as meaning "what is the number for y?".
*   **"in the equation"**: Again, "equation" is a common mathematical term often taught in English even in Hindi-medium schools, as are variable names like 'y'.
*   **Mathematical Symbols:** `+`, `=`, digits 4 and 9 are universally understood.

*   **Overall Language Difficulty:** The language is quite simple and standard mathematical English. It uses basic vocabulary directly related to the math concept. This is not a heavily text-based word problem. The most significant potential barrier related to language isn't the English words, but rather a lack of conceptual understanding of what "solving an equation" means, regardless of the language.

**3. Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**

*   **Application/Recall (leaning towards Application):** This is a direct application of a fundamental algebraic skill. While it requires recalling the procedure for solving a one-step linear equation, it's not rote recall of a fact. It's applying a learned algorithm.
*   **Minimal Reasoning:** There's very little complex reasoning involved. It's a procedural problem. The "reasoning" is mostly in understanding the inverse operations and balancing principle.

**4. How does it compare in difficulty to typical textbook exercises?**

*   **Easy End of Class 8 Algebra:** This is a *very* basic, one-step linear equation.
    *   Typical Class 8 textbooks would introduce equations of this form as the very first examples after introducing variables and the concept of an equation.
    *   Immediately after this, they would progress to `ax = b`, `x/a = b`, and then two-step equations like `2y + 5 = 11`. They also start involving variables on both sides, fractions, and decimals.
*   **The *only* point of potential difficulty beyond simple procedural knowledge is the negative answer.** In initial introductions, problems often yield positive integers. However, by Class 8, working with integers (positive and negative) is fundamental, and generating negative solutions should be common practice. Students should have extensively practiced integer addition and subtraction by then.

**Contrastive Analysis Summary:**

*   **Ease Factors:**
    *   One-step equation.
    *   Simple integers (no fractions, decimals).
    *   Clear and concise language with standard mathematical terms.
    *   Does not require setting up an equation from a word problem.
    *   Very direct application of a learned procedure.
*   **Hard Factors (relative to Class 8 math):**
    *   The only potential 'hard' factor is obtaining a negative integer as the answer (`-5`). Some students might try to avoid negative numbers, or make an arithmetic error like `9-4=5` or `4+9=13` instead of `4-9`.
    *   Lack of conceptual understanding of algebra itself if previous foundational topics were not grasped (e.g., what does 'y' mean, what does 'equals' mean in this context).
    *   A student might attempt trial and error with positive numbers first.
