Let's break down the question "Define fable" for Class 8 Indian government school students.

**Contrastive Analysis of "Define Fable"**

1.  **Prior Knowledge Assumed:**
    *   **Direct:**
        *   Understanding of the word "define" (though it's a relatively common instruction in academics).
        *   Familiarity with the concept of a "fable." This is the biggest hurdle. Fables (like Aesop's) are often introduced in earlier grades, usually in their native language or through simple English retellings. They might have *encountered* fables but not been *explicitly taught* the formal definition, especially in English.
        *   Basic vocabulary: "story," "animal," "character," "moral," "lesson." These are relatively common words for Class 8, even with limited English.
    *   **Indirect:** Understanding of narrative structures, allegories (though not needing the term itself).

2.  **Language Demands for Students with Limited English:**
    *   **Simplicity of "Define":** This is a fairly straightforward instruction. Unlike "describes," "write a theme," or "write a short paragraph (4-5 sentences) about...", "define" requires a specific, usually concise, type of answer. It's less open-ended than the common errors examples.
    *   **Target Vocabulary:** "Fable" itself is an English word. While they might understand the *concept* of stories with morals in their local language, associating it with the English word "fable" and then articulating its definition *in English* is the challenge.
    *   **Concision vs. Complexity:** The ideal answer is relatively concise. This works in their favor compared to needing to produce a longer, grammatically complex paragraph. However, expressing the *two core elements* (animal characters + moral lesson) accurately still requires some fluency.
    *   **Absence of Prompts:** There are no hints in the question itself to guide their definition, unlike some other English questions that might scaffold the task (e.g., "Mention what...").

3.  **Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**
    *   This is primarily a **recall** question. They either know the definition of "fable" or they don't.
    *   It also involves a low level of **application** in translating that recalled knowledge into coherent English.

4.  **Difficulty Compared to Typical Textbook Exercises:**
    *   **Textbook Exposure:** Fables are common content, so they've likely *read* fables in their textbooks (either in English or their native language with an English title/summary). However, dedicated *definition exercises* for terms like "fable" might be less common than comprehension questions *about* fables.
    *   **Memorization Focus:** If such definitions are taught, they are often memorized. This could be easier for some students than generating original ideas or lengthy responses.
    *   **Rubric Lenience:** The rubric is specific. They need "short story," "animals as characters," and "moral lesson." Even if they know *parts* of the definition, they need most of it to get a full score.
