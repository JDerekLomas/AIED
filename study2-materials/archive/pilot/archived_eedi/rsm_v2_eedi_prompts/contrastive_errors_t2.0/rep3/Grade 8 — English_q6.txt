Let's break down the question "Define fable" for Class 8 Indian government school students.

**Contrastive Analysis of "Define Fable":**

1.  **Prior Knowledge Assumed:**
    *   **Vocabulary:** Students need to know the English word "fable" itself. While fables are a universal story type (panchatantra stories, for example), the *term* "fable" in English might not be explicitly taught or encountered regularly in a formal definition context. They might have read or heard fables but not learned the explicit definition in English.
    *   **Concept of Definition:** They need to understand what it means to "define" something – to provide a concise explanation of its meaning, characteristics, or essence. This is a basic academic skill, but applying it in English can be tricky.
    *   **Story Elements:** Implicitly, they might need concepts like "story," "character," "animal," "moral lesson." These are common across languages, but articulating them in English is the challenge.

2.  **Language Demands on Students with Limited English:**
    *   **Vocabulary:** As mentioned, "fable" itself. If they don't know the word, they can't define it. Even if they know *a* word for it in their mother tongue, they need to know its English equivalent.
    *   **Productive Vocabulary:** They need to produce specific English terms like "short story," "animals as characters," "moral lesson," "teaches/imparts a lesson." These aren't extremely complex words, but combining them into a grammatically coherent definition can be hard.
    *   **Sentence Structure:** Constructing a single, clear, declarative sentence or a couple of related sentences to form a definition. This requires accurate subject-verb agreement and logical flow, which is a struggle for many students based on the common errors shown.
    *   **Abstract Concepts:** "Moral lesson" is an abstract concept. While they understand "right/wrong," expressing the idea of a "lesson" conveyed by a story can be harder than describing a concrete object.

3.  **Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**
    *   This is primarily a **recall** question. Students are expected to recall a specific definition of a literary term. There's very little application or reasoning involved beyond understanding what a definition is. If they haven't been taught this specific definition in English, they'll struggle significantly.

4.  **How Does It Compare in Difficulty to Typical Textbook Exercises?**
    *   **Potentially easier in some ways:** Compared to "Describe the poet’s writing style" (requires interpretation, analysis, and abstract vocabulary) or "Write a theme..." (requires higher-order thinking, inference, and synthesis), "Define fable" is a factual recall task. If they know the definition, it's straightforward.
    *   **Potentially harder in others:** Unlike questions that might be multiple-choice or gap-fills where clues are provided, this requires entirely *producing* the definition. There are no prompts beyond the single word "Define." The listed errors show a strong tendency towards empty responses or highly fragmented, non-responsive answers when productive English is required.
    *   **Comparison to "My Favourite Hobby":** "My Favourite Hobby" is an expository paragraph, requiring more sustained writing. "Define fable" is much shorter. However, the short answer common errors suggest that even short answers are challenging if the vocabulary isn't readily available and the task requires specific, academic language.
    *   **Relevance to Syllabus:** Fables are a common type of story introduced early. It's plausible that Class 8 students would have encountered the concept, but whether they've been taught to *define* it *in English* (and then asked to produce that definition) is key. Indian curricula often have sections on literary terms.
