Let's break down the question and rubric with the provided context in mind.

**Question:** "Which line from the poem do you like the most and why?"
**Rubric:** "Denotes a line from the poem and mentions why." (Max score: 1.5)

Here's an analysis considering the Class 6 Indian government school students from EWS background, limited English, average pass rate 29%, and the propensity of experts to overestimate:

1.  **Limited English Comprehension (Question Itself):**
    *   "Which line... do you like the most": This requires understanding "which," "line," "like," "most." "Like" is a common word, but "most" adds a comparative layer. "Line" might be misunderstood if not explicitly taught in the context of poetry.
    *   "and why?": This is the crucial part that separates a simple identification from an analytical response. "Why" is a basic question word, but generating a coherent *reason* in English is a significant hurdle.

2.  **Access to the Poem:** The question implicitly assumes the students *have the poem* in front of them, have *read* it, and *understood* at least some of it. If the poem itself is complex or uses vocabulary beyond their grasp, their ability to "like" a line and explain why diminishes significantly. We're not given the poem, so we have to assume *some* level of prior exposure/comprehension to *a* poem.

3.  **Concept of "Liking" a Line/Artistic Preference:**
    *   Do these students regularly engage with poetry in a way that encourages personal interpretation and expression of preference? Or is their English literature experience more focused on rote memorization and factual recall? Many students, especially in large classes, are trained to extract "correct" answers, not express subjective opinions.
    *   Explaining *why* one likes something requires internalizing an emotional or intellectual connection to a specific part of the text and then translating that into simple English.

4.  **"Basic" Concepts Aren't Basic:**
    *   While identifying a line and giving a reason seems basic to an English speaker, for someone struggling with the language, forming *any* sentence in English can be challenging, let alone a grammatically correct and semantically sound explanation for a subjective preference.
    *   "Because it's nice" or "Because it is true" might be the most complex they can manage. Does this count as "mentions why"? Probably, but it highlights the difficulty.

5.  **Exam Anxiety/Never Practiced This Type:**
    *   Many students might simply *freeze*. If they've never been asked to express their personal opinion about a poem in writing, they won't know how to start.
    *   The "and why?" component is the most likely stumbling block. Many might successfully "denote a line" (e.g., just write down a line from the poem) but then stop or write something completely unrelated for the "why."

6.  **The "Denotes a line from the poem":** Even this simple task isn't foolproof. They might copy the wrong line, copy part of a line, or try to paraphrase badly.

**Challenging this instinct:**

*   **Barely reads English:** A student who "barely reads English" would struggle immensely. Even understanding the *question* would be hard, let alone picking a line they genuinely connect with and explaining it. They might randomly pick a line or copy the first line. For "why," they'd likely leave it blank or write single, often inappropriate, words.
*   **Absent for the topic:** If they missed the class where the poem was introduced or discussed, they'd have no context for "liking" a line. They'd just see a collection of sentences.
*   **"Why?" difficulty:** This is the killer. Getting *any* explanation in English, even a basic one ("because it's happy," "it's good"), will be a significant achievement for many. Many will provide an explanation that is garbled, off-topic, or simply too minimal to count.
*   **The "Rubric" interpretation:** "Mentions why" is fairly broad. Does "I like this line because it's pretty" count? Probably. What about "This line is very"? Probably not.

Given the 29% average pass rate, this question asks for subjective interpretation and written expression, which are often *more* challenging than objective recall for these students.
