**3. Brainstorm Potential Errors (Based on Experience Marking Papers):**

*   **Misconceptions about Mathematics:**
    *   **Understanding "Ascending Order":** Some students might confuse "ascending" with "descending." This is a fundamental concept.
    *   **Place Value Errors:**
        *   Comparing numbers digit by digit from left to right, but making mistakes when numbers have different lengths (e.g., seeing 736 as "smaller than 3367" because it starts with 7 but 3367 starts with 3, *without* realizing it's a 3-digit vs 4-digit number). This is a common error.
        *   Ignoring the number of digits entirely, and just looking at the first digit (e.g., 736 vs 3367, seeing 7 > 3 and thinking 736 is larger). This is a severe place value misunderstanding.
        *   Incorrectly comparing digits within numbers of the same length (e.g., comparing 3367 and 4799, and thinking 3 is smaller than 4, which is correct, but then maybe getting confused if the subsequent digits were different in a way that reversed the order).
    *   **Simple Calculation Errors/Copying Errors:** Misreading a number, or writing it incorrectly. (Less likely to impact just finding the smallest, but possible for the full arrangement).
*   **Language Misunderstandings:**
    *   "Arrange": Unlikely to be a major issue, it's a common instruction.
    *   "Ascending order": This is the *most critical* language term. If they don't know "ascending" means smallest to largest, they will likely get it wrong (or reverse the order). However, even if they reverse it, they *might* still identify 736 as one of the "extremes" (either smallest or largest).
*   **Attempt vs. No Attempt:**
    *   This is a math question with clear numbers. Most students, even if confused, will likely *attempt* something. Complete non-attempts are less probable than for an abstract English question.
*   **Most Common Wrong Answer (related to finding the smallest):**
    *   **736 identified as largest:** If they confuse ascending with descending.
    *   **One of the 4-digit numbers identified as smallest:** This would happen if they fail to recognize that a 3-digit number is always smaller than a 4-digit number. This is a very common place value error at this level. For example, they might pick 3367 because it starts with a '3' which seems smaller than '7' (from 736) or '5' (from 5492).
