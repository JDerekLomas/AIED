**Likely Errors and Misconceptions:**

1.  **Misunderstanding "writing style":**
    *   Students might interpret "style" as referring to the poem's *theme*, *message*, or *content* rather than the *how* it is written.
    *   They might confuse it with the *type* of poem (e.g., "It's a poem about nature" or "It's a sad poem").
    *   They might think it refers to the poet's *personal life* or *reputation*.
2.  **Lack of appropriate vocabulary:**
    *   Even if they understand "writing style" conceptually, they may lack the specific English adjectives needed to describe it (e.g., "concise," "evocative," "metaphorical," "lyrical," "simple," "complex," "flowery," "direct").
    *   They might try to describe it with simpler words that aren't quite accurate or specific enough (e.g., "good," "nice," "beautiful").
3.  **Difficulty extracting and synthesizing information:** To describe a style, one needs to analyze the language used in the poem (word choice, sentence structure, imagery, tone). This is a higher-order thinking skill.
4.  **Limited exposure to literary analysis terms:** These students are unlikely to have extensive training in literary criticism or even basic terms used to describe writing.
5.  **Attempt vs. Failure:** Many students might attempt this, but their answers would likely be off-topic (describing content/theme) or use very generic, non-specific words that don't meet the rubric's "suitable and relevant" criteria. Some might leave it blank if they truly don't understand the question.
6.  **Common Wrong Answers:**
    *   "The poem is about..." (describing theme/content)
    *   "The poet writes beautifully/nicely/well." (too generic)
    *   "The poem is easy to understand." (getting closer to "simple" but might not be phrased correctly or specifically enough to earn a point depending on how strict "relevant word" is interpreted).
    *   "The poet is famous/Indian." (irrelevant information about the poet)
