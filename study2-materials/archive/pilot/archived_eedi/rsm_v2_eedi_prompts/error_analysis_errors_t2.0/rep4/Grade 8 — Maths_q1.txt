**Analysis of Errors:**

**1. Misconceptions about Mathematics:**

*   **Fundamental lack of understanding of inverse operations:** This is the biggest hurdle. Many students, especially at this level in an EWS background, might struggle with the concept that to isolate 'y', you need to do the *opposite* of what's happening to 'y'. They might see '+9' and think they need to *add* 9 to the other side, or not know what to do at all.
*   **Confusion with positive and negative integers:** Even if they attempt subtraction, the concept of subtracting a larger number from a smaller number to get a negative result is often a point of confusion. Many might not have a firm grasp of integer operations. They might arrive at `9 - 4 = 5` and then assume `y = 5`.
*   **Order of operations (though less relevant here):** While BODMAS/PEMDAS is often taught, the idea of "doing the same thing to both sides" to maintain balance in an equation might not be firmly established.

**2. Language Misunderstandings:**

*   **"Find the value of 'y'":** While "find the value" is standard, some might not immediately connect it to *solving* the equation. However, this is less likely to be the primary blocker given the simple nature of the equation.
*   **Lack of English proficiency overall:** While the mathematical symbols are universal, the instruction itself is in English. If they struggle significantly with English, it adds another layer of cognitive load before they even tackle the math. This could lead to a feeling of being overwhelmed.

**3. Attempt vs. Not Attempt:**

*   **Attempt but fail:** This is highly probable. The equation looks simple enough that most students will attempt *something*. The numbers are small and straightforward. It's not a complex problem that would intimidate them into not trying.
*   **Not attempt at all:** Less likely for this specific question. It's a very basic linear equation. Only the most disengaged or truly lost students would leave it blank.

**4. Most Common Wrong Answer:**

*   **`y = 5` (by subtracting 4 from 9 or by adding 9 and 4):**
    *   **Reasoning for `9-4=5`:** This is very common. Students focus on the larger number, subtract the smaller, and drop the negative sign or misinterpret the operation needed. They might incorrectly apply the "subtract 9 from both sides" but then fail to handle `4-9`. Instead, they might just calculate `9-4`.
    *   **Reasoning for `9+4=13`:** Some might just see '+9' and '4' and perform addition, not understanding the goal is to isolate 'y'.
*   **`y = 13`:** This indicates a simple addition error where they add 9 and 4.
*   **`y = -13`:** Less likely, but possible if they try to subtract from the 4.

**Likely Thought Process for `y = 5` (Most Common Wrong Answer):**

Student sees `y + 9 = 4`.
They need to isolate `y`.
They might think, "I need to get rid of the 9."
They subtract 9 from both sides: `y + 9 - 9 = 4 - 9`.
This simplifies to `y = 4 - 9`.
At this point, many will falter with negative numbers. Instead of `4 - 9 = -5`, they might switch it to `9 - 4 = 5` because that feels "right" (larger minus smaller).
Alternatively, some might simply move the 9 over and "change its sign" to subtraction, but then still handle the `4-9` as `9-4`.
