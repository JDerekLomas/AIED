Let's break down the potential pitfalls for Class 7 students in Indian government schools facing this question.

**Analysis of Potential Errors:**

1.  **"Describes" vs. "Describe":**
    *   **Misconception:** Many students at this level, especially with limited English exposure, struggle with the nuances of verb tenses and forms. "Describes" (present simple, 3rd person singular) might be perceived as a prompt to *list* things the poet writes *about*, rather than to *provide a description*. They might interpret it as "What does the poet describe?" or "What kind of things are described in the poet's writing?"
    *   **Language Misunderstanding:** The slight difference in "describes" vs. "describe" could be a critical misunderstanding for those still developing their grammatical sense. They might over-focus on "poet's writing" and miss the nuance of "describes the *writing style*."

2.  **"Writing Style":**
    *   **Core Misconception:** This is likely the biggest hurdle. "Writing style" is an abstract concept. For someone new to literary analysis and with limited vocabulary, "style" might simply mean "how they write" in the most literal sense.
        *   They might confuse "style" with the *content* or *theme* of the poem (e.g., "The poet writes about nature," "The poet writes sad poems").
        *   They might confuse "style" with the *effect* of the poem (e.g., "The poet writes beautiful poems," "The poet writes confusing poems").
        *   They might think of "style" as the *genre* (e.g., "The poet writes stories," though for Class 7, "poet" usually implies poetry).
    *   **Vocabulary Limitations:** Even if they grasp "style," they might lack the specific English vocabulary to describe it concisely (e.g., "simple," "complex," "lyrical," "metaphorical," "free verse"). They might try to describe it in a longer, convoluted way that doesn't fit a one-word answer.

3.  **Attempt vs. Not Attempt:**
    *   **Not Attempt:** Some students, completely lost on "writing style" or "describes," might not attempt it at all. This is less likely if there's *any* word recognition (e.g., "poet," "writing"), but for truly weak students, it's possible.
    *   **Attempt but Fail:** This will be the most common scenario. Most students will try to answer *something*, even if it's based on a misinterpretation. The questions "What does this mean?" or "What do I need to write?" will lead to attempts.

4.  **Most Common Wrong Answer:**
    *   **Focus on Content/Theme:** "He writes about nature," "He writes about love," "He describes birds." (Directly answering "What does the poet describe?").
    *   **Focus on Emotion/Effect:** "He writes sad poems," "His writing makes me happy," "It is a good writing."
    *   **Restating the Question with "is":** "The poet's writing style is writing poems." (Redundant, shows no understanding of "style").
    *   **Generic Positive/Negative:** "Good," "Bad," "Beautiful," "Nice," "Hard to understand." While "hard to understand" could arguably relate to style (complex), without "complex" or similar, it's vague. "Good" or "beautiful" describe the quality, not the style.
    *   **Focus on the *process* of writing:** "The poet writes with a pen," "He writes carefully." (Literal interpretation).
    *   **Grammatical errors:** Even if they pick a potentially correct word, it might be misspelled or used in a grammatically incorrect sentence that renders it invalid.
