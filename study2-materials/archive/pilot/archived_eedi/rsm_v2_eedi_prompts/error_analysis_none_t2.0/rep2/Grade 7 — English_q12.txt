**Likely Errors and Misconceptions:**

1.  **Limited Understanding of Voice (Active/Passive):**
    *   Many students might not have a clear conceptual understanding of "active" and "passive" voice, even if it's been taught. The abstract nature of grammar concepts can be challenging.
    *   They might recognize that *something* needs to change, but not *what* or *how*.

2.  **Vocabulary/Grammar for Passive Voice Transformation:**
    *   **Identifying the Object:** While "the leaves" is relatively clear, some might struggle to isolate it as the object that needs to become the new subject.
    *   **Auxiliary Verb 'to be':** This is a major hurdle.
        *   They might forget to use 'be' or 'were'.
        *   They might use the wrong form of 'be' (e.g., "The leaves is blown away").
        *   They might stick 'was' or 'were' in an incorrect position.
    *   **Past Participle:**
        *   While "blown" is somewhat common, some might default to "blewed" (regularizing an irregular verb), or simply keep "blew." This is a very common error with irregular verbs.
        *   They might know it's a past form, but use the simple past ("blew") instead of the past participle ("blown").
    *   **Preposition 'by':** Many will forget or misuse "by."
    *   **Subject/Agent Shifting:** Understanding that the original subject ("the wind") moves to the end, preceded by "by," can be complex.

3.  **Language Misunderstandings/Word-for-Word Translation:**
    *   Students might try to translate the structure directly from their native language (often Hindi or regional languages), which can lead to awkward or ungrammatical English.
    *   They might rearrange words randomly hoping it sounds "different."

4.  **Cognitive Load / Memory Recall:**
    *   Even if they were taught the rules, recalling all the specific components (identify object, use appropriate 'be' form, use past participle, add 'by' + agent) under exam pressure is difficult.

5.  **Focus on "Changing Something":**
    *   A common strategy when unsure is to just change *something* in the sentence, any word, or word order, rather than apply a specific grammatical rule.

**Would students attempt but fail, or not attempt at all?**

*   **Most will attempt.** Leaving a blank would be seen as completely failing, whereas attempting shows effort. They will write *something*.

**What would the most common wrong answer look like?**

Based on the errors described:

1.  **Incorrect Past Participle or Tense:**
    *   "The leaves *blewed* away by the wind." (Most common with irregular verbs)
    *   "The leaves *blew* away by the wind." (Retaining original tense)

2.  **Missing or Incorrect Auxiliary 'be' verb:**
    *   "The leaves blown away by the wind." (Very common, often forgetting 'were')

4.  **Combinations of the above:**
    *   "The leaves *blew* away by the wind." (Missing 'were' and incorrect past participle)
