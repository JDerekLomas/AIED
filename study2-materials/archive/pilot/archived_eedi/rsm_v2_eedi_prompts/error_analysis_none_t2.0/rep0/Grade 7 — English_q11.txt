**Analysis of Likely Errors:**

1.  **Misconceptions about "Phrase":** This is the biggest hurdle.
    *   Many students, especially at an average pass rate of 29%, will not know what an "adjective phrase" is. They might only be familiar with single adjectives. The concept of a *phrase* as a group of related words without a subject-verb pair is often not well understood or reinforced in early grammar education.
    *   **Common misunderstanding:** They might interpret "phrase" as "any group of words" or even just "any important word."

2.  **Limited Vocabulary/Understanding of "Adjective":**
    *   While "tiny" is a simple adjective, students might struggle to consistently identify *all* adjectives, or get confused if it's mixed with other parts of speech. However, in this simple sentence, "tiny" is fairly clear.
    *   **Language misunderstanding:** They might know a few common adjectives but lack the meta-understanding of *what an adjective does* (describes a noun).

3.  **Confusion with Other Parts of Speech/Phrases:**
    *   **Noun Phrase:** Students might identify "A tiny bird" as the "phrase," perhaps associating "tiny bird" more strongly than just "tiny." Or even just "bird."
    *   **Verb Phrase:** Less likely here, as there isn't an obvious verb "phrase." They might pick "built" or "built its nest."
    *   **Prepositional Phrase:** "in our balcony" is a very common type of phrase they might encounter. This is a highly probable wrong answer as "identifying phrases" is a common exercise. Many students might jump to the *first* obvious phrase they see or remember.
    *   **Single Adjective vs. Adjective Phrase:** The rubric states identifying "tiny" as an adjective is correct. This is key. If the question was strictly about "adjective *phrase*," and the expected answer was more complex (e.g., "a very tiny bird"), then *no* student would get this correct, as "tiny" on its own isn't technically a *phrase*. However, given the simplicity and the rubric's acceptance of "tiny," it suggests they're simplifying "adjective phrase" for this level, or expecting them to understand that "tiny" functions *as* the adjective *within* a larger conceptual phrase. *But most students won't even think that deeply.* They'll either find "tiny" and be correct, or miss it completely.

This would lead to non-attempts or random guesses.

*   A student needs to know what an "adjective" is.
