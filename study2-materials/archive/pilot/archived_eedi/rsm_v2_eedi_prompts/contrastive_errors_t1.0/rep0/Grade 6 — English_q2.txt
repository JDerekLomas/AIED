Let's break down the question "Who lived inside the book?" for Class 6 students in Indian government schools, considering the provided context.

**Contrastive Analysis:**

*   **Prior Knowledge Assumed:**
    *   **Implicit Comprehension of a Story:** The question *assumes* students have read a specific story where a character lived inside a book. Without having read the story, it's impossible to answer. This is the single biggest barrier. If the story itself was too complex or not properly understood, the question becomes unanswerable even if the English is simple.
    *   **Basic Vocabulary:** "Who," "lived," "inside," "the," "book." These are fundamental, high-frequency words typically introduced early in English language learning.
    *   **Basic Sentence Structure:** Subject-verb-object (or in this case, a "wh-" question asking for the subject). This is also foundational.

*   **Language Demands on Students with Limited English:**
    *   **Vocabulary:** The vocabulary is very simple and concrete. "Book" is a common object. "Lived" is a common verb. "Inside" is a common preposition. "Who" is a common interrogative pronoun.
    *   **Sentence Structure:** The question structure is straightforward (Wh- + verb + prepositional phrase). It's not a complex compound or complex sentence.
    *   **Response Format:** The expected response ("a magical fairy" or "fairy") is also quite simple – typically a noun phrase. It doesn't require a full sentence or elaborate explanation.
    *   **Comparison to Common Errors:**
        *   The common errors cited ("Describes the poet’s writing style," "Write a theme or central idea," "Write a short paragraph") all require *production* of more complex language, understanding of abstract concepts (writing style, theme), or extended writing.
        *   "Who lived inside the book?" is a factual recall question requiring a very short, concrete answer. It avoids the pitfalls of explaining abstract ideas or generating multiple sentences.
        *   The common "Empty Response" suggests a complete lack of comprehension or inability to formulate any answer. This question is less likely to lead to an "Empty Response" if the story was understood, because the answer is so direct.
        *   The "I am reading" or "My hobby is cricket play" errors demonstrate an inability to correctly answer the specific question asked, often defaulting to a simpler, known phrase or a tangential answer. This question, being very direct, reduces the likelihood of such tangential responses *if the story is known*.

*   **Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**
    *   This is purely a **recall** question. Students need to remember a specific piece of information (who the character was) from the story. It requires no application of rules or complex reasoning beyond simple comprehension.

*   **Comparison to Typical Textbook Exercises:**
    *   This type of "who/what/where/when" factual recall question is *extremely common* in early English textbooks after reading a story. It's often the most basic level of comprehension check.
    *   It is significantly easier than tasks like describing a poet's style, writing a theme, or generating a paragraph, which are more advanced comprehension and production tasks.

**Factors Making it Easier:**

1.  **Simple Vocabulary:** All words are highly frequent and concrete.
2.  **Simple Sentence Structure:** Direct "wh-" question.
3.  **Factual Recall:** Requires remembering a specific detail, not abstract interpretation or elaboration.
4.  **Short, Concrete Answer:** "Fairy" is a straightforward noun.
5.  **Lack of Abstract Concepts:** No need to understand "style," "theme," or "bargain."
6.  **Direct Question:** Less room for misinterpretation of the question itself, compared to open-ended tasks.

1.  **Dependency on Story Comprehension:** If the *story itself* (where a fairy lived in a book) was too difficult, not read, or not understood, then the question becomes impossible. This is the biggest single variable.
2.  **"Magical Fairy" vs. Just "Fairy":** While the rubric accepts "fairy," if the story specifically used "magical fairy," students might struggle to recall the full descriptor or simplify it. However, "fairy" itself is a common and often visually associated character for children.
