Let's break down the potential errors for Class 7 students in Indian government schools, considering their background and the nature of the question.

**Analysis of Potential Errors:**

1.  **"Describes" Misunderstanding:**
    *   **Misconception:** Students might interpret "describes" as "to write something about" rather than "to characterize or define." They might feel compelled to *produce* a description of the style, rather than *identify* a word that describes it.
    *   **Language Misunderstanding:** "Describes" itself is a fairly common verb, but the nuance of "describing the *style*" might be lost. They might focus on *what* the poet describes in their poems, rather than *how* they describe it.
    *   **Common Wrong Answer:** Instead of a single word, they might write a short phrase about what the poet writes about (e.g., "He writes about nature," "He writes about feelings"). Or they might struggle to find a single adjective.

2.  **"Poet's Writing Style" Misunderstanding:**
    *   **Misconception:** This is the biggest hurdle. "Writing style" is an abstract concept. Many Class 7 students, especially those not exposed to extensive literary analysis, might not have a clear understanding of what "style" refers to in a literary context. They might confuse it with:
        *   **Content/Theme:** What the poet writes *about*.
        *   **Form/Structure:** Whether it's a poem, story, etc., or specific poetic devices (which they likely haven't studied in depth).
        *   **Their own personal liking:** "I like his writing."
    *   **Language Misunderstanding:** While "writing" and "style" are common words, their combination here creates a specific academic term that might be beyond their current vocabulary/conceptual understanding.     *   **Common Wrong Answer:**
        *   "He writes good poems." (Evaluating the quality, not describing the style).
        *   "He writes about trees and animals." (Describing the content).
        *   "He writes stories." (Confusing genre).
        *   "His poems are nice." (Personal opinion).

3.  **Vocabulary Deficit for "Suitable and Relevant Word":**
    *   **Misconception:** Even if they grasp "writing style," finding the *right* adjective to describe it can be challenging. Words like "simple," "descriptive," "informal," "direct" are relatively common, but abstract adjectives are often harder to recall or use appropriately than concrete nouns or verbs.
    *   **Language Misunderstanding:** They might know what "simple" means, but connecting it specifically to *writing style* requires a leap.
    *   **Common Wrong Answer:** They might use vague adjectives like "good," "nice," "beautiful," or "interesting," which describe the *effect* or *quality* rather than the specific *style*. Or they might try to use a word that isn't an adjective (e.g., "Poem").

4.  **Attempt vs. Non-Attempt:**
    *   Given the prompt is short and directly asks for something, most students will likely *attempt* to write something.
    *   However, many attempts will be incorrect because they fundamentally misunderstand what is being asked.
    *   Some, facing the abstract nature of "writing style," might write "I don't know" or leave it blank if they are truly stumped and don't want to guess wildly. But the "pressure to write something" is usually high.

**Likely Most Common Wrong Answers:**

*   **Content-focused:** "He writes about nature/love/feelings."
*   **Quality-focused:** "Good," "Nice," "Beautiful," "Interesting."
*   **Genre confusion:** "Poem," "Story."
*   **Vague positive:** "He writes well."

*   **Translation Issues:** Even if they try to translate "writing style" mentally, the direct translation might not evoke the same meaning as in English literary criticism.
