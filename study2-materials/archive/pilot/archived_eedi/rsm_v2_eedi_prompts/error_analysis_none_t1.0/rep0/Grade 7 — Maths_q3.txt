**Analyzing Likely Errors:**

*   **Forgetting the "1/2" in the formula:** This is arguably the most common mathematical error. Students might remember "base × height" but forget the factor of one-half, especially if they confuse it with the area of a rectangle.
*   **Confusing area with perimeter:** Less likely for a specific "area of a triangle" question, but some students might add the numbers or try to find a "boundary" if the concept of area isn't firmly grasped.
*   **Incorrect multiplication:** Even if they have the formula, basic multiplication errors (e.g., 8 * 6 = 42 instead of 48) could occur.
*   **Units confusion/omission:** Students might not write the correct units (cm²) or omit units entirely, though the rubric focuses on the formula itself.

**2. Language Misunderstandings:**

*   **"Find the area":** While "area" is a fundamental term taught in mathematics, some students, especially those with very limited English exposure, might struggle with this precise phrasing. However, for Class 7, it's generally a known term in a math context.
*   **"base" and "height":** These are specific geometric terms. While taught, the English terms themselves might be less familiar than their regional language equivalents, potentially causing a slight cognitive load or brief confusion if they don't immediately translate.
*   **"whose base is...":** The possessive "whose" might be a minor linguistic hurdle for some, but context usually makes it clear.

**3. Attempt vs. Non-Attempt:**

*   **Attempt but fail:** This will be the most common scenario. Most students would recognize it as a geometry problem and attempt to apply a formula. Their failure would likely stem from incorrect formula recall or calculation errors.
*   **Not attempt at all:** A smaller proportion of students might not attempt if they have absolutely no idea about the area of a triangle or are intimidated by the English phrasing. This group would likely have very low math proficiency overall.

**4. Most Common Wrong Answer:**

*   **"48" (without units or with incorrect units):** This would be the most prevalent wrong answer, resulting from `base × height` without the `1/2`.

However, for a *wrong* answer, if they made a multiplication error (e.g., 8 * 6 = 24 by mistake), it's less likely.
*   **Adding the numbers: "14"**: Less likely for area, more for perimeter, but a basic misconception.
