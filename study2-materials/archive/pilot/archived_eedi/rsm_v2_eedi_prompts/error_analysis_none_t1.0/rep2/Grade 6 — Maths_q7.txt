Let's break down the potential pitfalls for a Class 6 student in an Indian government school, given the context.

**Analysis of Errors:**

**1. Misconceptions about Mathematics:**

*   **Difficulty with time calculations:** This is the most significant mathematical hurdle.
    *   **Direct subtraction (4 - 10):** Students might try to subtract 10 from 4 directly, leading to a negative number or simply confusion. They haven't typically learned about negative numbers in this context, or how to handle time across AM/PM.
    *   **Ignoring AM/PM transition:** They might calculate 10 am to 4 pm as 4 hours (thinking 10, 11, 12, 1, 2, 3, 4 = 7 hours, or just going 10-4= -6, ignoring it).
    *   **Counting on fingers incorrectly:** While they might attempt to count on their fingers, errors in counting from 10 am to 4 pm are highly probable.
        *   "10 to 11 is 1, 11 to 12 is 2, 12 to 1 is 3, 1 to 2 is 4, 2 to 3 is 5, 3 to 4 is 6." This is the correct method, but it requires careful execution.
        *   A common mistake could be counting 10 and 4 as separate points without understanding the "between" concept.
    *   **Ignoring the "per hour" concept for multiplication:** Once they calculate the hours, some might add 2 to the initial temperature instead of multiplying 2 by the number of hours.
*   **Order of Operations (less likely to be the primary error):** While they might struggle with the overall problem, the sequence of operations (calculate hours, then multiply by change per hour, then add to initial temperature) is relatively straightforward once the hours are found.
*   **Basic arithmetic errors:** Even if they get the steps right, simple multiplication or addition errors are always a possibility (e.g., 6 * 2 = 10, or 30 + 12 = 40).

**2. Language Misunderstandings:**

*   **"Temperature increases by... per hour":** While "increases" is clear, "per hour" might be interpreted as "add 2 once" rather than "add 2 for each hour."
*   **"at 4:00 in the evening":** "Evening" clarifies PM, but some students might not automatically convert 4:00 in the evening to 16:00 or understand it as 4 PM, especially if they are more accustomed to 12-hour clock with AM/PM stated explicitly. *   **Most students would attempt this question.** It has numbers and a clear "what will be..." structure, which usually prompts an attempt.
*   **Partial attempts:** Many would likely attempt to calculate the initial increase (30 + 2 = 32), or calculate *some* duration of time, even if incorrect.
*   **Blank answers:** A smaller proportion might leave it blank if they are completely overwhelmed by the time calculation.
