This question is a classic test of "order of operations" in reverse when solving linear equations. 

**Tom's method** follows the standard "isolate the term" approach: adding 5 to both sides ($12 + 5 = 17$). Most students find this intuitive as it deals with the constant first.

**Katie's method** is a common trap. Students often think they can multiply by the denominator (3) at any time. However, to correctly multiply by 3, they must multiply *every* term. Katie has multiplied $\frac{x}{3}$ and $12$, but failed to multiply the $-5$. Her line should have been $x - 15 = 36$.

**Analysis of student performance:**
*   **Below Basic:** Many will see "36" and think "3 times 12 is 36, that looks right," leading them to choose **C** or **B**. They struggle to identify the specific error in distributive property.
*   **Basic:** These students usually know how to solve the equation themselves using Tom's method. However, they are susceptible to "verification bias"â€”seeing Katie's "36" and assuming she did something correct that they simply didn't think of.
*   **Proficient:** These students recognize that Katie didn't multiply the 5. They are confident in Tom but can spot the specific algebraic error in Katie's logic.
*   **Advanced:** Almost all will correctly identify that only Tom followed the rules of equality.
