### Contrastive Analysis of "Define fable."

**1. Prior Knowledge Assumed:**

*   **Positive:** The concept of fables is often introduced in primary school (Class 3-5) through stories like those from Panchatantra or Aesop's Fables. Even if not explicitly called "fables," these moralistic animal stories are a cornerstone of early education in India. Students would likely have *encountered* many fables.
*   **Negative:** While students may have heard *of* fables or stories that are fables, they might not have learned the formal *definition* or the English term itself. Their understanding might be implicit rather than explicit. The ability to articulate the definition rather than just recognize examples is key.
*   **Neutral:** They would likely understand the basic concepts of "story," "animal," and "lesson/moral."

**2. Language Demands (for students with limited English):**

*   **Word "Define":** This is a fairly common instructional verb. Most students would understand they need to *explain what it is*. This isn't particularly difficult.
*   **Word "fable":** This is the core vocabulary word.
    *   **Exposure:** Students in Hindi-medium schools might not encounter this word frequently in their daily academic English. They might learn it as part of an English textbook unit on stories or literature.
    *   **Phonetics/Cognates:** There isn't an obvious Hindi cognate, which means direct transliteration or prior intuitive understanding is unlikely without direct instruction.
    *   **Conceptual Clarity:** The word itself is not abstract, but if they haven't learned it explicitly, it's just a new word for them.
*   **Expected Output (English expression):** To score a point, they need to formulate a grammatically acceptable English sentence or two that captures the essence.
