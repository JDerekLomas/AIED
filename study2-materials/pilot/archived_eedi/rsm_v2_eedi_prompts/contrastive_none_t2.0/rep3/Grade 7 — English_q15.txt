    *   **Grammar/Structure:** Basic sentence formation (Subject-Verb-Object), tenses (simple present for stating hobby, habitual present for when they do it, expressing feelings), conjunctions (e.g., 'and,' 'because').     *   **"Why you enjoy it"**: Requires expressing feelings or benefits (e.g., "it makes me happy," "I learn new things," "it helps me relax").

2.  **Language Demands (for students with limited English):**
    *   **Reading Comprehension:** Understanding the prompt itself. The core terms like "paragraph," "hobby," "favourite," "when," "why," "enjoy" need to be grasped.     *   **Writing Production:**
        *   **Lexical Gaps:** Students might know their hobby in their mother tongue but struggle to find the precise English equivalent (e.g., *patang udana* for 'kite flying,' *kho-kho khelna* for 'playing Kho Kho').         *   **Syntactic Challenges:** Forming grammatically correct sentences in English, especially when linking ideas with 'why' clauses ("I enjoy it *because*..."). Subject-verb agreement, correct tense usage.
        *   **Cohesion:** Connecting 4-5 sentences to form a coherent paragraph, rather than just disconnected sentences.
        *   **Spelling:** Common words are likely manageable, but specific vocabulary related to their unique hobby could be challenging.
        *   **Idea Generation vs. Expression:** They might have strong ideas about their hobby but struggle significantly to express them simply and correctly in English.
    *   **Direct Translation Trap:** Students might try to directly translate Hindi sentence structures, leading to awkward or incorrect English.

3.  **Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**
    *   Primarily **Application**. It requires applying known vocabulary and grammatical structures to a personal context.
    *   It's **not recall** of specific factual information.
    *   It requires minimal **reasoning** (identifying *why* they enjoy it), but the main cognitive load is on language production.

4.  **Comparison to Typical Textbook Exercises:**
    *   **Similarities:** "My Hobby" is a very common topic in early English composition in India, often introduced through fill-in-the-blanks or sentence starters. So, the *topic* itself is likely familiar.
    *   **Differences (Increased Difficulty):**
        *   **Open-ended vs. Structured:** Textbook exercises often provide much more scaffolding: vocabulary lists, sentence frames (e.g., "My favourite hobby is _____."), or short answer questions that can be copied. This question requires *generating* the content entirely, albeit with explicit points to cover.
        *   **Paragraph Length:** Writing a full 4-5 sentence paragraph from scratch, maintaining coherence, is significantly harder than completing sentences or answering one-word questions.
        *   **Rubric's Completeness:** The rubric ("clearly mentions the hobby, when it is done, and why it is enjoyable") means merely writing *some* sentences won't get a full score; all specific elements must be present and intelligible.
