Let's break down the likely errors for Class 6 students in Indian government schools tackling this question.

**Analysis of Likely Errors:**

1.  **Misconceptions about Mathematics:**

    *   **Time Calculation - The Biggest Hurdle:** This is *the* most significant mathematical challenge.
        *   **Counting on fingers, but inaccurately:** They might start at 10 and count: 11, 12, 1, 2, 3, 4. If they count "10" as the start and "4" as the end, and just count the numbers between, they're likely to get 5 hours.
        *   **Direct Subtraction (4 - 10):** A common error with time is treating it like simple numbers. They might try to subtract 10 from 4, get -6 (which makes no sense for duration), or switch it to 10-4=6 but without understanding AM/PM transition.
        *   **Ignoring AM/PM transition:** They might correctly calculate 2 hours to reach 12 pm, but then restart counting from 1 for 1 pm, 2 for 2 pm etc., and struggle to add these segments correctly.
        *   **Counting 12 twice:** When going from 11, 12, 1, they might count 12 twice, or miss it entirely.
        *   **Confusion between 12-hour and 24-hour format:** While the question uses 12-hour, the mental transition from 12 PM to 1 PM can be tricky for young students not fully comfortable with time conversions.
    *   **Multiplication vs. Addition Confusion:** Once they get the hours, they might add 2 to the temperature instead of multiplying the hours by 2. (e.g., 30 + 6 + 2 instead of 30 + 6*2). This is less likely to be the *primary* error if they understand "per hour", but can happen if they rush.
    *   **Final Addition Error:** Simple arithmetic mistake in adding 30 to the calculated temperature increase.

2.  **Language Misunderstandings:**

    *   **"per hour":** While relatively common, some students might interpret "per hour" as "add 2 degrees once" instead of "for *each* hour."
    *   **"increases by":** This phrase should be straightforward for most, indicating addition.
    *   **"at 10 am in the morning" vs. "at 4:00 in the evening":** The AM/PM distinction can be tricky. "Morning" and "evening" are helpful context, but the numerical representation (10 vs 4) is still the core of the time calculation issue.
    *   **General vocabulary:** While the words are relatively simple, a student with very limited English might struggle to form a coherent mental model of the problem. However, the numbers and symbols ($^0 C$) are universal enough to give clues.

3.  **Attempt vs. Not Attempt:**

    *   **Attempt but fail:** Most students will attempt this. It involves numbers and a clear question. They will likely try to calculate the time and then the temperature.
    *   **Not attempt at all:** Only the very weakest students, or those completely overwhelmed by the language, would not attempt it. The numerical nature of the problem makes it approachable even with some language barrier.
