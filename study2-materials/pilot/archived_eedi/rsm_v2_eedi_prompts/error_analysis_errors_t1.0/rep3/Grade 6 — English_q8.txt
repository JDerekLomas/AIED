Let's break down the likely errors for the question: "Rearrange the words to make a meaningful sentence:\nbook/reading/a/is/he" with the target answer "He is reading a book."

Given the context of Class 6 students in Indian government schools, mostly from economically weaker sections with limited English, and the common error patterns observed, here's an analysis:

**Likely Errors and Misconceptions:**

1.  **Lack of Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) Understanding:** This is fundamental. Many students, especially when learning English, struggle with the basic word order. Their native language (many Indian languages) often has a different sentence structure (e.g., SOV for Hindi). This directly causes incorrect arrangements.
    *   **Misconception:** They might not understand that the *doer* (he) generally comes first, followed by the *action* (is reading), and then *what* is being acted upon (a book).

2.  **Confusion with Auxiliaries and Main Verbs:** "is" is an auxiliary verb here, part of the continuous tense. Students might struggle to place "is" correctly in relation to "reading."
    *   **Misconception:** They might see "is" and "reading" as two separate, equally weighted words that can be placed almost anywhere, rather than "is reading" functioning as a unit.

3.  **Literal Word-by-Word Translation/Proximity Bias:** Students might try to piece words together based on what sounds "okay" to them, or based on the order they see the words presented, rather than a grammatical rule. They might group words that appear close in the original list.

4.  **Limited Vocabulary/Cognitive Load:** While all words are common, the task of *rearranging* (a meta-cognitive skill) on top of forming a grammatically correct sentence can be overwhelming.

5.  **Not Attempting at All / "Empty Response":** Given the high "Empty Response" rates on other questions, some students might simply find this too difficult or not understand the instruction "Rearrange the words to make a meaningful sentence." However, this question is relatively more constrained and simpler than writing a paragraph or explaining a writing style, so non-attempt might be lower for this specific type.

**Would students attempt but fail, or not attempt at all?**

*   **Attempt and Fail (Highly Likely):** This task *looks* simple enough that many will try. They have all the words, so there's no need to generate content. The failure will stem from incorrect word order. This is the most probable outcome for the majority who don't get it right.
*   **Not Attempt (Less Likely for this specific question, but still possible):** Some might be intimidated or not understand the instruction, leading to an empty response. However, compared to explaining a poem, rearranging given words is a more concrete task, making non-attempt less common.

**What would the most common wrong answer look like?**

Based on the above, common wrong answers would likely include permutations that violate SVO structure or misplace "is."

*   **"Reading a book he is."** (SOV influence, or putting the action first)
*   **"He reading a book is."** (Misplacing 'is')
*   **"A book he is reading."** (Object-first, or trying to start with 'a')
*   **"Is he reading a book."** (Question form, if they confuse "rearrange" with "form a question")
*   **"Book reading a is he."** (Random arrangement, perhaps just copying the given order)
*   **"He is book reading a."** (Misplacing 'book')
