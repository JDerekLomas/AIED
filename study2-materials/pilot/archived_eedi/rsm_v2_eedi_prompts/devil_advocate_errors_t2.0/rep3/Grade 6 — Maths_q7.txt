Let's break down the thought process for calibrating this estimate, focusing on the common pitfalls teachers make.

**Initial Teacher Instinct (and why it's likely wrong):**

*   "It's simple math. Addition and multiplication. They learn about time. Everyone knows what 10 AM to 4 PM is."
*   "The numbers are small and round."
*   "The English is straightforward 'temperature increases', 'per hour'."

**Challenging Assumptions (the "forgetting" part):**

1.  **Limited English:**
    *   "At `10` am in the morning..." - `am` and `morning` is redundant but harmless.
    *   "`30^0 C`" - They probably recognize degrees Celsius.
    *   "If the temperature increases by `2^0 C` per hour" - `increases by`, `per hour` are crucial. Do they understand "per" clearly translates to "each"? Or just think it's extra info?
    *   "then what will be the temperature of Jodhpur at 4:00 in the evening?" - `evening` with `pm` might be another point of redundancy or slight confusion for some who barely read. The core is `4:00`.
    *   **Self-correction:** A student with limited English might understand *parts* but miss the full *relation* in "increases by 2 per hour." They might just see numbers and not know how to combine them logically. They might struggle to extract the operations required.

2.  **"Basic" Concepts Aren't Basic for Struggling Learners:**
    *   **Time calculation:** This is the *trickiest* part for many. `10 am to 4 pm`.
        *   Do they count on their fingers?
        *   Do they know `12 pm` is the pivot point?
        *   `10 -> 11 -> 12 -> 1 -> 2 -> 3 -> 4`. That's 6 hours. But it's very easy to miscount, especially for anxious students or those without a firm grasp of time telling and intervals.
        *   Some might do `4 - 10 = -6` (simple subtraction ignoring AM/PM).
        *   Some might do `12 - 10 = 2` and then `4 - 0 = 4` and add `2 + 4 = 6`. This is a sophisticated step for many.
        *   **Self-correction:** The elapsed time calculation (`10 AM to 4 PM`) is *much harder* than most teachers assume. It requires understanding of the 12-hour clock, crossing noon, and finding an interval.

    *   **Multiplication:** Once they get 6 hours, multiplying `6 * 2 = 12`. This is likely manageable if they understand "per hour."
    *   **Addition:** `30 + 12 = 42`. This is simple addition.
    *   **Self-correction:** Even if they get the time correct, mistranslating "increases by 2 per hour" to just adding 2 once, or adding 6 once, or some other operation, is common.

3.  **Exam Anxiety/Never Practiced:**
    *   If they see words they don't fully understand, or a question structure they haven't practiced, anxiety can lead to a blank stare or just guessing at operations.
    *   **Self-correction:** The lack of specific practice on "temperature change over time" story problems means many won't have a pre-loaded mental schema to apply. They have to construct the logic from scratch, which is demanding under exam conditions.

**Re-evaluating based on Class 6, Economically Weaker Sections, Average Pass Rate 29%:**

*   **Average Pass Rate 29%:** This is a *critical* benchmark. Most questions for such a cohort will fall below or around this, not significantly above, unless it's truly trivial.
*   **Time Calculation Difficulty:** This will trip up a substantial portion of students. I estimate at least half, probably more, will struggle to correctly identify "6 hours."
*   **"Per hour" concept:** Even if they get 6 hours, misunderstanding this could lead to `30 + 2 = 32`, or `30 + 6 = 36`.
*   **Combined operation:** They need to identify time elapsed, multiply, and then add. This is a multi-step problem that requires careful sequencing. Many will perform one step correctly but not all three in the right order.
