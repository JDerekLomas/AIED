Let's break down the question "Who lived inside the book?" and consider the common pitfalls for Class 6 students in Indian government schools from economically weaker sections.

**Analysis of the Question and Rubric:**

*   **"Who lived inside the book?"**: This is a direct factual recall question *if* they read a story about someone living in a book. It requires understanding the question (Who? lived? inside? the book?).
*   **Rubric: "Correctly states that a magical fairy lived inside the book. (Accept: 'a magical fairy', 'fairy')"**: This implies the students have recently encountered a specific story (likely "The Little Old Lady Who Lived in a Book" or a similar text) in their curriculum. The correct answer is very specific.

**Factors to Consider from the Prompt:**

1.  **Limited English:** This is the most crucial factor.
    *   **Vocabulary:** Do they understand "lived," "inside," "book"? While "book" is common, "lived" might be a stretch for some beyond just "live" (present tense).
    *   **Question Comprehension:** Can they piece together "Who lived inside the book?" to form a meaningful request for information? The previous examples show difficulties with "Describes," "Write a theme," "Write a short paragraph." This question is simpler grammatically, but still relies on vocabulary.

**Challenging Assumptions for "Who lived inside the book?":**

*   **"They must have read this story."** *Assumption:* The school covered this specific story recently and effectively. *Challenge:* Students might have been absent, the teaching might have been ineffective, or they might have forgotten the details from the story even if they "read" it. The high rate of "Empty Response" suggests many students cannot access basic information even when the topic *should* be familiar.
*   **"Fairy is a common word."** *Assumption:* All students know "fairy" in English. *Challenge:* Many might know it in their regional language, but not connect it to the English word, especially for those with very limited English. Even if they understand the question, they might not recall the specific English term for the character.
*   **"It's just one word!"** *Assumption:* Simple answers are easy. *Challenge:* The difficulty isn't just about length, but precise recall, English vocabulary, and successful decoding of the question. If they don't remember the *specific* story or the *specific* character, they can't answer correctly. They might offer a different character from a different story, or "book" if they only grasped the last word.
*   **Anxiety:** A direct "Who" question might feel less intimidating than "describe" or "write a theme," reducing the chance of *completely* blank responses *if* they know the answer. However, if they *don't* know, it still results in an empty or incorrect answer.
