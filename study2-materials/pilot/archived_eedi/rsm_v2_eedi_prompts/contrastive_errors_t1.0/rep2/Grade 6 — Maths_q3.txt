Let's break down the question and its demands on Class 6 students in Indian government schools.

**Contrastive Analysis:**

*   **Prior Knowledge Assumed:**
    *   **Number Recognition:** Students need to be able to read and identify multi-digit numbers (up to four digits). This is firmly established by Class 6, typically even earlier.
    *   **Place Value:** Implicitly, students need an understanding of place value to compare numbers effectively. They should know that a number with more digits is generally larger, and if the number of digits is the same, they compare digit by digit from the leftmost position. This is also well-covered by Class 6.
    *   **Concept of "Ascending Order":** This is a key mathematical term. Students must understand that "ascending" means from smallest to largest. This concept is taught early and reinforced.

*   **Language Demands:**
    *   **"Arrange":** A common instruction verb in mathematics.
    *   **"in ascending order":** This is the crucial phrase. For students with limited English proficiency, "ascending" might be a challenge. They might know "small to big" or "bottom to top" in Hindi (छोटे से बड़ा, नीचे से ऊपर), but translating "ascending" could be a barrier. They might confuse it with "descending."
    *   The numbers themselves are universal symbols, so no language demand there.
    *   The overall sentence structure is simple.

*   **Recall, Application, or Reasoning:**
    *   This is primarily an **application** task. Students are applying their knowledge of number comparison and the definition of ascending order.
    *   It requires very little complex reasoning beyond direct comparison. It's not a multi-step problem.
    *   It does involve **recall** of the meaning of "ascending order."

*   **Difficulty Compared to Typical Textbook Exercises:**
    *   **Typical:** This type of question (arranging numbers in ascending/descending order) is a very standard and common exercise in Class 3, 4, and 5 mathematics textbooks. By Class 6, it's generally considered a foundational skill that should be mastered.
    *   **Numbers:** The numbers chosen are straightforward (no negative numbers, no decimals, no fractions). They are all positive whole numbers, making direct comparison easier. The mix of a three-digit and four-digit numbers is a common strategy to test basic understanding that more digits usually means a larger number.
    *   **Complexity:** It's one of the simpler tasks students would encounter involving number operations/ordering. There are only four numbers, which is a manageable set.

**Specific Challenges:**

*   **"Ascending Order" Terminology:** This is the *primary* potential stumbling block due to English proficiency. If a student doesn't understand "ascending," they might either not attempt it, guess, or reverse the order.
*   **Carelessness/Sloppy Work:** Even if they know the concept, students might accidentally miss the smallest number or place it incorrectly due to haste. However, the rubric specifically gives half marks for just identifying the smallest and placing it first, which mitigates this a bit for a *partial* score.
