Let's break down the question "Which line from the poem do you like the most and why?" for Class 6 students in Indian government schools, considering their background and common errors.

**Contrastive Analysis of Difficulty:**

*   **Prior Knowledge Assumed:**
    *   **Low:** Students are not expected to know specific literary terms or complex analytical frameworks. They just need to have read and understood *a* poem (the context implies they would have just read one).
    *   **Moderate:** They need to understand the concept of "liking" something in a text, which is a fairly basic emotional response but still requires some introspection.
    *   **Implicit:** The ability to *identify* a "line from the poem" assumes a basic understanding of what a "line" is within a poem's structure.

*   **Language Demands on Students with Limited English:**
    *   **Vocabulary:**
        *   "Which": Very common, likely understood.
        *   "line": Fairly common, especially in an academic context where they've just read a poem.
        *   "poem": Core vocabulary for this subject.
        *   "like": Very common, easily understood.
        *   "most": Superlative, common.
        *   "why": Crucial, common question word, but generating the *reason* in English is harder.
    *   **Sentence Structure:**
        *   The question itself is relatively straightforward: "Which X do you like most and why?"
    *   **Output Demands:**
        *   **Identification:** Students need to accurately reproduce or quote a specific line. This can be difficult if their memory or transcription skills are weak, or if they don't grasp "line" precisely.
        *   **Justification:** This is the *hardest part*. Explaining "why" requires:
            *   Formulating a thought or feeling about the line.
            *   Translating that thought/feeling into English.
            *   Structuring a simple sentence or two to convey the reason.

*   **Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**
    *   **Recall:** Recalling the specific line from the poem. This is relatively low-level but can be tricky if they haven't memorized or copied it.
    *   **Application:** Applying the concept of "liking" to a specific textual element.
    *   **Reasoning:** Explaining *why* they like it. This involves basic reasoning and personal expression. It's not complex analytical reasoning, but it's a step beyond simple recall or identification.

*   **How does it compare in difficulty to typical textbook exercises?**
    *   **Identification of "line":** Similar to "Find a word/phrase that means..."
    *   **Expression of preference ("like the most"):** Common in early English exercises ("What is your favourite colour?").
    *   **Justification ("and why"):** This is where it becomes harder than typical recall/fill-in-the-blank exercises. Many textbook exercises might ask "Do you like X? Yes/No" or "What is X?" Asking "why" is a higher-order skill, even if the "why" can be simple.

*   **Reduced Abstractness:** "Liking" is a concrete, personal emotion. "Line" is a concrete textual unit. This is much less abstract than "writing style" or "theme."
