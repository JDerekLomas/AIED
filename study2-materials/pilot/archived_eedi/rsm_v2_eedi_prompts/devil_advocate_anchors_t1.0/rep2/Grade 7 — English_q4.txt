**Question Analysis:** "Do you think the king's questions are important in real life? Give one reason."
*   **Part 1: "Do you think the king's questions are important in real life?"** This requires a simple "yes" or "no" answer, or an implicit agreement. Crucially, the student needs to recall *what* the king's questions were, but the previous item (pass rate 50%) explicitly asked this, suggesting many students can identify them. However, they don't need to *list* them again.
*   **Part 2: "Give one reason."** This is the more challenging part. It requires:
    *   **Understanding "important in real life":** This is a fairly straightforward concept, even for limited English speakers.
    *   **Formulating a "reason":** This demands some level of original thought and expression. The reason can be quite simple and still be valid (e.g., "because it helps you succeed," "to make good decisions," "so you don't fail").

**Comparison to Calibration Anchors:**

*   **Item (pass rate 5%): Theme of "The Last Bargain."** This required deep comprehension and abstract thinking, plus complex sentence construction. Much harder.

*   **Item (pass rate 30%): Short paragraph on "My favorite season."** This required sustained writing (6-8 lines) on a personal topic, but with explicit prompts for content. The open-ended nature of the "reason" in our target question is similar, but it's only *one* reason, not a paragraph. The examples show even very broken English can score.
*   **Item (pass rate 50%): What were the three questions the king wanted to answer?** This was pure recall from the text. Our target question builds on this, assuming students have some familiarity with those questions. If 50% could recall them, a good portion understands them.

**Challenging Assumptions:**

*   **Limited English:** The question uses common words: "think," "king's questions," "important," "real life," "reason." It's not highly idiomatic or complex. Students might use very simple sentences or even fragments, but if the meaning is clear, they will score.
*   **"Basic" concepts:** "Importance" and "real life" are fairly basic concepts. The ability to give a "reason" is a foundational critical thinking skill, even if the reason is very simple.
*   **Exam anxiety/blank responses:** While this is a factor for all questions, the "yes/no" initial part might encourage students to at least attempt the "reason." The threshold for a "valid reason" is likely quite low.
*   **Never practiced this exact question type:** While true, asking "why is X important?" is a common question format in various subjects.
