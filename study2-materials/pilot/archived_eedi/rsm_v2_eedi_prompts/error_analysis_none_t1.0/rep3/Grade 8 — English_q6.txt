Let's break down the likely errors for Class 8 Indian government school students from economically weaker sections with limited English attempting to define "fable."

**Likely Errors and Misconceptions:**

1.  **Limited Vocabulary/Unknown Word:**
    *   **"Fable" itself might be an unfamiliar word.** Many students might have encountered fables (like those of Aesop) in their regional languages or as stories, but not explicitly learned the English term "fable" or its definition.
    *   **Even if they recognize "fable," key parts of the definition like "imparts a moral lesson" might be challenging to articulate in English.** "Moral lesson" is somewhat abstract.

2.  **Language Misunderstandings (English Grammar/Syntax/Vocabulary):**
    *   **Difficulty in forming a coherent English sentence/definition.** They might know bits and pieces but struggle to construct a grammatically correct and meaningful sentence.
    *   **Confusing "fable" with similar-sounding or related words:**
        *   **"Table"**: While seemingly distinct, in a stressful exam situation, especially with limited English proficiency, a student might misread or mishear and write about a table. (Less likely, but possible for the very weakest).
        *   **"Story"**: This is the most likely partial answer. They know it's a story, but can't elaborate further.
        *   **"Myth" / "Legend"**: If they know these terms, they might confuse them with fable, as all are types of stories.
        *   **"Animal"**: They might latch onto the animal aspect but fail to connect it to a story or moral.
    *   **Inability to express the abstract concept of "moral lesson":** This is the hardest part of the definition. They might understand the idea of a lesson but not have the English vocabulary to express it ("lesson," "moral," "teach us something good," etc.).

3.  **Attempt vs. No Attempt:**
    *   **Attempt but fail (most common):** Many will attempt something. They might write "It is a story" or "It has animals" or a jumbled phrase of these elements. They're trying to recall what they know, even if incomplete or grammatically flawed.
    *   **Not attempt at all:** A significant portion will leave it blank, either because they genuinely don't know the word, are completely overwhelmed by the English, or fear writing something incorrect. This is a common strategy when unsure.

**Most Common Wrong Answers:**

*   **"It is a story."** (Most frequent, partial truth)
*   **"It is a story with animals."** (Slightly better, but still incomplete)
*   **"A story about animals."** (Similar to above)
*   **"Stories."** (One-word answer, too vague)
*   **"Animal story."** (Closer, but lacks the moral element)
*   **"It teaches us something."** (Focuses only on the moral, lacks story/animal)
*   **A jumbled or grammatically incorrect sentence attempting to combine "story" and "animal."** (e.g., "Fable is animal story.")
*   **Leaving it blank.**

**Re-evaluating based on the Rubric and Student Profile:**

The rubric requires *both* animals as characters *and* imparting a moral lesson. This is a high bar for students with limited English.

*   Many might get the "story" part.
*   Some might get the "animals" part.
*   Very few will accurately articulate "imparts a moral lesson" in coherent English.

Even if they know what a fable is in their regional language, translating that nuanced definition into correct English syntax and vocabulary ("typically uses animals as characters," "imparts a moral lesson") is very challenging. They are more likely to provide fragments or simpler definitions.

Given the average pass rate of 29% for these students on general questions, this specific question is quite demanding due to:
*   Vocabulary ("fable" as a term)
*   Abstract concept ("moral lesson")
*   Requirement for a multi-part, grammatically sound definition.

I would expect a significant drop from the average pass rate for a question requiring such specific English articulation.
