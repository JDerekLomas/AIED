Here's a breakdown of the likely errors and my estimated pass rate:

**Likely Errors and Misconceptions:**

*   **Understanding "Line from the poem":**
    *   **Misconception:** Some students might misunderstand "line from the poem" to mean *any* sentence they write related to the poem, or even a line *about* the poem (e.g., "The poem is good"). They might not explicitly quote a line.
    *   **Failed attempt:** They might quote a line but struggle with transcription, or only quote part of a line incorrectly.
    *   **Not attempting/generic:** They might write "I like the poem very much" without referring to a specific line.

*   **Understanding "Why":**
    *   **Misconception:** Explaining "why" requires more abstract thought and articulation. Many students, especially at this level and with limited English, might struggle to elaborate beyond a simple adjective. They might just say "it is good" or "I liked it" without giving a reason related to the content or emotion of the line.
    *   **Limited vocabulary:** They might have a positive feeling but lack the English vocabulary to express *why* that specific line resonated with them.
    *   **Translating ideas:** They might have a strong "why" in their native language but fail to translate it into coherent English.
    *   **Generic reasons:** They might offer a very generic reason applicable to any part of the poem, rather than specific to the chosen line.
    *   **Failed attempt:** They might try to explain but the English is too broken or uninterpretable.

*   **Poem Comprehension:**
    *   Students might not have fully understood the poem itself (e.g., "The Last Bargain"). If they don't grasp the meaning of different lines, choosing one and explaining its significance becomes much harder. This impacts both selecting the "line" and explaining "why."
    *   If the poem itself has challenging vocabulary, selecting a line they understand *and* can articulate a reason for is doubly difficult.

*   **English Language Proficiency:**
    *   **Sentence structure:** Formulating a grammatically correct sentence for both quoting the line (especially if it's long or complex) and providing a reason will be a significant hurdle.
    *   **Spelling/Punctuation:** Even if they choose a good line and have a reason, spelling and punctuation errors can make their answer difficult to understand or appear less articulate.
    *   **Simply too difficult:** For many, the cognitive load of recalling a specific line, correctly transcribing it (or approximating it), and then explaining a nuanced reason in a foreign language will be overwhelming.

*   **"Attempt but fail" vs. "Not attempt at all":**
    *   This question is highly likely to receive attempts, as it asks for personal preference. However, the quality of these attempts, especially the "why," will be a major differentiating factor.
    *   Many will quote a line, but fail on the "why." Some might not quote a line but try to give a reason related to the general poem.
    *   Given the "Max score: 1.5" and a rubric of "Denotes a line from the poem *and* mentions why," it's highly likely that a large portion will only achieve part of the criteria.

**Most Common Wrong Answer:**

*   "I like this poem. The man wanted to be free. I like it." (Fails to quote a specific line, gives a very generic "why")
*   "The poem 'I became servant no longer' is my favorite line. Because I like freedom." (Quotes a line, but the "why" is a bit simplistic and could apply generally)
*   [Transcription of a random line from the poem] "Because it is good." (Quotes a line, but insufficient "why")
