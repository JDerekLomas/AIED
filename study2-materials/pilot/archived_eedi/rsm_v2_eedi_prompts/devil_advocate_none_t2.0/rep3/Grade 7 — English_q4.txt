Let's break down the question and the context for Class 7 students in Indian government schools.

**Question:** "Do you think the king's questions are important in real life? Give one reason."

**Rubric:** The king wanted to answer (based on the reading passage) the right time to do everything, the right thing to do, and the right people to work with. Any valid reason. Max score: 1

**Contextual Challenges:**

*   **English Comprehension:** The prompt "Do you think the king's questions are important in real life?" requires understanding "important," "real life," and linking it to "the king's questions." Many students, especially those struggling with English, might get stuck on "king's questions" and wonder if it literally means *questions asked by a king*, rather than the *content* of his inquiry. The follow-up "Give one reason" is straightforward, but hinges on understanding the first part.
*   **Connecting to Passage:** The rubric explicitly states the king's questions are "based on the reading passage." This implies students have *read and understood* a passage about a king asking three specific questions: "What is the right time to do everything?", "Who are the most important people?", and "What is the most important thing to do?" If they haven't retained these, or misunderstood the passage, they can't connect.
*   **Abstract Thinking:** "Important in real life" requires a degree of abstract thinking – applying fictional story elements to practical life. While these three questions (right time, right people, right action) *are* universally relevant, students from a struggling background might interpret "real life" very concretely. Their "real life" might be about getting food, basic survival, helping family. Abstract strategic thinking (like "the right time to do everything") might feel less immediately relevant *to them* than it does to an adult.
*   **Vocabulary/Concepts:** "Important," "real life," "reason." All seem basic to us, but for a struggling student, each word can be a hurdle.
*   **Anxiety & Practice:** As highlighted, exam anxiety can cause blanks. Students unaccustomed to analytical, opinion-based questions might simply parrot facts from the story rather than offering an opinion. "Have you ever practiced this exact question type?" — Unlikely for many. They are often tested on recall, not personal reflection.
*   **"Basic" Concepts Aren't Basic:** The questions the king asks (right time, right people, right thing) *are* fundamentally important. However, understanding *why* they are important and articulating that understanding, even simply, requires a cognitive step. A student might *intuitively* grasp their importance but struggle to put it into words in English.
*   **Absenteeism:** Many students might have missed the lesson on the specific story. If they haven't read the passage, this question is impossible to answer correctly.

*   **A student who was absent for this topic:** No chance of a correct answer unless they've read the story independently and understood it, which is a rare exception for this demographic.
