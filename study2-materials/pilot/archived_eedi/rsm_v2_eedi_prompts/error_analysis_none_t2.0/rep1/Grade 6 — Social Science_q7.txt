Let's break down how Class 6 students in Indian government schools from economically weaker sections might approach this question, keeping their limited English proficiency and the general 29% pass rate in mind.

**Analyzing Potential Errors:**

**1. Misconceptions about Social Science (especially Geography/Resources):**

*   **Definition of "Natural Resource":** Students might confuse natural resources with man-made structures or common objects that aren't *resources* in the economic sense.
    *   *Example:* Identifying "schools," "hospitals," "roads" as resources.
    *   *Example:* Naming "chappatis" or "clothes" as a resource (they consume it, but it's not a *natural* resource).
    *   *Example:* Focusing on crops like "wheat" or "bajra" which are agricultural products, not raw natural resources, though "soil" or "water" enable them. This might be a borderline case, depending on how strict the interpretation of "natural resource" is.
*   **Specificity vs. Generality:** They might provide extremely general answers instead of specific resources.
    *   *Example:* Saying "trees" instead of a specific type of timber/forest product (though "trees" could be accepted depending on interpretation).
    *   *Example:* Saying "minerals" without naming one.
*   **Recall Difficulty (Rajasthan Specifics):** While they might know what natural resources *are*, remembering two *specific to Rajasthan* might be hard if they haven't had recent or in-depth lessons. Their world might be more focused on their immediate village/town.
    *   *Confusion with other states:* Naming resources common elsewhere but not prominent in Rajasthan.
*   **Lack of Linkage (Explanation Part):** Even if they identify resources, the "how it influences lives" part requires a higher level of analytical thinking and expressing cause-and-effect. They might just state the resource exists, not its impact.
    *   *Example:* "Marble is found in Rajasthan." (No explanation).
    *   *Example:* "Water is there, people drink it." (Too basic, doesn't really explain *influence* beyond simple consumption). A better explanation would link it to agriculture, livestock, or daily survival in a desert climate.

**2. Language Misunderstandings:**

*   **"Mention any two natural resources":**
    *   "Mention" might be understood as "tell about" which might lead to brief descriptions instead of just listing.
    *   "Natural resources": This is the most critical term. If they don't understand "natural" or "resource" in the given context, the entire answer will be wrong. They might translate "natural" literally to things in nature, without understanding the 'resource' aspect (e.g., birds, animals as resources).
*   **"explain how one of them influences the lives of the people":**
    *   "Influences": A moderately complex verb. They might struggle to grasp "how it *impacts* or *shapes*" lives. They might just describe what people *do* with the resource, without making the link to its broader effect on their lifestyle, economy, etc.
    *   "Lives of the people": They might just talk about their own family or immediate community, not a broader societal impact.

**3. Attempt vs. Not Attempt:**

*   **Will Attempt:** Given it's a "mention two and explain one" question, students are very likely to attempt it. There are keywords ("Rajasthan," "natural resources," "lives of people") that trigger some prior knowledge. Even if their knowledge is weak, they'll likely try to put something down.
*   **Failure:** The failure will mostly come from:
    *   Incorrect identification of natural resources.
    *   Lack of specificity for Rajasthan.
    *   Inability to explain the "influence" adequately, or at all.

**4. Most Common Wrong Answers:**

*   **Category 1: Listing generic things in nature without understanding "resource":**
    *   "Animals," "birds," "trees," "mountains," "river."
*   **Category 2: Listing agricultural products or human settlements:**
    *   "Wheat," "bajra," "milk," "cities," "villages," "schools."
