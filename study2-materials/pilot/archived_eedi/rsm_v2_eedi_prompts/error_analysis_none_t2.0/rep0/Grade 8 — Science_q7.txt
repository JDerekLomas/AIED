**Analysis of Errors:**

**1. Misconceptions about Science (Decomposition):**

*   **Lack of Understanding of "Change":** Many students might not grasp the concept of decomposition as a *transformation* of matter. They might think "change" only refers to a dramatic visual alteration (e.g., something burning or breaking), not a slow process of decay.
*   **Ignorance of Microbes/Microorganisms:** The idea that microscopic organisms are responsible for decomposition is likely alien to many. They might not understand *what* causes the change.
*   **Perceiving "Waste" as Stagnant:** "Waste" to them might simply be something discarded and static, not something that interacts with its environment over time. They might expect it to stay exactly the same.
*   **Focus on Immediate Gratification/Results:** They might expect changes to happen quickly. If they don't see immediate changes, they might conclude there are none, failing to consider a process that takes "some days."
*   **Confusing with non-biodegradable waste:** Some might associate all waste with things like plastic or metal that don't decompose quickly.

**2. Language Misunderstandings:**

*   **"Notice any change":** This phrase might be too abstract. They might not connect "change" with decomposition. The word "notice" implies observation, but if they don't understand *what* to look for, they won't "notice" anything.
*   **"Pit":** While "pit" is common, they might not understand its context as a composting pit or simply a place for waste.
*   **"Give reasons":** This requires analytical thinking and expressing causality, which can be challenging for students with limited language proficiency.
*   **"Evaluate if this is beneficial for soil health":** This is a higher-order thinking skill. "Evaluate" means judging its worth. They need to understand what "beneficial," "soil," and "health" mean in this context and connect them to decomposition. The abstract nature of "soil health" might be difficult to grasp beyond "it grows plants."
*   **Limited vocabulary:** Students might understand the individual words but not the phrase or the scientific concept embedded within them. They might struggle to articulate any scientific reasoning due to language barriers.

**3. Attempt vs. Not Attempt:**

*   **Not attempt:** The difficulty in language and scientific concepts means a significant portion might not attempt to give detailed reasons or evaluation, especially the latter part. They might provide a simple "yes" or "no" to the first part and stop there.
*   **Attempt and fail:** Many would attempt but provide incomplete or incorrect answers due to the reasons above. They might say "yes" but then give a vague reason like "it gets old" or "it smells."
*   **Answering only part of the question:** Students often pick out what they *do* understand and answer only that, ignoring the more complex parts ("Give reasons," "Evaluate if this is beneficial for soil health").

**4. Most Common Wrong Answer:**

*   **"No, the waste remains the same."** This shows a complete lack of understanding of decomposition.
*   **"Yes, it will smell bad/rot."** This shows some awareness of a change but doesn't connect it to a scientific process or its benefits. It's an observation, but not an *explanation*. It also misses the benefit part entirely.
*   **"Yes, it turns into soil/mud."** This is a step closer, but often lacks the "why" and "how," and still misses the *benefit to soil health* explicitly.
*   **Simple "Yes."** without any reasoning.
*   **Answering "Yes" but providing reasons completely unrelated to decomposition.** For example, "Yes, because the sun makes it hot."
