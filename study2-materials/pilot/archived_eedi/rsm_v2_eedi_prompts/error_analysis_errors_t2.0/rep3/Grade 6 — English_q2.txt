*   **Language Misunderstandings:**
    *   **Contextual Understanding:** The primary challenge will be whether the students understood the story that the question is based on. If they didn't understand the story, they wouldn't know the answer regardless of their English proficiency. This is probably the biggest factor here.

*   **Attempt vs. Non-Attempt:**
    *   **Attempt:** Given the simple nature of the question (it's a direct factual recall question), students are very likely to attempt it if they understand *any* part of the story. They won't get stuck on the question *itself* as much as on the *content* it refers to. "Empty Response" would likely mean they have absolutely no clue about the story or a severe mental block, which is less likely for a simple "who" question after they've made it through an exam.

        *   Not understanding the story well enough to recall the detail.
        *   Recalling *other* characters from the story who *didn't* live inside the book, or simply guessing a random character's name.
        *   Possibly mistaking "book" for "story" in a general sense and describing *another part* of the narrative or even an external character.

    *   "Some boy/girl" (if a generic character was prominent elsewhere).
    *   Naming another character from the story that wasn't the correct answer.
    *   "A child" (as seen in the "The Last Bargain" example, students might provide a very general answer if they grasp *some* aspect but not the specific detail).
    *   Potentially a single, unconnected word if they pick up on a prominent word from the story but can't link it correctly.
