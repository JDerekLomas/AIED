I will analyze the question, rubric, and calibration anchors, considering potential student errors, before estimating the pass rate.

**Question Analysis:**
"Which line from the poem do you like the most and why?"

*   **Task 1: Identify a line from the poem.** This requires recall or access to the poem itself.
*   **Task 2: State *why* they like it.** This requires some level of explanation or justification, even if simple.

**Rubric Analysis:**
"Denotes a line from the poem and mentions why." (Max score: 1.5)
This implies that just stating a line isn't enough; the "why" is crucial for a pass.

**Likely Errors & Misconceptions:**

1.  **"Which line from the poem":**
    *   **Not having access to the poem:** This is a major issue. If the poem isn't provided or they haven't memorized it, they can't answer. Assuming this question is *after* the poem has been read/studied, they might still struggle with exact recall.
    *   **Quoting an incorrect line or a line not from *this* poem:** They might remember a general idea but misquote or invent.
    *   **Simply writing a word or phrase, not a full "line":** They might pick a key word they liked but not present it as a line.
    *   **Ignoring the "from the poem" part:** They might state a line they like *in general* or from a different context.

2.  **"do you like the most and why":**
    *   **Difficulty with explaining "why":** This is a significant hurdle for students with limited English and analytical skills. They might state the line but then struggle to articulate *why* they like it beyond very basic reasons ("it is good," "I like it").
    *   **Simple, unelaborated reasons:** E.g., "I like the line '...' because it is nice." While this *might* pass the "mentions why" criteria, it's minimal.
    *   **Translating emotions/reasons from their native language imperfectly:** They might have a good reason in their head but lack the English vocabulary or grammatical structure to express it.
    *   **Not attempting the "why" at all:** They might just write a line and stop.
    *   **Misinterpreting "why":** They might explain what the line *means* rather than why *they* like it personally.

3.  **Language Barriers:**
    *   Difficulty forming coherent sentences, especially for the "why" part.
    *   Limited vocabulary to express nuanced reasons.
    *   Grammar mistakes might make their reasons unclear.

4.  **Effort/Engagement:**
    *   Some students might provide a line but genuinely not care or be able to articulate a reason.
    *   Others might simply copy a line without any personal connection.

**Common Wrong Answers/Failure Modes:**

*   **Line only, no reason:** "I like the line 'my heart goes out to the children'." (Would likely get 0, or partial score depending on marker leniency, but the rubric emphasizes "why").
*   **Line from elsewhere / incorrect line.**
*   **Vague reason:** "I like the line '...' because it is good." (Might pass if "good" is considered a reason, but weak).
*   **Completely off-topic answer.**
*   **Empty response.**
*   **Attempting the "why" but failing to communicate it due to language barriers.**
