Here's an analysis of the "Define fable" question, considering the student population and calibration anchors:

**Likely Errors and Misconceptions:**

*   **Vocabulary Gap:** This is the most significant hurdle. "Fable" is not an everyday word for students from economically weaker sections with limited English exposure. Many will simply not know the definition.
*   **Partial Knowledge:** Some students might have heard the word but only associate it with one aspect (e.g., "stories with animals") without grasping the "moral lesson" component.
*   **Confusing with Related Terms:**
    *   **Story/Tale:** They might define it simply as "a story," missing the specific characteristics.
    *   **Poem:** Less likely, but if they're struggling with English literary terms, they might miscategorize it.
    *   **Myth/Legend:** These are longer, often with gods/heroes, and a broader cultural purpose, but a confused student might conflate them due to the "old story" aspect.
*   **Grammar/Sentence Structure Issues:** Even if they know parts of the definition, constructing a coherent English sentence to express it will be a challenge. Their answers might be fragmented, ungrammatical, or difficult to understand.
*   **Direct Translation Issues:** If they try to translate from a local language, the concept might not map perfectly, or the translation might be awkward.
*   **Non-Attempt/Irrelevant Answers:** Given the difficulty of the vocabulary, a significant number will likely leave it blank or write something completely irrelevant (similar to the "Viser to epumat" example for Fundamental Rights).
*   **Common Wrong Answers:**
    *   "It's a story." (Missing animals and moral)
    *   "Story with animals." (Missing moral)
    *   "A story that teaches something." (Missing animals)
    *   "Don't know" / "No idea" (In their local language or broken English)

**Synthesizing and Estimating:**

The question "Define fable" sits somewhere between the 5% (abstract literary analysis) and 15% (defining specific historical terms) anchors.

*   It's a definition, which is generally more straightforward than abstract "theme."
*   However, the word "fable" itself is likely *less* familiar to the average Class 8 student in this context than "archaeological source" (which might be covered in history lessons) or "Fundamental Rights."
*   Getting *both* parts of the rubric (animals + moral) will be very difficult for most. Many might get one part, or neither.
*   The "limited English" aspect is crucial. Even if they grasp the concept in their local language, expressing it accurately and concisely in English is a major challenge.

Considering the high likelihood of non-attempts due to unfamiliar vocabulary, partial answers (e.g., "story with animals"), and poor English construction even if they have some idea, the pass rate will be low. It's harder than differentiating historical sources because "fable" is less likely to be drilled as a specific curriculum term they've memorized.

I anticipate a significant number of blanks or very vague answers.
