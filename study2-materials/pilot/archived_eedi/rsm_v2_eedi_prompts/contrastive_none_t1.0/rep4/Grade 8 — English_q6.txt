Let's break down the question "Define fable" for Class 8 students in Indian government schools, considering their specific background.

**Contrastive Analysis of "Define fable"**

**1. Prior Knowledge Assumed:**

*   **Positive:**
    *   **Concept of "story":** Very likely present. Even without formal schooling in English literature, students will be familiar with the concept of stories through oral tradition, local languages, and possibly some basic Hindi or regional language textbooks.
    *   **Moral lessons:** The idea of stories having a "teaching" or "lesson" is deeply ingrained in Indian culture, through religious texts, folk tales, and family narratives.
    *   **Animals as characters:** Animal stories (Panchatantra, Jataka tales, local folklore) are incredibly common and popular in India, even among children from economically weaker sections. This is a huge advantage.
*   **Negative/Potential Gaps:**
    *   **"Fable" as a specific literary term:** This is the primary hurdle. While they might *know* what a fable *is* conceptually (animal story with a moral), they might not know the *English word* "fable" for it. They might call it "kahani" (story), "seekh wali kahani" (story with a lesson), or similar in their local language.
    *   **Formal definition structure:** They might struggle to articulate a formal, concise definition in English, even if they understand the components.

**2. Language Demands (for students with limited English):**

*   **Low Receptive Demand:** The question itself, "Define fable," is short and uses a relatively common verb "Define." "Fable" is the only unknown vocabulary, but the *concept* behind it is likely known.
*   **High Productive Demand:** This is where the difficulty lies.
    *   **Vocabulary for definition:** To provide a correct definition, students need specific English vocabulary: "short story," "typically," "uses," "animals," "characters," "imparts," "moral lesson." While "story," "animals," "lesson" might be known, words like "typically," "imparts," and "characters" might be challenging or require them to use simpler, less precise English.
    *   **Sentence structure:** Constructing a grammatically correct and coherent sentence or two in English to form a definition is a significant challenge for students with limited English proficiency. They might know the individual words but struggle to string them together meaningfully.
    *   **Recall vs. Recognition:** It's easier for them to recognize the characteristics of a fable than to recall the exact English terms to define it.

**3. Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**

*   This is primarily a **recall** question. Students need to recall the definition of a specific literary term.
*   It requires some **understanding** of the components, but not deep application or reasoning in the sense of analyzing a fable or identifying its moral. It's about retrieving stored information (ideally, the definition).

    *   **Easier (conceptually):** The *concept* is simpler than many complex grammar rules or abstract comprehension tasks. The core idea is accessible.

The biggest challenge is the **gap between conceptual understanding (knowing what a fable is) and linguistic proficiency (articulating it in correct English with appropriate vocabulary).** Students might describe the characteristics of a fable in their local language or very broken English but fail to meet the rubric's standard for a formal definition.
