### 1. Analysis of Misconceptions

#### **Option B ($<$)**
*   **Buggy Reasoning:** The student calculates the values correctly ($11$ and $5$) but fundamentally confuses the inequality symbols. Alternatively, they may incorrectly process the division on the left: $8+12 \div 4 \rightarrow (8+12) = 20 \rightarrow 20 \div 4 = 5$, and then make a further error on the right side by ignoring parentheses, resulting in a number higher than 5. However, the most common reason for picking B after doing the math is **Symbol Reversal**.
*   **Attractiveness:** Very high for students with dyscalculia or those under time pressure. The math is correct, but the "hungry alligator" points the wrong way.

#### **Option C ($=$)**
*   **Buggy Reasoning (Given):** "Carries out operations from left to right regardless of priority order."
    *   Left side: $8+12 = 20$, then $20 \div 4 = \mathbf{5}$.
    *   Right side: Parentheses first $(8+12) = 20$, then $20 \div 4 = \mathbf{5}$.
*   **Attractiveness:** Extremely high. This is the "classic" order of operations error. The student perceives the brackets as redundant because they are performing that operation first anyway. It creates a satisfying, symmetrical result ($5=5$), which makes the student feel confident they have found a "trick" in the question.

#### **Option D ($\geq$)**
*   **Buggy Reasoning:** This is usually a "safety net" guess. If a student calculates the values and finds $11 > 5$ (correct), but they have seen $\geq$ used in higher-level contexts, they might assume it is a "more complete" or "more formal" version of the $>$ symbol. 
*   **Attractiveness:** Low for younger students, but slightly more attractive for "Basic/Proficient" students who are currently learning about inequalities and are over-applying the "greater than or equal to" notation without understanding the necessity of the "equal to" component.
