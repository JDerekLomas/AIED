### 1. Analysis of Wrong Answer Options

#### **Option A: Problem P: \(4-5\); Problem Q: \(4+5\)**
*   **Buggy Reasoning:** As identified, the student **carries out operations from left to right regardless of priority order**. They treat mathematics like reading a sentence. They do not look ahead to see if a high-priority operator (like $\times$) exists; they simply execute the first operation their eyes land on.
*   **Attractiveness:** **Very High.** This is the most "natural" way for a human to process information. For a student who hasn't fully internalised BIDMAS, there is no internal "alarm" telling them to skip the addition and look at the multiplication. It is the default setting for novice learners.

#### **Option C: Problem P: \(5+6\); Problem Q: \(4+5\)**
*   **Buggy Reasoning:** This student likely suffers from a **misunderstanding of the BIDMAS acronym structure**. In the word "BIDMAS," the 'A' (Addition) comes before the 'S' (Subtraction). Therefore, they believe they must *always* add before they subtract, even if the subtraction is further left. For Problem Q, they remain tethered to the "Left-to-Right" instinct, ignoring the 'M' (Multiplication) priority.
*   **Attractiveness:** **Low/Moderate.** It is a confusing hybrid error. Most students who know enough to try and add first in Problem P would also know to multiply first in Problem Q. This option represents a student who is inconsistently applying rules.

#### **Option D: Problem P: \(5+6\); Problem Q: \(5 \times 6\)**
*   **Buggy Reasoning:** This is the **"Literal BIDMAS" error.** This student correctly identifies that multiplication takes priority over addition (Problem Q), but they incorrectly believe that addition *must* take priority over subtraction (Problem P). They do not realise that Addition and Subtraction have equal priority and should be solved left-to-right.
*   **Attractiveness:** **High (for intermediate students).** This is a very common plateau in the UK curriculum. Teachers often teach the acronym without emphasizing that DM and AS are "tied" pairs. Students "over-correct" their left-to-right instinct and mistakenly prioritize addition.

---

### 2. Analysis of the Correct Answer (B)
*   **Correct Logic:** In Problem P, Addition and Subtraction are equal priority, so we move left-to-right ($4-5$). In Problem Q, Multiplication takes precedence over addition ($5 \times 6$).

---

### 3. Predicted Response Distribution

This distribution reflects typical UK classrooms across Key Stage 3 (ages 11-14).

*   **Below Basic:** These students generally read left-to-right. They have heard of BIDMAS but don't know how to apply it.
*   **Basic:** They know "Multiplication comes first" (correcting Problem Q), but they follow the acronym literally (making the error in Problem P).
*   **Proficient:** Most will get it right, but some still fall for the "Addition before Subtraction" trap.
*   **Advanced:** These students understand the "Equal Priority" rule.

### Teacher's Summary
The battle in this question is between the **Left-to-Right instinct (Option A)** and the **Literal Acronym interpretation (Option D)**. Option A is the dominant error for weaker students, while Option D is actually more common in "middle-ability" students who are trying to follow the rules they've been taught but lack the nuance of equal priority.
