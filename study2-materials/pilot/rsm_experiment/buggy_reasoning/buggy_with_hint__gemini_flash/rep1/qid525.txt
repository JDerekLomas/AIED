### 1. Analysis of Wrong Options

#### **Option A: \( \frac{99}{20} \)**
*   **Reasoning:** The student has performed the calculation entirely correctly, finding the common denominator (20) and arriving at the correct value. However, they **failed to read the final instruction** "Leave your answer as a mixed number." They converted their correct working into an improper fraction: $2\frac{14}{20} + 2\frac{5}{20} = 4\frac{19}{20} = \frac{99}{20}$.
*   **Attractiveness:** This is a high-level distractor. It is particularly "cruel" because the mathematical heavy lifting is correct. Students who are over-confident or rushing are most likely to pick this. They see their calculated number in the list and tick it immediately without checking the required format.

#### **Option C: \( \frac{99}{40} \)** (The specified misconception)
*   **Reasoning:** As specified, the student adds the numerators and denominators. Here, they likely converted to improper fractions first ($27/10 + 9/4$) and then added horizontally: $27+9=36$ and $10+4=14$. This doesn't lead directly to C, but a more common path for this specific option is converting to improper, finding a "common denominator" by multiplying ($10 \times 4 = 40$), but then failing to adjust the numerators correctly, or simply doing: $(27 \times 4 = 108) + (9 \times 10 = 90) = 198/40$ and then potentially simplifying or making a secondary arithmetic slip to land on 99. *Note: Most often, the "add across" error leads to $4 \frac{8}{14}$, while C represents a student trying to use 40 as a denominator but getting lost in the conversion.*
*   **Attractiveness:** Moderate. For a student who knows they need a common denominator but forgets to scale the numerators, 40 is a very tempting denominator to use.

#### **Option D: \( 4 \frac{8}{14} \)**
*   **Reasoning:** This is the most "pure" form of the specified misconception. The student adds the whole numbers ($2+2=4$), then adds the numerators ($7+1=8$), and then adds the denominators ($10+4=14$). 
*   **Attractiveness:** Extremely high for lower-ability students. It feels intuitive and "fair"â€”doing the same operation to the top and bottom.
