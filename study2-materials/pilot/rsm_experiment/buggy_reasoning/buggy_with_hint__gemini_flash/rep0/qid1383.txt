As an experienced teacher, I see this specific question as a classic "trap" for students navigating the transition from arithmetic to algebraic substitution. The power of 4 is high enough to differentiate between those who understand indices and those who revert to multiplication.

### 1. Analysis of Misconceptions

**Option B: $12$**
*   **Buggy Reasoning:** The student identifies the operation as $k \times 4$ rather than $k$ to the power of $4$. They calculate $(-3) \times 4$, then often ignore the negative sign (or incorrectly assume it's positive).
*   **Nature of Error:** Confusing "powers" with "multiples." This is a foundational misconception in early secondary (Year 7/8).

**Option C: $-81$ (Known Misconception)**
*   **Buggy Reasoning:** "Believes multiplying two negatives gives a negative answer."
*   **Detailed Chain:** The student correctly performs $3^4 = 81$ and knows that negative numbers are involved. However, their rule-retrieval is flawed. They may think "Since the base is negative, the answer must stay negative," or they process the multiplication as $(-3) \times (-3) = -9$, then $(-9) \times (-3) \times (-3) \dots$ concluding that any calculation starting with a negative should end negative.
*   **Nature of Error:** Incorrect application of integer multiplication rules or a failure to track parity in negative products.

**Option D: $-12$**
*   **Buggy Reasoning:** The student makes the same "power vs. multiple" error as Option B but maintains the negative sign from the base: $(-3) \times 4 = -12$.
*   **Nature of Error:** Combined conceptual error (powers) and basic linear calculation.

---

### 2. Attractiveness of Distractors

*   **Option C (The "Top Tier" Distractor):** This is highly attractive, even to Mid-to-High ability students. It feels "right" because the input is negative. Students often default to the rule "A negative and a positive (the index) makes a negative," incorrectly applying the rule for multiplication/division to exponents. It is very easy to fall into this if the student doesn't write out $(-3) \times (-3) \times (-3) \times (-3)$ and group the pairs.
*   **Option D:** Very attractive to lower-ability students. It requires the least "work" (a single multiplication) and feels logically consistent with the signs given in the question.
*   **Option B:** Less attractive than D because it requires the student to ignore a negative sign they can clearly see in the question, though some students do this "to keep things simple."

*   **A=95%** B=0% C=5% D=0%
*   *Note: Advanced students rarely make the multiplication error; only a momentary lapse in concentration leads them to C.*
