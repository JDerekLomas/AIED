To predict these percentages, I’ve analysed the common pitfalls for a Year 9 student encountering linear equations in a table of values:

*   **The Trap for A (5):** This is the most "attractive" wrong answer. Students see $x=0$, ignore the $4x$ term entirely, and then see $2y=10$, solving for $y=5$. This feels "right" because it's a simple one-step division.
*   **The Trap for B (20):** This is a classic "operation reversal." A student might move the $2$ from $2y$ to the other side by multiplying instead of dividing ($10 \times 2 = 20$), or they erroneously try to relate the numbers $4$ and $5$ (from $4x+2y$) in a way that leads to $20$.
*   **The Correct Logic for A (5):** Wait—looking at the equation $4(0) + 2y = 10$, we get $2y = 10$, so $y = 5$. **Option A is actually the correct answer.**
*   **The Trap for C (2.5):** Students often confuse $x$ and $y$ coefficients. They might try to divide $10$ by $4$ (the coefficient of $x$), resulting in $2.5$.
*   **The Trap for D (3):** Students who lack algebraic fluency often "guess and check" small integers that look like they fit the pattern of the numbers in the table (0, 1, 2, 3, 4).
