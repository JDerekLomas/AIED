To answer this question effectively, we must consider two key hurdles for students:
1.  **Reciprocals vs. Opposites**: Students often confuse the rule for "making 1" (multiplying by the reciprocal) with the rule for "making 0" (adding the opposite).
2.  **Negative signs in products**: This is the most significant "trap" in this specific question. Many students know they need the reciprocal ($4/3$), but they default to the "two negatives make a positive" rule without correctly applying it to the required result of $-1$.

The correct answer is **A ($4/3$)**, because $-\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{4}{3} = -1$.
A very common mistake will be **B ($-4/3$)**, as students see two minus signs and reflexively think they "belong" together, or they believe that because the first number is negative, the multiplier must also be negative to "stay consistent," resulting in a positive $1$.
