As an experienced UK maths teacher, I see this specific problem as a "classic" diagnostic question. It probes whether a student understands that a common denominator requires a **multiplicative** relationship rather than an **additive** one, and whether they can handle the algebraic variable correctly.

For a Year 9 class (13-14 year olds), this sits squarely in the middle of the "Algebraic Fractions" topic, which is often introduced toward the end of Key Stage 3 for higher-ability sets.

### DIFFICULTY: 6/10
While the arithmetic is simple, the "distractor" logic is very strong here. Many Year 9 students can solve linear equations or expand brackets confidently but revert to incorrect "intuitive" rules when faced with algebraic fractions.

---

### Predicted Response Distributions

**Rationale for the distractors:**
*   **A (Correct):** Student identifies common denominator $2x$ and correctly doubles the numerator of the second fraction $(8/2x)$.
*   **B (Misconception):** "Add across the top, add across the bottom." Extremely common for lower-attaining students who treat numerators and denominators as independent integers ($3+4=7$ and $2x+x=3x$).
*   **C (Calculation Error):** Student finds the correct numerator ($3+8=11$) but forgets the denominator is $2x$, or incorrectly simplifies $11/2x$ to $11/x$.
*   **D (Denominator Ignored):** Student correctly identifies the common denominator $2x$ but fails to scale the numerator of the second fraction, simply adding $3+4$.
