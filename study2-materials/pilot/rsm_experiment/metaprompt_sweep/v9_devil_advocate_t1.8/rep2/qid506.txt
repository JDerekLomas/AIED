To predict student performance on this question, we must look beyond the simple algebraic step of "dividing by 3." Many students, especially those at below_basic and basic levels, struggle with the reciprocal nature of fractions.

The most common misconception is the "multiplication by the numerator" or "ignoring the fraction" error. Many students see 3 and 12 and immediately think "4," leading them to either Option A (placing 4 in the denominator) or Option C (treating it as $12 \div 3$). Option B is the mathematically correct operation ($ \frac{1}{12} \div 3 = \frac{1}{36} $), but students frequently miscalculate $12 \times 3$ or confuse it with $12 \div 3$ when fractions are involved.

**Analysis of Ability Levels:**
*   **Below Basic:** Students often see the 3 and 12 and assume the answer is 4 (Option C) or simply multiply 3 and 3 (Option D). They struggle to apply any formal inverse operations to fractions.
*   **Basic:** Many students know they should divide by 3, but their fraction arithmetic is weak. They often calculate $12 \div 3 = 4$ and arrive at $p = 1/4$ (Option A). This is a very common "trap" for students at this level.
*   **Proficient:** These students recognize that $p = \frac{1}{12} \div 3$. However, a significant portion will still perform the mental shortcut of $12 \div 3$ incorrectly or flip the wrong part of the expression.
*   **Advanced:** These students consistently apply the rule $\frac{1}{a} \div n = \frac{1}{an}$.
