As an experienced UK maths teacher, I see this question as a test of "structural fluency" in algebra. In the UK curriculum, students are introduced to the fraction bar as a replacement for the division symbol in Year 7 (Key Stage 3).

### Step 1 — Reasoning for each option

*   **A) Correct reasoning:** The student understands that the fraction bar represents the division operator and that everything in the parentheses must be treated as a single denominator (the divisor). This shows a firm grasp of algebraic notation.
*   **B) Over-extension / Guessing:** The student may feel that algebra requires a specific "look" where terms are repeated, or they have misread the numerator. They are likely confused by the presence of the constant '+4' and believe it should be applied to both parts of the fraction.
*   **C) Incorrect Order of Operations:** The student has ignored the brackets/parentheses. They see $c \div h$ and write $\frac{c}{h}$, then simply append the $+4$ at the end. This is a common error for students who read expressions strictly from left to right without applying BIDMAS/PEMDAS rules.
*   **D) Reversal Error:** The student understands that division becomes a fraction but does not understand that division is non-commutative. They have confused the dividend (what we are dividing) with the divisor (what we are dividing by).
