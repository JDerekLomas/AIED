To give you an honest prediction for a typical Year 9 class, we have to look at the cognitive "trap" in this question. Many students see "x", "y", and "0.5" and immediately try to perform the most convenient operation without carefully rearranging the equation.

The most common error in this specific set will be students seeing the $10x$, the $6$, and the $30$, and somehow trying to make $y$ the subject or accidentally adding $6y$ to $30$ and getting $33$, leading them to **C**. Alternatively, some will substitute $0.5$ for $x$ instead of $y$, leading to a completely different (and often frustrated) calculation.

**The Breakdown:**
*   **Below Basic (The "Guessers"):** These students struggle with two-step equations and decimals. They will likely see $30 \div 10 = 3$ and then add or subtract the $0.3$ or $0.5$ to get **C**.
*   **Basic (The "Arithmetic" group):** They will try to substitute $y=0.5$ correctly: $10x - 3 = 30$. However, many will then do $30 - 3$ instead of $30 + 3$. $27 \div 10$ gives them **D**. This is where the majority of the "near misses" happen.
*   **Proficient:** These students know they need to add $3$ to $30$, getting $33$. Dividing $33$ by $10$ gives them **3.3**. Note that **C** is actually the correct answer mathematically ($10x - 3 = 30 \implies 10x = 33 \implies x = 3.3$), but it feels "too simple" to some.
*   **Advanced:** These students move quickly and accurately, though a small percentage may over-complicate it and look for a negative answer (**B**) if they mismanage the signs.
