### DIFFICULTY: 6/10
For a Year 9 student, this isn't mathematically complex in theory, but it is high in **misconception activation**. 

**The Barriers:**
1.  **Rearrangement vs. Substitution:** Many students try to rearrange the formula first and make sign errors ($10x = 30 - 6y$ is a common mistake).
2.  **Negative Coefficients:** The $-6y$ is the primary "boss" of this question. When students substitute $y=0.5$, they must calculate $10x - 3 = 30$.
3.  **The "Add/Subtract" Slip:** A significant number of students will see the minus sign and, in a moment of cognitive load, add 3 to 30 but then somehow divide by 10 incorrectly, or vice versa.

---

### Predicted Response Distributions

**Below Basic (Working at Grade 1-2):**
These students often struggle with the order of operations or inverse operations. They are likely to ignore the $x$ and $y$ labels and just perform a random operation with the numbers visible (10, 6, 30, 0.5).
*   **A=10% B=10% C=40% D=40%** (High guessing; often choosing 3.3 because $33/10$ feels "round").

**Basic (Working at Grade 3):**
These students know to substitute $0.5$ for $y$, but they often fail at the negative sign or the two-step equation logic.
*   **A=30% B=15% C=35% D=20%**
*   *Distractor C (3.3):* They get $10x - 3 = 30$, but then accidentally treat it as $10x = 33$ or they think $30 + 3 = 33$, leading to 3.3. This is the "confident mistake" zone.

**Proficient (Working at Grade 4-5):**
These students follow the process: $10x - 6(0.5) = 30 \Rightarrow 10x - 3 = 30 \Rightarrow 10x = 33 \Rightarrow x = 3.3$.
*   **A=10% B=5% C=75% D=10%**
*   Most will reach the correct answer. The ones who miss it will likely pick A (4.25) because they accidentally added 6 and then halved it, or some other procedural tangle.

**Advanced (Working at Grade 6+):**
These students have high fluency in algebraic manipulation. 
*   **A=2% B=1% C=95% D=2%**
*   Errors here are almost exclusively "fat-finger" calculator errors or rare mental arithmetic slips.

**Teacher's Note:** Option D (2.7) is a clever distractor. It comes from the calculation $10x + 3 = 30 \Rightarrow 10x = 27$, which happens if the student ignores the negative sign in front of the $6y$. In a mid-set Year 9 class, C and D will be the two most fought-over answers.
