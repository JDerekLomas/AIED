As a UK maths teacher, I find this question fascinating. On the surface, it is a one-step rearrangement. However, for a Year 9 student, it contains a significant "bottleneck" concept: **the unknown as the denominator.**

Most students are used to the structure $\frac{p}{5}=10$, where they simply multiply. When $p$ is on the bottom, it triggers a "confidence trap." Many students will perform an operation they know (multiplication) without checking if the logic holds.

### DIFFICULTY: 6/10
While the arithmetic is Year 4 level, the algebraic manipulation required to handle a denominator variable makes this harder than standard linear equations (like $2x + 3 = 11$). It ranks a 6 because it specifically targets a common structural misconception.

### Teacher's Analysis of the Errors:

*   **The "Multiply Everything" Trap (Choice B - 50):** This is the most common error for Basic/Below Basic students. They see a '5' and a '10' and a fraction bar. Their brain signals "inverse of divide is multiply," so they do $10 \times 5 = 50$. This is a classic "Confident Wrong" answer.
*   **The "Commutative Division" Trap (Choice C - 2):** Students see 10 and 5 and simply divide the larger by the smaller. They assume $\frac{5}{p}$ is the same as $\frac{p}{5}$ or they believe $10 \div 5$ must be the intended path. This is a common "guess" for students who feel 50 is too large.
*   **The "Correct Swap" (Choice A - 0.5):** Proficient students know that to solve for $p$, they must multiply by $p$ then divide by 10 (effectively swapping the 10 and the $p$). They arrive at $p = \frac{5}{10} = 0.5$.
*   **The "Over-thinker" (Choice D):** Usually chosen by students who believe you "can't have a smaller number on top of a bigger one" in an equation, or those who get frustrated and assume it's a trick question.
