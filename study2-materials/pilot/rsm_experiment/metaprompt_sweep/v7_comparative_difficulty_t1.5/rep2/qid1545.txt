As a UK maths teacher, I find this question fascinating. It isn’t testing calculation depth; it’s a high-stakes test of **Order of Operations (BIDMAS/BODMAS)**.

In a Year 9 context, this is a classic "trap" question. Students who act on instinct will read left-to-right or cluster numbers that "look" like they belong together. This is a high **misconception activation** question rather than a guessing question—students will feel very confident in their wrong answers.

### Mathematical Breakdown
*   **Left Side:** $8 + 12 \div 4$. Many students will do $8+12=20$, then $20 \div 4 = \mathbf{5}$. The correct order is $12 \div 4 = 3$, then $8 + 3 = \mathbf{11}$.
*   **Right Side:** $(8 + 12) \div 4$. The brackets force the addition first: $20 \div 4 = \mathbf{5}$.

The comparison is $11 > 5$. Therefore, the answer is **A**.

---

### DIFFICULTY: 4/10
While the arithmetic is "Difficulty 2," the cognitive load of resisting the left-to-right urge pushes this to a 4. It separates students who *know* the rule from students who can *consistently apply* the rule when it contradicts their intuition.

#### Teacher’s Commentary on the Distributions:

*   **Below Basic (Working at Year 6 level):** These students often suffer from "Optical Processing." They see the same numbers on both sides and instinctively gravitate toward **C (=)**, assuming the brackets are just "decoration." If they do calculate, they almost always go left-to-right on the LHS, getting $5 \square 5$.
*   **Basic (Low Year 9):** This is the "Confident Misconception" zone. They remember that brackets matter, so they calculate the RHS correctly as $5$. However, they still calculate the LHS left-to-right. This leads them to $5 = 5$, making **C** a very "sticky" distractor.
*   **Proficient (GCSE Grade 4/5 potential):** These students have internalised BIDMAS. They will pause at the LHS, identify the division must happen first, and calculate $11 > 5$.
*   **Advanced (GCSE Grade 7+ potential):** These students will spot the structure immediately. They may not even calculate fully; they will see that on the left, only 12 is divided by 4, whereas on the right, both 8 and 12 are divided by 4, making the left side obviously larger.
