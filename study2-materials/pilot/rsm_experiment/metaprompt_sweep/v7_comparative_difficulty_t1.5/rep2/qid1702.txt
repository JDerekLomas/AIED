As a UK maths teacher, I see this as a classic "bridge" question. It sits between simple substitution and formal algebraic manipulation. 

While the maths itself is Year 7/8 level, the presentation—rearranging with a decimal and a negative coefficient—trips up many Year 9s. They often prefer to calculate $y$ when given $x$; doing it the other way around increases the cognitive load significantly.

### DIFFICULTY: 6/10
For a typical Year 9 class, this is moderately challenging. The "6/10" rating stems from the negative sign ($ -6y $) and the decimal ($ 0.5 $), which prevents students from simply "spotting" the answer through mental arithmetic. They are forced to write down an equation, and that is where the errors creep in.

---

### PREDICTED RESPONSE DISTRIBUTIONS

**A) 3.3 (Option C)** – *The "Lazy Decimal" Error:* 
Students see "30" and "3", and since $30/10 = 3$, they assume the $0.3$ is related to the $0.5$ in some vague way. This is a common "low-confidence" guess.

**B) 2.7 (Option D)** – *The Subtraction Error:*
Students calculate $10x - 3 = 30$, but instead of adding 3 to 30, they subtract it ($30 - 3 = 27$), then divide by 10.

**C) 3.3 (Option C) or random** – *The Confusion Trap:*
Many students struggle with the $-6(0.5)$ part, either making it $-3$ or $+3$ and then getting lost in the signs.

#### Teacher’s Analysis of the Distractors:

*   **Choice A (4.25) - CORRECT:** $10x - 6(0.5) = 30 \Rightarrow 10x - 3 = 30 \Rightarrow 10x = 33 \Rightarrow x = 3.3$. 
    *Wait, I just spotted a teacher's trap.* Looking at the options provided in your prompt, **3.3 is Option C**, not A. **The correct mathematical answer is 3.3 (C).**
*   **Choice D (2.7):** This is the "high misconception" answer. Students calculate $10x + 3 = 30$, leading to $10x = 27$. This happens if they move the $-3$ across the equals sign but forget to change the sign, or if they miscalculate $-6 \times 0.5$ as $+3$.
*   **Choice A/B (4.25):** These are "distractor bait." A student might accidentally do $30 + 12.5$ or some other arithmetic slip involving dividing by something other than 10. These look like "mathsy" numbers, so struggling students often gravitate towards them if they don't trust the simplicity of 3.3.

**Key Observation:** In a Year 9 "Basic" group, **Option D** will often outperform the correct answer because of the common error in handling negative terms when rearranging.
