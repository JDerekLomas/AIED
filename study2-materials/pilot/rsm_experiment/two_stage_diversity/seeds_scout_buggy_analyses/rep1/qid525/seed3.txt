## Step 1: Identify the specific misconception or procedural error
The specific misconception or procedural error that a UK Year 9 student might apply is incorrectly adding the whole numbers and then adding the fractions without finding a common denominator, but more specifically, this could involve adding the denominators of the fractions when converting mixed numbers to improper fractions or in the addition process.

## 2: Describe how a student with this misconception would work through the question step by step
A student might start by converting the mixed numbers into improper fractions or keeping them as mixed numbers and then attempt to add them directly. For example, they might think: \(2 \frac{7}{10} + 2 \frac{1}{4} = 2 + 2 + \frac{7}{10} + \frac{1}{4}\). Then, they add the whole numbers: \(2 + 2 = 4\). For the fractions, instead of finding a common denominator, they might incorrectly add the numerators and denominators directly or add the fractions as if they had a common denominator without properly converting: \(\frac{7}{10} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{7+1}{10+4} = \frac{8}{14}\).

## 3: Identify which answer option this misconception leads to
Following the incorrect procedure described, the student would get: \(4 + \frac{8}{14}\). To express \(\frac{8}{14}\) in its simplest form, they might simplify it to \(\frac{4}{7}\), but since the question asks to leave the answer as a mixed number and they are likely to leave it as is or not simplify correctly, they would have \(4 \frac{8}{14}\). ## 4: Assess how natural/attractive this error is
This error can be quite natural and attractive because it involves a straightforward but incorrect application of operations. Students might not realize they went wrong because the steps seem logical at first glance (adding whole numbers together and then trying to add fractions directly), and it doesn't require the recognition of needing a common denominator for fraction addition. The mistake here is enticing because it avoids the slightly more complex step of finding a common denominator for \(\frac{7}{10}\) and \(\frac{1}{4}\), which is \(20\), and then adding the fractions correctly: \(\frac{7*2}{10*2} + \frac{1*5}{4*5} = \frac{14}{20} + \frac{5}{20} = \frac{19}{20}\), leading to the correct answer of \(4 \frac{19}{20}\) (option B).
