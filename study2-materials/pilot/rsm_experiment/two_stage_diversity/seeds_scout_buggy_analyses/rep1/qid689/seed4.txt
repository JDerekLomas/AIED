## Step 1: Identify the specific misconception or procedural error.
The specific misconception or procedural error that a UK Year 9 student might apply in solving the equation \(10=\frac{5}{p}\) is inverting or misapplying the operation when solving for \(p\). A common error is to multiply both sides by \(p\) but then not properly isolate \(p\), or to inversely apply operations without considering the equality.

## 2: Describe how a student with this misconception would work through the question step by step.
To specifically identify the misconception: let's say the student incorrectly thinks that to solve for \(p\), they can multiply \(\frac{5}{p}\) by \(p\) and \(10\) by \(p\), or they mistakenly use inverse operations without balance.

## 3: Detail the incorrect step-by-step process.
1. Start with \(10 = \frac{5}{p}\).
2. A student with a misconception might think, "To get rid of the fraction, I'll multiply the 10 by \(p\) and keep the 5 as is," resulting in \(10p = 5\).
3. Then, they solve for \(p\) by dividing both sides by 10, getting \(p = \frac{5}{10} = 0.5\).

## 4: Identify which answer option this misconception leads to.
This misconception leads to answer option A: \(p = 0.5\).

## 5: Assess how natural/attractive this error is.
This error is quite natural and attractive because it stems from a misunderstanding of how to handle fractions in equations and a misapplication of algebraic manipulations. The student might not even realize they went wrong because their steps seem logical at first glance: they're trying to eliminate the fraction, which seems like a reasonable step.

## 6: Validate the misconception with correct solution for comparison.
The correct steps are:
1. \(10 = \frac{5}{p}\)
2. Multiply both sides by \(p\): \(10p = 5\)
3. Divide both sides by 10: \(p = \frac{5}{10} = 0.5\)
