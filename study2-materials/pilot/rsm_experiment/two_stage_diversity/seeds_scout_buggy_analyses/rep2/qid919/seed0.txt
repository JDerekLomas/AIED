## Step 1: Identify the specific misconception or procedural error.
The specific misconception or procedural error that a UK Year 9 student might apply in this context is incorrectly applying the power of 2 to the coefficient of \(x\) instead of \(x\) itself. This can be described as "confusing the distribution of the exponent" or more simply, mistakenly calculating \(y = 3 \cdot 0.3^2\) as \(y = (3 \cdot 0.3)^2\).

## 2: Describe how a student with this misconception would work through this question step by step.
A student with this misconception would calculate \(y\) as follows:
1. They see the equation \(y = 3x^2\).
2. They substitute \(x = 0.3\) into the equation.
3. Mistakenly, they compute \(y\) as \((3 \cdot 0.3)^2\) instead of \(3 \cdot 0.3^2\).
4. So, they calculate \(3 \cdot 0.3 = 0.9\).
5. Then, they square \(0.9\) to get \(0.9^2 = 0.81\).

## 3: Determine which answer option this misconception leads to.
Following the mistaken procedure described, the student would calculate \(y = (3 \cdot 0.3)^2 = 0.9^2 = 0.81\). Therefore, this misconception leads to answer option A: \(0.81\).

This error can be quite natural and attractive because it involves a common operation (multiplying the coefficient by \(x\) before applying the exponent) that might seem logical but is incorrect in the context of the given equation. Students might not realize they went wrong if they are not thorough in checking their algebraic manipulations against the given equation or if they haven't solidified the correct procedure for handling exponents in algebraic expressions. The mistake seems logical in a misleading way: "First, do the multiplication by 3, then square the result," which could lead to a false sense of correctness without a strict check against the equation \(y = 3x^2\).
