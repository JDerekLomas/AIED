## Step 1: Identify the specific misconception or procedural error.
The specific misconception or procedural error that a UK Year 9 student might apply in this context is substituting $x = 0$ and $y = \bigstar$ into the equation $4x + 2y = 10$ and then solving for $y$ without properly isolating $y$ or incorrectly applying the equation. However, a common mistake could be not substituting $x=0$ correctly into the equation or misinterpreting how to solve for $y$ when $x=0$. A prevalent bug could be "Ignoring the coefficient of y" or more likely, "Incorrect rearrangement of the equation to solve for y".

## 2: Describe how a student with this misconception would work through this question step by step.
Let's assume the student incorrectly rearranges the equation to solve for $y$. The correct steps are:
1. Substitute $x = 0$ into $4x + 2y = 10$.
2. This gives $4(0) + 2y = 10$ or $2y = 10$.
3. Solving for $y$ yields $y = 10 / 2 = 5$.

However, a student with a misconception might:
- Write $4x + 2y = 10$ as $2y = 10 - 4x$ (correct so far, but let's see a wrong turn).
- For $x = 0$, they get $2y = 10$, which leads to $y = 5$ (which is correct, so this student's path isn't actually exhibiting a misconception yet).

A more fitting example of a misconception might involve a student thinking they can directly substitute values without solving the equation properly or misunderstanding the equation's structure.

## 3: A more common error - wrong evaluation.
A more common error could be: The student knows that $4x + 2y = 10$, and when $x=0$, the equation becomes $2y = 10$. However, instead of dividing both sides by 2, they might accidentally multiply both sides by 2 to get $y = 20$, which mistakenly leads to $y = 20$ when $x = 0$.

## 4: Identify which answer option this misconception leads to.
If a student mistakenly solves $2y = 10$ by thinking $y = 10 * 2$ (instead of $10 / 2$), they would get $y = 20$.

## 5: Assess how natural/attractive this error is.
This error can be quite natural if a student mistakenly applies arithmetic operations. For instance, if they are in a hurry or not fully attentive to the operations needed to solve the equation, they might simply double the 10 (which could seem logical for some operations but is incorrect for solving $2y = 10$).
