The specific misconception or procedural error that a UK Year 9 student might apply in this context is incorrectly rearranging the equation \(10x - 6y = 30\) to solve for \(x\) or \(y\). ## 2: Describe how a student with this misconception would work through this question step by step
Let's assume the student aims to find \(x\) when \(y = 0.5\). The correct steps are:
1. Substitute \(y = 0.5\) into the equation: \(10x - 6(0.5) = 30\).
2. Solve for \(x\): \(10x - 3 = 30\), then \(10x = 33\), and finally \(x = 3.3\).

A student with a misconception might:
- Incorrectly substitute or rearrange the equation, for instance, \(10x = 30 + 6y\), leading to \(10x = 30 + 6(0.5)\), which simplifies to \(10x = 30 + 3 = 33\), and hence \(x = 3.3\). This actually seems correct and doesn't directly reveal a misconception without further incorrect steps.

However, a more common error could involve misinterpreting the equation as \(10x = 6y + 30\), then substituting \(y = 0.5\) to get \(10x = 6(0.5) + 30 = 3 + 30 = 33\), which still yields \(x = 3.3\) correctly but through a misunderstanding.

Another potential misconception could be reversing the operation, such as thinking \(10x = 30 - 6y\), leading to \(10x = 30 - 6(0.5) = 30 - 3 = 27\), and \(x = 2.7\).
