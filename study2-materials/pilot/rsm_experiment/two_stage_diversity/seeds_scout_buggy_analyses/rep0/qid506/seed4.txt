## Step 1: Identify the specific misconception or procedural error.
The specific misconception or buggy procedure that a UK Year 9 student might apply in solving the equation \(3p = \frac{1}{12}\) is incorrectly handling the division or multiplication by fractions. A common error is treating fractions in a similar way to integers without properly considering the operation to isolate \(p\), but more precisely, the bug here can be "inverting and multiplying both sides unappropriately" or simply forgetting to divide both sides by 3 correctly.

A student with the misconception might think, "To get \(p\) on its own, I need to get rid of the 3." They might mistakenly invert the fraction or misapply operations:
1. Starting with \(3p = \frac{1}{12}\),
2. They might think to divide both sides by 3 but get confused with fractions, leading them to possibly inverting the 3 (treating it as \(\frac{1}{3}\) and then multiplying) or simply forgetting that to isolate \(p\), one should divide by 3.

The student might do: \(3p = \frac{1}{12}\), then to solve for \(p\), they multiply both sides by \(\frac{1}{3}\) (in an attempt to get rid of the 3, but incorrectly):
\[p = \frac{1}{12} \times \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{36}\]

This line of thinking leads to:
\[p = \frac{1}{36}\]
