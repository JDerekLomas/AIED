Unlike most introductory linear table problems, which present equations in **Slope-Intercept Form** ($y = mx + b$), this equation requires students to treat $y$ as a dependent variable that is not yet isolated.

### The Specific Difficulty: The "Substitution-to-Isolation" Gap
When a student identifies that $x = 0$ at the position of the star, they easily reach the intermediate stage: $4(0) + 2y = 10$. However, the specific trap lies in the final operation. 

Research shows that students often suffer from **Operation Confusion** when an equation "looks" multiplicative. Because the problem starts with $4x$ and $2y$, and the result is $10$, a significant number of students will mentally subtract the coefficient of $y$ rather than divide by it.

**How this manifests in the distractors:**
*   **The Subtraction Error:** A student reaches $2y = 10$ and intuitively "moves" the $2$ by subtracting it ($10 - 2$), leading them to an answer of **$8$** (not listed, but a common write-in error).
*   **The Coefficient Misidentification (Distractor C):** A student might see $4(0) + 2y = 10$ and ignore the coefficient $2$ entirely, simply assuming $y$ must be $10$. They then might get confused by the relationship between the numbers $10$, $4$, and $2$. If they perform $10 \div 2$, they get **$5$** (Option C), which is a common error for students who solve for the $y$-intercept correctly but then fail to account for the coefficient when $x$ is non-zero in subsequent steps.
*   **The "First Number" Trap:** Some students see $x=0$ and assume the answer is the constant $10$ or its half, without performing the algebraic steps.

**Why this is a "Visual Trap":**
The star is located at $x=0$. In a $y = mx + b$ equation, the answer is simply the constant. Because this equation is $4x + 2y = 10$, students who have over-generalized the rule "the $y$-intercept is the number at the end" will reflexively pick **5** (because $10/2 = 5$) or incorrectly pick **10** because they haven't been trained to isolate $y$ first when the equation is in standard form.
