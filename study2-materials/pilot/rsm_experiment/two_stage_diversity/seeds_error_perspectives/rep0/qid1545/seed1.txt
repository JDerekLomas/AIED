One specific reason why students might find this question difficult is the **visual "Gestalt" trap of the repeated numbers**, which leads to an erroneous application of the **Distributive Property**.

Students often perceive the expression $8 + 12 \div 4$ and $(8 + 12) \div 4$ as a demonstration of the rule $a + (b \div c) = (a + b) \div c$. Because the numbers $8, 12, \text{ and } 4$ appear in the exact same left-to-right order on both sides of the box, students who have a fragile grasp of the Order of Operations (PEMDAS/BIDMAS) often assume the expressions are **equivalent** (Choice C) without calculating.

The psychological pull of this trap is reinforced by two factors:
1.  **Similarity to legitimate distribution:** Students are trained that $x(y+z) = xy + xz$. They may intuitively but incorrectly extend this "grouping" logic to division, assuming that moving the parentheses does not change the total value as long as all components are present.
2.  **Left-to-Right Processing:** A student reading from left to right may see $8 + 12$ as the primary unit in both expressionsâ€”either because it appears first (on the left) or because it is grouped (on the right). This leads them to perform the addition first on both sides ($20 \div 4 = 5$), overlooking that on the left side, the division $12 \div 4$ must be prioritized, resulting in $8 + 3 = 11$.

Consequently, the most common error is choosing **C (=)** due to a visual "pattern match" rather than a computational analysis.
