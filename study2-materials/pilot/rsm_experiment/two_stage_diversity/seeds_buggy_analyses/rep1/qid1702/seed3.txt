As an expert in mathematical misconceptions, I identify the following buggy procedure for this Year 9 question:

### 1. The Specific Misconception: "Variable Reversal" (Substitution Swap)
This is a very common **procedural substitution error**. The student identifies that a value (0.5) must be plugged into the equation, but they substitute the given $y$-value into the $x$ position. This often stems from a lack of "checking back" and the habitual tendency to always solve for the variable on the right-hand side of an equation.

### 2. Step-by-Step Walkthrough
A student with this misconception would process the question as follows:
1.  **Identify the input:** They see the value $0.5$ in the table.
2.  **Mis-substitute:** Instead of replacing $y$ with $0.5$, they replace $x$ because $x$ is the first variable in the equation and "starts" the problem. 
    *   *Equation becomes:* $10(0.5) - 6y = 30$
3.  **Simplify:** They calculate $10 \times 0.5 = 5$.
    *   *Equation becomes:* $5 - 6y = 30$
4.  **Isolate the variable term:** They subtract 5 from both sides.
    *   $-6y = 25$
5.  **Final Division:** They divide 25 by $-6$ to find $y$.
    *   $y = 25 \div -6 = -4.166...$
6.  **"Best Fit" Adjustment:** Finding that $-4.16$ is not an option, they look at the values. They may notice that if they had ignored the negative sign in the subtraction (treating $5 - 6y$ as $6y - 5$ or simply forgetting the sign in the division), they get $30 + 5 = 35$; $35 \div 10 = 3.5$. 

However, a more precise "Variable Reversal" path that leads directly to a distractor is **incorrectly rearranging the formula first**:
1.  The student tries to make $x$ the subject but treats the equation as $y = \dots$
2.  They write: $10x = 30 + 6y$
3.  Then $x = \frac{30 + 6y}{10}$
4.  They perform the calculation: $x = \frac{30 + 6(0.5)}{10}$
5.  $x = \frac{30 + 3}{10} = \frac{33}{10} = 3.3$.
