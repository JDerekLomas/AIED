The specific bug is a form of **procedural parsing error**. ### 3. Which answer option this leads to: Option C (5)
While "5" is the result of $10 \div 2$, the misconception often isn't a "correct" division of the remaining coefficient. In Year 9, students often hold the **"Linear Shortcut" misconception**. They see the 0 in the table and assume that $y$ must simply be the "result" divided by the "number of variables," or they identify that when $x=0$, the answer is just the "other side" divided by 2. This seems correct mathematically, but for the student, it is often a "pattern match" (10 and 2 makes 5) rather than formal substitution.

### 4. How natural/attractive is this error?
This error is **highly attractive** because it yields a "clean" integer that feels right.
*   **The Trap of the Zero:** Because $x=0$ is the easiest number to substitute, students often become lazy with their formal working. They "hide" the $4x$ with their thumb and just look at $2y=10$.
*   **Lack of verification:** At Year 9, students rarely substitute their answer back into the equation to see if $4(0) + 2(5) = 10$ works. Because 5 is a factor of 10, the answer "feels" mathematically related to the numbers in the question.
