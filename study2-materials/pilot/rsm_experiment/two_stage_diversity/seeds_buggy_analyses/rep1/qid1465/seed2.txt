As an expert in mathematical misconceptions, I will focus on a pervasive error involving the Order of Operations (BIDMAS/BODMAS).

### 1. The Specific Misconception: "Strict PEMDAS/BIDMAS Linearity"
The misconception is the belief that the order of the letters in the acronym (Brackets, Indices, **Division, Multiplication, Addition, Subtraction**) must be followed in an absolute, rigid sequence. Specifically, the student believes that **Addition must always be performed before Subtraction** because 'A' comes before 'S' in the acronym. They fail to understand that Addition and Subtraction have equal priority and should be performed from left to right.

### 2. Step-by-Step Procedure
A student with this "Addition over Subtraction" bug would process the expression $3 - 5 + 4 \times 2$ as follows:

*   **Step 1:** The student correctly identifies that multiplication comes first ($M$ before $A/S$).
    Calculation: $4 \times 2 = 8$.
    Remaining expression: $3 - 5 + 8$.
*   **Step 2:** The student looks at their acronym (BIDM**AS**). Because $A$ is before $S$, they feel compelled to perform the addition ($5 + 8$) before the subtraction.
    Calculation: $5 + 8 = 13$.
*   **Step 3:** The student brings down the remaining part of the expression.
    Calculation: $3 - 13$.
*   **Final Step:** $3 - 13 = -10$.

### 3. Corresponding Answer Option
This misconception leads directly to **Answer D: $-10$**.

### 4. Attractiveness of the Error
This is an exceptionally "sticky" and attractive error for Year 9 students for several reasons:

*   **Reliance on Mnemonics:** Many students use BIDMAS as a "safety blanket" rather than understanding the underlying mathematical structure. To them, "the rules are the rules"; if 'A' is before 'S' in the song or acronym they learned, they follow it religiously.
*   **Aversion to Negative Numbers:** In the expression $3 - 5 + 8$, calculating $3 - 5$ first yields $-2$. Many students find starting a calculation with a negative result unintuitive or "messy." By jumping to $5 + 8$, they deal with positive integers for longer, which feels "more correct" to a struggling learner.
*   **Illusion of Order:** Following the letters alphabetically provides a clear, unambiguous path through the problem.
