### 1. The Specific Misconception: "Left-to-Right Priority" (LRP)
This is a specific procedural "bug" within the Order of Operations. While students are taught **BIDMAS** (Brackets, Indices, Division/Multiplication, Addition/Subtraction), many retain a primitive intuition that arithmetic should be processed linearly from left to right, regardless of the operator. They treat the expression as a "story" that unfolds chronologically.

### 2. Step-by-Step Procedure
A student with the **Left-to-Right Priority** bug would calculate the problem as follows:

**Step 1: Evaluate the Left Side ($8 + 12 \div 4$)**
*   The student starts at the left: $8 + 12 = 20$.
*   They then take that result and divide by $4$: $20 \div 4 = \mathbf{5}$.
*   *Note: They ignore the BIDMAS rule which dictates division happens before addition.*

**Step 2: Evaluate the Right Side ($(8 + 12) \div 4$)**
*   The student sees the brackets. Even with this bug, students are generally conditioned to "do brackets first."
*   They calculate $8 + 12 = 20$.
*   They divide by $4$: $20 \div 4 = \mathbf{5}$.

**Step 3: Comparison**
*   The student compares their two results: $5$ and $5$.
*   They conclude that both sides are equal.

### 4. Attractiveness of the Error
This error is **highly attractive** and often feels "correct" to the student for three reasons:

1.  **Confirmation Bias:** Because the student arrived at the same number (5) for both sides, they feel a sense of mathematical "harmony." They assume that if they reached the same integer twice, their method must be robust.
2.  **Linguistic Mapping:** In English, we read from left to right. When a student reads "Eight plus twelve divided by four," their brain naturally wants to perform the operations in the order they are spoken.
3.  **The "Brackets are Redundant" Myth:** Year 9 students often struggle to understand *why* brackets are used. By getting the same answer for both sides, the student reinforces a dangerous secondary misconception: that brackets "don't actually change anything" and are just there to look tidy. 

Because the resulting comparison ($5=5$) is so "clean," the student is very unlikely to realise they went wrong until the correct order of operations is explicitly pointed out.
