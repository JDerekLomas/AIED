### 1. The Misconception: Strict Hierarchical Addition-before-Subtraction (The "AS" Bug)
The specific misconception is that **Addition must always be performed before Subtraction** because the 'A' comes before the 'S' in BIDMAS (or BODMAS/PEMDAS). 

In reality, Addition and Subtraction have equal priority and should be performed left-to-right. However, many Year 9 students view the acronym as a rigid six-step ladder rather than a four-tiered hierarchy.

### 2. Step-by-Step Execution
A student with this misconception would follow these steps:

*   **Step 1: Multiplication.** They correctly identify that $4 \times 2$ comes first.
    *   Calculation: $3 - 5 + 8$
*   **Step 2: Strict Addition.** Looking at the remaining expression ($3 - 5 + 8$), they see the plus sign. Because 'A' comes before 'S' in the acronym, they believe they must add the 5 and 8 first. 
    *   Calculation: $5 + 8 = 13$
*   **Step 3: Final Subtraction.** They bring down the remaining terms to finish the calculation.
    *   Calculation: $3 - 13 = -10$

### 3. The Resulting Option
This misconception leads directly to **Option D: $-10$**.

*(Note: Option A is the correct answer; Option B comes from strict left-to-right processing ignoring BIDMAS; Option C comes from doing the subtraction first then multiplying the result by 2).*

### 4. Attractiveness of the Error
This is an **extremely attractive and high-confidence error** for a Year 9 student. 

*   **Reliance on the Tool:** The student feels they are being mathematically rigorous. They aren't "guessing"; they are "following the rule" (BIDMAS) exactly as they memorized it. To them, the "A before S" rule feels as absolute as "Multiplication before Addition."
*   **Aesthetic Symmetry:** By adding $5+8$ first, they are dealing with positive integers before introducing the "difficult" subtraction at the end. This feels cleaner to a developing mathematician than dealing with the negative result of $3-5$.
*   **False Confirmation:** Because $-10$ appears as an answer choice (D), the student receives immediate "validation" that their methodology was correct. They would likely finish the question and move on without a second thought, unaware that they fell into a common pedagogical trap.
