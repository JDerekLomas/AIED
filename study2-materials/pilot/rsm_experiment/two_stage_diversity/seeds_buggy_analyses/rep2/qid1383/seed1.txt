### 1. The specific misconception: The "Independent Minus" Error (Misinterpreting Order of Operations with Negatives)

This is a **structural/notation-based misconception** related to BIDMAS/BODMAS. The student views the negative sign as an operation to be applied *after* the exponentiation, rather than viewing the negative sign as a component of the value of $k$ being raised to the power. They effectively interpret $(-3)^4$ as $-(3^4)$.

### 2. How the student works through the question
A student with this misconception would follow these steps:
1. **Direct Substitution (Faulty):** They write $T = -3^4$. Because they do not automatically bracket negative substitutions, they see the negative sign and the "3" as separate entities.
2. **Exponents first (Indices):** Following the rules of BIDMAS, they correctly identify that the index (4) comes before the "subtraction" or "multiplication by -1."
3. **Calculation:** They calculate $3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 81$.
4. **Applying the Sign:** They then "attach" the minus sign to the result of the calculation. 
5. **Conclusion:** $T = -(81) = -81$.

### 3. Resulting Answer Option
This misconception leads directly to **Option C: $-81$**.

### 4. How natural/attractive is this error?
This error is **highly attractive and persistent** for several reasons:

*   **Calculator Reliance:** This is one of the most famous "traps" in secondary school mathematics. If a student types `- 3 ^ 4` into most scientific calculators (including the Casio models standard in the UK), the display will show `-81`. This confirms the student's incorrect logic. Only by typing `( - 3 ) ^ 4` would they get the correct result.
*   **Logical Consistency (in their mind):** The student feels they are correctly following BIDMAS (Indices before Subtraction). They see the "-" as a subtraction operator rather than a signifier of the number's identity.
*   **The "Rule of Two":** Many Year 9 students internalise a simplified rule: *"Two minuses make a plus."* Because there is only one visible minus sign in the substitution $T = -3^4$, their intuition fails to remind them that there are actually *four* minus signs being multiplied together ($(-3) \times (-3) \times (-3) \times (-3)$).

**Would the student realize?**
Likely not. Because -81 is "in the list," the student receives a dopamine hit of validation. Without the explicit habit of **"substitute using brackets,"** the student will move to the next question confident that they correctly applied the rules of powers and negative signs.
