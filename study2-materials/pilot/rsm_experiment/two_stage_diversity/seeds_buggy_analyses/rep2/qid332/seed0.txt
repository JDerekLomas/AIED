### 1. The Misconception: "The Floating Inverse" (Incomplete Multiplication)
This is a procedural "bug" where a student recognizes the correct inverse operation (multiplying by 3) but fails to apply the property of equality across the entire side of the equation. They treat the multiplication as a localized action that "removes" the denominator from the fraction, without realizing that **every term** on that side of the equation must be scaled to maintain balance.

### 2. Step-by-Step Student Thinking
A student with this misconception would look at Katie's suggestion ($x - 5 = 36$) and follow these steps:

*   **Step 1:** Look at the left side: $\frac{x}{3} - 5$.
*   **Step 2:** Decide to "undo" the divide-by-3.
*   **Step 3:** Perform the "shortcut": Move the 3 to the other side to multiply the 12. 
*   **Step 4:** Logic: "The 3 is gone from the fraction, so I just have $x$ left. I haven't touched the $-5$ yet, so it stays there."
*   **Result:** They write $x - 5 = 12 \times 3$, leading to $x - 5 = 36$.
*   **Step 5:** Evaluate the options: They see Tom's step (adding 5 first) as correct, and they see Katie's step (multiplying by 3) as also correct.

### 3. Answer Selection
This misconception leads the student to choose **C) Both Tom and Katie.**

In the student's mind, both routes are valid starting points. They do not realize that while Tom followed the standard order of operations (BIDMAS in reverse), Katie performed a "legal" move (multiplying) but executed it illegally by failing to multiply the constant $(-5)$.

### 4. Attractiveness of the Error
This error is **highly attractive and "natural"** for several reasons:

*   **Visual Displacement:** Students often perceive an equation as a collection of separate "blocks." To them, the "3" belongs only to the "$x$." Multiplying the "5" feels counter-intuitive because the 5 wasn't "over" the 3.
*   **Efficiency Bias:** Students are often taught to "clear the fraction" as a priority. This makes Katie's line look like a sophisticated, fast-track method.
