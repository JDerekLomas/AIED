As an expert in mathematical misconceptions, I have identified a prevalent "buggy procedure" that frequently occurs when students transition from arithmetic to algebraic manipulation.

### 1. The Misconception: The "Operator-to-Operation" Confusion (Additive Bug)
The specific misconception here is **Operation Substitution** (specifically, adding instead of multiplying). 

In the context of expanding brackets, the student correctly identifies the two terms indicated by the arrows ($-1$ and $-4$) but fails to apply the distributive law, which requires **multiplication**. Instead, they see two signed numbers placed near each other and default to **addition** (combining the terms). They interpret the task of "combining" the constants as a sum rather than a product.

### 2. Step-by-Step Student Logic
A student with this misconception would process the question as follows:
*   **Step 1:** Look at the arrows and identify the two numerical values involved: $-1$ and $-4$.
*   **Step 2:** Note that the goal is to find the result of these two terms being "joined" or "put together" as part of the expansion.
*   **Step 3:** Instead of multiplying ($-1 \times -4$), the student mentally performs an addition: "I have minus one, and I subtract another four" or "Negative one plus negative four."
*   **Step 4:** They calculate $-1 - 4 = -5$.

### 3. Selection
This misconception leads directly to **Option D: $-5$**.

*(Note: Option C (+5) would represent a secondary misconception where the student adds the absolute values but assumes "two negatives make a positive" applies to addition as well as multiplication.)*

### 4. How natural/attractive is this error?
This is an **extremely attractive error** for a Year 9 student, often termed a "low-road" cognitive slip. 

*   **Fluency vs. Concept:** By Year 9, students are often so focused on the "FOIL" or "Grid" procedure that they lose sight of the fact that brackets signify multiplication. 
*   **Linguistic Ambiguity:** The teacher may say, "Now we do the last terms, negative one and negative four." To a struggling student, "and" is linguistically synonymous with addition.
*   **Metacognition:** The student is **highly unlikely to realize they went wrong.** The number "5" feels "correct" because it is a result of $1$ and $4$.
