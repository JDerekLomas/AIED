In my experience marking this specific topic with Year 9 students, the "negative base" is one of the most reliable discriminators of algebraic understanding. 

The most common error (C) comes from students performing $3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3$ but applying the negative sign at the end, often influenced by how non-scientific calculators handle $-3^4$. The second most common error (B and D) comes from students confusing indices with multiplication (the classic $3 \times 4$ error).

**Analysis of Errors:**
*   **A (Correct):** Recognising that an even power of a negative number results in a positive product.
*   **B (Misconception):** Treating the exponent as a multiplier ($3 \times 4 = 12$) and ignoring the sign.
*   **C (Trap):** The "left-to-right" or "calculator error" where the student ignores the fact that the negative is part of the base being squared then squared again (i.e., thinking $-(3^4)$).
*   **D (Double Misconception):** Multiplying $k$ by the exponent and retaining the negative sign ($-3 \times 4 = -12$).
