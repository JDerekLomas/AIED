First, recall the formula for the volume of a sphere:  

\[
V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3
\]  

We solve for \(r\):

\[
r^3 = \frac{3V}{4\pi} \quad\Rightarrow\quad r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{3V}{4\pi}}
\]  

That corresponds to option **C**.

---

**Why students might pick other options:**  
- **Option A**: This is \(\sqrt[3]{\frac{4V\pi}{3}}\), which comes from rearranging \(V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\) wrongly by multiplying \(V\) by \(\pi\) instead of dividing. A common error is to swap \(\pi\) position incorrectly.  
- **Option B**: This is \(\sqrt[3]{\frac{4V}{3\pi}}\), which could come from incorrectly starting with \(V = \frac{4}{3} r^3\) (missing \(\pi\)) and then trying to compensate by putting \(\pi\) in denominator, but still wrong.  
- **Option D**: This is \(\frac{3\sqrt[3]{V}}{4\pi}\), which is dimensionally wrong (cube root of volume is length, but then dividing by \(4\pi\) gives a length smaller by factor \(4\pi\), not matching correct formula). This might come from misremembering \(r = \frac{3V}{4\pi}\) and then trying to cube root \(V\) first.

---

**Difficulty analysis:**  
This is a *rearrangement* question, not a calculation-with-numbers question. Students have to recall the correct volume formula and rearrange it step-by-step. Common traps:  
1. Misplacing \(\pi\) in the rearrangement.  
2. Confusing whether to divide or multiply by \(\pi\) at the end.  
3. Not recognizing that \(r^3 = \frac{3V}{4\pi}\) and instead solving \(V = \pi r^3 \times \frac{4}{3}\) wrongly.

Given the options are symbolic, weaker students might guess based on what "looks familiar" rather than deriving carefully.
