Let’s break this down step-by-step.

The equation is:  

\[
10x - 6y = 30
\]  

Table given:

\[
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}
\hline
\( x \) & \( \bigstar \) \\
\hline
\( y \) & \( 0.5 \) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\]

So \( y = 0.5 \), \( x = \bigstar \).

Substitute \( y = 0.5 \) into equation:  

\[
10x - 6(0.5) = 30  
10x - 3 = 30  
10x = 33  
x = 3.3
\]

So the correct answer is \( 3.3 \), which is option **C**.

---

**Possible errors:**

1. **Misreading table** — swapping \( x \) and \( y \) values:  
If they think \( x = 0.5 \) and \( y \) is unknown, then \( 10(0.5) - 6y = 30 \) → \( 5 - 6y = 30 \) → \( -6y = 25 \) → \( y \approx -4.166\ldots \), which is close to \( -4.25 \) (B). This is the most likely trap.

2. **Sign error** when rearranging:  
\( 10x - 3 = 30 \) → \( 10x = 27 \) → \( x = 2.7 \) (D). This could come from mistakenly thinking \( -6 \times 0.5 = -3 \) but then incorrectly adding 3 to 30 → 27 instead of 33.

3. **A) 4.25** — could come from adding 3 to 30 to get 33, then dividing by 10 wrongly? Not likely. More likely from swapping \( x \) and \( y \) but also a sign mistake in rearrangement:  
\( 10(0.5) - 6y = 30 \) → \( 5 - 6y = 30 \) → \( -6y = 25 \) → \( y = -25/6 \approx -4.17 \), not positive 4.25, so A is probably a random guess.

---

**Difficulty considerations:**  
This is a straightforward substitution into a linear equation, but the table is presented with \( x \) in first row and value missing, \( y \) in second row given as 0.5. This layout is slightly unusual — students may misread which variable is which. The main trap (B) is plausible if they swap \( x \) and \( y \) in their heads.

---

**Predictions by ability group:**

- **Below basic**: Likely to swap variables (leading to B) or guess randomly. Some might compute correctly but many will fall into the sign/variable swap trap.  
- **Basic**: Many still fall for the variable swap (B), but more get correct than below basic.  
- **Proficient**: Most get C, some still slip into B trap if rushing.  
- **Advanced**: Nearly all get C, rare errors.
