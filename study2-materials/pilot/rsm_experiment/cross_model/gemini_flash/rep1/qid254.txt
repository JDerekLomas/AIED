As a UK maths teacher, I evaluate this question based on two specific "traps": the treatment of zero when calculating a mean and the order of operations (BIDMAS/BODMAS).

**The Challenge:**
1.  **The Zero Problem:** Lower-ability students often ignore zero when calculating the mean, believing it "doesn't count." This leads them to divide by 4 (Option C).
2.  **The Notation Problem (The BIDMAS Trap):** Option B is the "calculator error." Students often type exactly this into a non-fraction calculator ($0+1+3+4+7 \div 5$), which results in $0+1+3+4+1.4$.
3.  **The Subtle Trick:** Option A is mathematically identical to the correct process because $0+1+3+4+7$ is the same as $1+3+4+7$. However, many students are trained that they *must* show the zero, or they will overlook that the sum is divided by 5 (the count including the zero).

**Performance Prediction:**
*   **Below Basic:** Many will ignore the zero entirely and choose C. Others will see the numbers laid out in a string in B and assume that's "how you write it."
*   **Basic:** This group is prone to the classic misconception that zero doesn't affect the denominator. They will split between C and A, with many choosing A because it "looks right" but still feels like a trick.
*   **Proficient:** These students know to divide by 5, but many will be tripped up by the lack of brackets in Option B or will overthink Option A, wondering if the zero *must* be written. Most will correctly identify that A is the only mathematically sound calculation.
*   **Advanced:** These students will instantly recognize that $x + 0 = x$ and that Option B is incorrect due to BIDMAS. They will select A confidently.
