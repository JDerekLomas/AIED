**Analysis of the Distractors:**
*   **Option A (Correct):** Students recognize that the sum of $0, 1, 3, 4, 7$ is the same as $1, 3, 4, 7$, but they must divide by 5 because there are five values in the data set.
*   **Option B (The Calculator Trap):** This is a very common error. If entered into a scientific calculator exactly as written without brackets, it would follow BIDMAS ($7 \div 5$ first). However, many low-ability students view this as a written "instruction" and see it as correct.
*   **Option C (The Zero-Is-Nothing Trap):** This targets students who think that because zero has no value, it shouldn't be counted in the divisor. This is the classic "mean of a data set with a zero" misconception.
*   **Option D:** Often chosen by high-ability students who are overthinking, potentially looking for a version that explicitly writes the $+ 0$ in the numerator, or those who correctly identify that B is mathematically incorrect due to BIDMAS and then mistakenly doubt A because the 0 is "missing."
