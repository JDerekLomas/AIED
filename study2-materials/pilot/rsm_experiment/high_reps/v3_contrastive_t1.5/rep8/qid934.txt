As an experienced UK maths teacher, I’ve analyzed this specific question. The equation $4x + 2y = 10$ is presented in an implicit form, which is inherently more difficult for students than the standard $y = mx + c$. 

To find the star, students must substitute $x=0$. This reduces the equation to $2y=10$. 

**Teacher’s Analysis:**
*   **The Trap (C):** $x=0$ is the easiest substitution, but many students "switch off" the $4x$ and simply look at the $10$. Students who struggle with two-step equations or the concept of a coefficient often guess $5$ because $10 \div 2 = 5$. However, some students simply see the numbers 4, 2, and 10 and try to relate them linearly.
*   **The Misconception (B):** A very common error in UK classrooms is students seeing the $4$ and the $2$ and subtract/halving or trying to find a "pattern" from the equation itself without substituting.
*   **The Procedural Error (D):** Dividing $10$ by $4$ (the first coefficient they see) leads to $2.5$.
*   **The Calculation Error (A):** Multiplying $4 \times 5$ or $10 \times 2$ incorrectly.

This specific question is actually a "gift" if students understand that $x=0$ eliminates a term, but the implicit format ($ax+by=c$) causes significant friction for lower-ability students who are only used to $y=...$ tables.
