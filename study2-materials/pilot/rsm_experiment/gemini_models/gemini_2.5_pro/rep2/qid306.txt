As an experienced UK maths teacher marking 200 Year 9 papers, here is my analysis and prediction based on common student misconceptions.

**My thought process:**

*   **The Core Concept:** The question tests the order of operations (BIDMAS/BODMAS). This is a foundational concept taught for years, but one where misconceptions are incredibly persistent.

*   **Problem P (4 - 5 + 6):** This is the subtler trap. Students are drilled on "BIDMAS", and many misinterpret this to mean "Addition always comes before Subtraction". They see "5 + 6" and jump on it, ignoring the fact that Addition and Subtraction have equal priority and should be done left-to-right. The correct first step is `4 - 5`.

*   **Problem Q (4 + 5 x 6):** This is a more straightforward test of BIDMAS. Multiplication clearly comes before Addition. Students who forget or ignore the rule will simply work left-to-right (`4 + 5`). This is a classic "trap" for those with a weaker grasp of the topic.

*   **Analyzing the Options and Student Profiles:**
    *   **A (P: 4-5, Q: 4+5):** This student correctly identifies the left-to-right rule for P but then incorrectly applies the same rule to Q, ignoring BIDMAS. This is the "one rule for everything" approach, common in students who struggle to retain multiple rules.
    *   **B (P: 4-5, Q: 5x6):** **This is the correct answer.** It requires knowing both rules: left-to-right for operations of equal priority, and multiplication before addition. Students who have properly understood BIDMAS will choose this.
    *   **C (P: 5+6, Q: 4+5):** This is a combination of the two most common errors. The student wrongly prioritises Addition over Subtraction in P, and wrongly works left-to-right in Q. This indicates a very weak understanding or a complete guess.
    *   **D (P: 5+6, Q: 5x6):** This is the most "attractive" wrong answer. The student has remembered to apply BIDMAS to Q but has *over-applied* it to P, falling into the "Addition before Subtraction" trap. This is a very common error for students who have learned the rule but don't yet fully grasp the nuance of the D/M and A/S pairings.
