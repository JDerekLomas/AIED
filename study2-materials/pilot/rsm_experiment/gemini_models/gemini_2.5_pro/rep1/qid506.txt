Alright, I've got the red pen out and I've been wading through these papers. This question, a classic one-step equation but with a fractional answer, always sorts the students out. You see the same familiar misconceptions crop up every year. Here’s my analysis and prediction based on a typical Year 9 cohort.

**Thinking Process & Error Analysis:**

*   **The Correct Method:** The equation is \( 3p=\frac{1}{12} \). To isolate *p*, you must divide both sides by 3. Dividing a fraction by a whole number means you multiply the denominator: \( p = \frac{1}{12 \times 3} = \frac{1}{36} \). This is the correct answer, **B**.

*   **Analysis of Distractor A) p = 1/4:**
    *   **Error:** This is the most common and attractive error. The student thinks "to get rid of the 3, I need to divide by 3." They then incorrectly apply this operation to the denominator of the fraction: \( \frac{1}{12 \div 3} = \frac{1}{4} \).
    *   **Attraction:** It "feels" right. They are "doing the same thing to both sides" in a visual but mathematically incorrect way. Students who lack a solid foundation in dividing fractions fall for this hook, line, and sinker.

*   **Analysis of Distractor C) p = 4:**
    *   **Error:** This is a combination of errors. The student might get to \( \frac{1}{4} \) using the mistake above, and then think the answer is the denominator, or they might flip the fraction (a common mistake when dividing). Or they might multiply 12 by 3 to get 36, get completely lost, and guess at the '4' which they derived from 12 ÷ 3.
    *   **Attraction:** This is less common but happens with students who are very muddled about operations. They associate '12' and '3' with '4' and just grab it.

*   **Analysis of Distractor D) p = 1/9:**
    *   **Error:** This comes from seeing '3' and '12' and thinking about subtracting. "The difference between 12 and 3 is 9", leading them to \( \frac{1}{9} \). Alternatively, they might do \( p = \frac{1}{12-3} \).
    *   **Attraction:** This is an error made by students who are really struggling and just start "number grabbing" – performing a random operation on the numbers they see without understanding the algebraic structure.

**Student Profile Predictions:**

*   **Below Basic:** These students often panic with fractions and algebra combined. They will most likely fall for the most visually simple, "obvious" trap (A) or resort to random operations (D).
*   **Basic:** A significant portion of this group will also fall for trap (A). They understand the goal is to "divide by 3" but don't know the correct procedure for fractions. A few will get it right, but they are the most likely group to choose (A).
*   **Proficient:** These students generally know the correct method. They will divide by 3 and are much more likely to execute the fraction division correctly. A small number might make a careless slip and pick (A), but most will get (B).
*   **Advanced:** These students see this as a straightforward question. They will confidently and quickly divide by 3, get the correct answer (B), and move on. Errors would be extremely rare.
