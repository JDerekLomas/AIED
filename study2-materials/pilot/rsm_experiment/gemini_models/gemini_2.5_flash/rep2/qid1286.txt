The formula for the volume of a sphere is $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$.
We need to make *r* the subject.

Step 1: Get rid of the fraction. Multiply both sides by 3.
$3V = 4\pi r^3$

Step 2: Isolate $r^3$. Divide both sides by $4\pi$.
$r^3 = \frac{3V}{4\pi}$

Step 3: Isolate *r*. Take the cube root of both sides.
$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{3V}{4\pi}}$

This is option C. This is the correct answer.

Let's break down the incorrect options and the likely errors:

**Option A: $ \sqrt[3]{\frac{4 V \pi}{3}} \mathrm{~cm} $**
Error: The $\frac{4}{3}$ has been inverted to $\frac{3}{4}$, but the $\pi$ has been left in the numerator when it should have gone to the denominator. Or, more simply, students have inverted the entire $\frac{4\pi}{3}$ as if it was $\frac{3}{4\pi}$, but then stuck the $V$ on top. It looks like they tried to do $V \div (\frac{3}{4\pi})$ or $V \times (\frac{4\pi}{3})$ and took the cube root. The "attractive" part is perhaps multiplying by $4\pi$ and dividing by 3, getting them in the 'wrong' order in the final fraction. $V \times \frac{4\pi}{3}$ gives $r^3 = \frac{4V\pi}{3}$. This is a common error for students who flip *some* terms but not all correctly. Often, it's about seeing $V = (\text{stuff}) r^3$, so $r^3 = V \div (\text{stuff})$, but then doing $V \times (\text{stuff})$ instead of $V \times \frac{1}{\text{stuff}}$.

**Option B: $ \sqrt[3]{\frac{4 V}{3 \pi}} \mathrm{~cm} $**
