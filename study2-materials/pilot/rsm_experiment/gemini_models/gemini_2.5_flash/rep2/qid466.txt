**Analysis of Errors:**

*   **Correct Answer (A: 4/3):** This requires understanding that \( a \times \frac{1}{a} = 1 \) and also that \( -\times - = + \). So, you need the reciprocal of 3/4, which is 4/3, AND it needs to be positive to counteract the negative 3/4 and result in -1. ( \( -\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{4}{3} = -1 \)) Ah, careful here! This is actually the trick. The product *is* -1. So if we have -3/4, and we multiply by something to get -1, then that something must be a positive 4/3.

    Let's re-evaluate:
    \( -\frac{3}{4} \times \star = -1 \)
    To isolate star, divide both sides by -3/4:
    \( \star = \frac{-1}{-\frac{3}{4}} \)
    \( \star = -1 \times -\frac{4}{3} \)
    \( \star = \frac{4}{3} \)
    So yes, A is correct. The common "multiplying by the reciprocal" rule typically focuses on \( a \times \frac{1}{a} = 1 \). Students might forget that if the starting number is negative, and the product is negative, then the "reciprocal" itself (the numerical value) must be positive. Or, they might think \( - \times - = + \), and since the answer is negative, one of the factors must be negative and the other positive.
    The rule for \( a \times \star = 1 \) where \( \star = 1/a \) is simpler.
    Here it is \( -a \times \star = -1 \).
    So, \( \star = \frac{-1}{-a} = \frac{1}{a} \). So, we need the positive reciprocal.

This is a very common error. They want the 'negative reciprocal' because the result is negative, or they just 'flip' the original fraction *and* keep its sign.
