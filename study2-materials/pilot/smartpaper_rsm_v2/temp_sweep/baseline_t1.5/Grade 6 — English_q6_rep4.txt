As an experienced teacher in a Class 6 English government school in India, given the demographics and challenges, here's my assessment of student performance on the question "Which line from the poem do you like the most and why?" with a max score of 1.5.

First, let me clarify my interpretation of "full marks" (1.5). It requires:
1.  **Denoting a line from the poem:** This means quoting the line or accurately referencing it.
2.  **Mentioning why:** This implies a *reason* for liking it, even a simple one.

Here's my breakdown by ability group:

**Struggling: 5% full marks**
*   **Language barriers:** Most struggle significantly with reading and comprehending the poem itself. Identifying a "favorite" line requires understanding multiple lines and then making a choice.
*   **Recall vs. reasoning:** This question primarily requires *reasoning* and personal reflection, which is difficult without foundational comprehension. If they don't understand the poem, how can they have a favorite line or explain why?
*   **Common mistakes:**
    *   Not being able to read/copy a line accurately.
    *   Writing "I like the poem" or "I like this line" without providing a specific line or reason.
    *   Writing a random word or phrase not from the poem.
    *   Giving a reason completely unrelated to the poem or the specific line.
    *   Remaining silent/writing nothing due to lack of comprehension or confidence.
*   **Why 5%?** A very small fraction might, through sheer luck or imitation, copy a line and provide a generic, simple reason like "it is nice" or "it is beautiful," which might scrape by as a minimal explanation for 1.5 marks. Or they might have been specifically helped to pick one line during class.

**Basic: 25% full marks**
*   **Language barriers:** They can usually decode words but struggle with deeper meaning. They might have a surface-level understanding after translation or repeated readings.
*   **Recall vs. reasoning:** They can often *recall* a line, especially if it was highlighted in class or is simple/memorable. The "why" part is harder. They might give very simple reasons.
*   **Common mistakes:**
    *   Copying a line correctly but providing a very weak or general "why" (e.g., "I like it because it's good"). Depending on the strictness, this might just get full marks.
    *   Misremembering parts of the line.
    *   Confusing the meaning of a line with another.
*   **Why 25%?** After extensive scaffolding (reading aloud, explaining meanings, perhaps even suggesting what *might* be liked about certain lines), a quarter of these students could pick a line and provide a simple, acceptable reason ("It makes me happy," "It talks about birds," "It is easy to understand"). Their reasons might be brief and simple, but they would meet the criteria.

**Competent: 70% full marks**
*   **Language barriers:** They can read the poem with fair comprehension, often without needing line-by-line translation. They grasp the main idea and some nuances.
*   **Recall vs. reasoning:** They can identify and recall a line easily. They can articulate a reason, even if it's not profound.
*   **Common mistakes:**
    *   Their reasons might be somewhat generic or similar to others, but they will be coherent and connected to the line.
    *   Slight grammatical errors in their explanation, but the meaning is clear.
*   **Why 70%?** These students understand the poem well enough to form an opinion. They can correctly identify a line and provide a logical (even if simple) reason for liking it. For example, "I like the line 'The little bird sang joyfully' because it shows happiness" or "I like 'The sun shines bright' because it is a happy image."

**Advanced: 95% full marks**
*   **Language barriers:** They comprehend the poem well, including figurative language (if any).
*   **Recall vs. reasoning:** They can accurately recall a line and articulate a clear, specific, and often more nuanced reason for their choice.
*   **Common mistakes:** Very few. Perhaps a minor grammatical slip, but the answer will be robust.
*   **Why 95%?** These students will confidently select a line, quote it correctly, and provide a thoughtful reason for their preference. They might connect it to their own experiences, emotions, or specific imagery.
