This is a great question to assess both comprehension and critical thinking, but it presents some unique challenges in my classroom.

Let's break down the question and the rubric:
*   **Question:** "Do you think the king's questions are important in real life? Give one reason."
*   **Scoring Rubric:** The king wanted to answer (based on the reading passage) the right time to do everything, the right thing to do, and the right people to work with. Any valid reason. Max score: 1.

The rubric allows for "any valid reason," which is helpful, but it also implicitly requires understanding *what* the king's questions *were*. Many students, especially those struggling with reading, might not even recall the questions accurately, let alone connect them to real life.

Here's my breakdown by student group:

**Struggling (35% of class):**
*   **Language barriers:** Many would struggle to read the question itself, let alone the passage well enough to recall the king's questions. They might grasp "king" and "important" but struggle with "real life" and "reason."
*   **Recall vs. Reasoning:** This requires *both*. First, recall the questions, then reason about their real-life importance. The recall part would be a major hurdle.
*   **Specificity:** While "any valid reason" is broad, they still need to connect it to the *spirit* of the king's questions.
*   **Common Mistakes:**
    *   Misunderstanding the question entirely.
    *   Giving a "yes/no" answer without a reason.
    *   Giving a reason unrelated to the king's actual questions (e.g., "Yes, because kings are important").
    *   Unable to formulate an answer in English.

**struggling: 5% full marks**
*(This 5% would likely be students who, despite overall struggles, had a momentary flash of understanding during the lesson, or perhaps even a friend whispered an idea to them. Very few would independently achieve this.)*

**Basic (35% of class):**
*   **Language barriers:** They could likely read the question, though perhaps slowly. Recalling the king's questions might require some prompting or re-reading the relevant part of the text.
*   **Recall vs. Reasoning:** They might recall *some* of the questions (e.g., "what is the right time?"). Connecting it to "real life" and articulating a reason in English would be challenging.
*   **Specificity:** They might give a very general reason. "Yes, because it helps you know what to do." This *could* be considered valid, but the language might be clunky.
*   **Common Mistakes:**
    *   Giving a vague reason that doesn't fully demonstrate understanding.
    *   Struggling to articulate their thoughts clearly in English.
    *   Only recalling one of the king's questions and basing their reason on that.

**basic: 25% full marks**
*(Many would get a partial mark if available, but for *full* marks, the reason needs to be clear enough. Some would manage a simple, valid reason like "Yes, because we need to know what to do at the right time.")*

**Competent (20% of class):**
*   **Language barriers:** They would read the question and the passage with reasonable comprehension.
*   **Recall vs. Reasoning:** They would likely recall the king's questions easily. Connecting them to real life and providing a reason would be within their ability.
*   **Specificity:** They would likely offer a more coherent and relevant reason.
*   **Common Mistakes:**
    *   Perhaps a slightly less well-articulated reason, but generally correct.
    *   Focusing on only one of the king's questions for their reason, rather than the collective importance.

**competent: 70% full marks**
*(Most would be able to articulate a valid reason like "Yes, because knowing the right time and the right people helps us succeed in our work.")*

**Advanced (10% of class):**
*   **Language barriers:** No issue at all.
*   **Recall vs. Reasoning:** They would not only recall the questions but also quickly grasp the philosophical implications and apply them to real life effectively.
*   **Specificity:** Their reasons would be clear, concise, and directly relevant to the core themes of the king's questions.
*   **Common Mistakes:** None for this specific item. They might even offer more nuanced reasons than required.
