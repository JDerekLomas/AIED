Let's break down the question for Class 8 students in Indian government schools, considering their background.

**Question:** A gardener has collected waste of plants, vegetables and fruits in a pit. Does gardener notice any change after some days? Give reasons and evaluate if this is beneficial for soil health.

**Contrastive Analysis:**

*   **Prior Knowledge Assumed:**
    *   **Implicitly:** The concept of decomposition, or at least that organic matter "goes bad" or "rotts." They would likely have seen fruits/vegetables decay at home.
    *   **Science Curriculum:** Class 8 science curricula in India generally cover topics like microbes (bacteria, fungi) and their role. The concept of composting might be introduced or alluded to, often in the context of waste management and agriculture. Key terms like "manure," "compost," or "fertilizer" might be familiar, though not necessarily linked directly to this specific process.
    *   **Everyday Experience:** Most students would have observed waste decaying or attracting insects, which gives them a rudimentary understanding of change.

*   **Language Demands:**
    *   "**Gardener has collected waste...in a pit.**": Simple, common words. "Waste" and "pit" are very understandable.
    *   "**Does gardener notice any change after some days?**": Straightforward yes/no question, then elaboration. "Change" is a fundamental word.
    *   "**Give reasons**": This requires explanation, moving beyond simple observation. It demands connecting the observation to a cause.
    *   "**evaluate if this is beneficial for soil health**": This is the most demanding part.
        *   "Evaluate" requires making a judgment.
        *   "Beneficial" is a relatively common word, but might be slightly less automatic for some than "good."
        *   "Soil health" is a science-specific term, but "mitti" (soil) is ubiquitous, and "health" in this context is often understood as "fertility" or "quality." The *concept* of soil fertility from adding organic matter might be discussed in the context of farming.

    *   **Overall English Difficulty:** The vocabulary is generally accessible at a Class 8 level, but the requirement to "give reasons and evaluate" elevates it from simple comprehension to expressing scientific understanding in English. *   **Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**
    *   **Does gardener notice any change...?**: This is primarily **recall/observation-based**, likely drawing on everyday experience.
    *   **Give reasons**: This requires **reasoning** to connect the observation (decay) to scientific principles (microbes, decomposition). It also draws on **recall** of related science concepts.
    *   **Evaluate if this is beneficial for soil health**: This is primarily **application/reasoning**. Students need to apply their understanding of decomposition and organic matter to the concept of soil health. It might also involve **recall** if the specific benefits of compost were taught.

*   **Difficulty Compared to Typical Textbook Exercises:**
    *   **Slightly higher complexity than direct recall.** A typical Class 8 exercise might be "What is decomposition?" or "List the benefits of compost."
    *   This question is a multi-part, scenario-based question that integrates observation, explanation, and evaluation. It asks for *why* and *so what?* rather than just *what?* This moves beyond rote memorization.
    *   The "evaluate" part often comes with a higher cognitive load than just "list" or "describe."

**Summary of Difficulty Drivers:**

*   **Relatable Scenario:** The scenario of plant waste decaying is very common and relatable, making the first part (noticing a change) easy.
*   **Core Science Concept:** Decomposition is a fundamental concept taught, even if terminology like "compost" isn't explicitly used, the idea that waste "rots" or "breaks down" is understood.
*   **Language Demands for Explanation:** Articulating *reasons* and *evaluating benefits* in precise English can be challenging for those with limited proficiency, even if they grasp the concept in Hindi. They might get the "yes, there's a change" but struggle with the "why" and "so what."
*   **Evaluation Component:** The evaluative part ("beneficial for soil health") requires a higher-order thinking skill compared to simple recall.
