1.  **Prior Knowledge Assumed:**
    *   **"Poet"**: Most Class 7 students would know this word, though its exact role in literature might be vague for some. They read poems.
    *   **"Writing style"**: This is the crucial, high-level concept. It requires understanding that authors have *distinct ways* of writing, not just what they write *about*. This goes beyond merely comprehending a text to analyzing its *form*.
    *   **Knowledge of suitable descriptive words**: They need a vocabulary of literary terms or general adjectives that can be applied to writing (e.g., simple, complex, sad, joyful, descriptive, straightforward, direct, imaginative, emotional, flowery, difficult). While the rubric gives examples, the student must spontaneously recall one.

2.  **Language Demands (for students with limited English):**
    *   **"Describes"**: A common verb, likely understood.
    *   **"Poet's"**: Possessive form, probably manageable.
    *   **"Writing style"**: This phrase is the biggest hurdle.
        *   "Writing": Common word.
        *   "Style": This word is tricky. In everyday English, "style" can mean fashion, a way of doing something, or flair. In this context, it's a specific literary analysis term. Students might understand "how they write" in Hindi (जैसे कवि लिखते हैं / लेखक की लेखन शैली), but connecting that precise meaning to "writing style" in English might be difficult without direct instruction in this analytical terminology.
    *   **Output language**: The student needs to provide an English word (e.g., "simple," "descriptive," "informal," "direct"). This requires both understanding the concept and having the English vocabulary to express it. The rubric's examples are common adjectives, which helps, but generating one unprompted might be hard.

3.  **Recall, Application, or Reasoning?**
    *   This question requires **application and reasoning**, rather than simple recall.
    *   **Application**: They must apply their understanding of "writing style" to a specific poet's work (which is *implied* as prior context, likely a poem they just read). This analysis is a higher-order thinking skill than just comprehending the poem's content.
    *   **Reasoning**: They need to evaluate the poem's characteristics and infer a suitable descriptive word. This moves beyond merely identifying elements of a poem (e.g., stanza, rhyme) to interpreting its overall form.

    *   **Analyzing "writing style" is generally a more advanced analytical task.** While they might discuss *the message* of a poem, dissecting *how* the poet conveyed that message through their style is less common.
