## Step 1: Understand the question and the transformation involved
The question asks about the transformation that maps the graph of \(y=f(x)\) to the graph of \(y=f(x)+1\). This involves understanding how adding a constant to a function affects its graph. Adding 1 to \(f(x)\) shifts the graph of \(f(x)\) upwards by 1 unit.

## 2: Recall the effect of adding a constant to a function
When a constant is added to \(f(x)\), i.e., \(y = f(x) + c\), the graph of \(y = f(x)\) is translated (or shifted) vertically by \(c\) units. If \(c > 0\), the shift is upwards; if \(c < 0\), the shift is downwards. For \(y = f(x) + 1\), \(c = 1 > 0\), so the graph of \(y = f(x)\) is shifted upwards by 1 unit.

## 3: Translate the transformation into vector form
A vertical shift upwards by 1 unit corresponds to a translation by a vector \(\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]\), since there is no horizontal shift (the x-coordinate does not change) and the vertical shift is +1.

## 4: Analyze the options given
- A: Translation by vector \(\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]\) matches our understanding of shifting the graph of \(y=f(x)\) to \(y=f(x)+1\).
- B: Translation by vector \(\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ -1 \end{array}\right]\) would shift the graph downwards, not upwards.
- C: Translation by vector \(\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right]\) would shift the graph to the right, not upwards or downwards.
- D: Translation by vector \(\left[\begin{array}{l} -1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right]\) would shift the graph to the left, not upwards or downwards.
