To approach this question, let's consider what makes a number rational and the common misconceptions students might have, particularly regarding $\pi$ and $\frac{1}{\pi}$.

A rational number is any number that can be expressed as the quotient or fraction $\frac{p}{q}$ of two integers, where $p$ is the numerator and $q$ is the non-zero denominator. 

- $\pi$ is an irrational number because it cannot be expressed as a simple fraction and its decimal representation goes on infinitely without repeating.
- $\frac{1}{\pi}$ is also irrational because $\pi$ is irrational, and the reciprocal of an irrational number (other than $\pm 1$) is irrational.

They might mistakenly think $\pi$ is rational because they are familiar with it being approximately 3.14, but they are less likely to have deep insights into $\frac{1}{\pi}$. Given the options, they might incorrectly identify $\pi$ as rational due to its common decimal approximation.

   - C: Both - 5% (unlikely to have understanding to consider both)
   - D: Neither - 45% (some may recognize $\pi$ is not rational and are cautious)

2. **Basic**: At this level, students have a basic understanding of rational and irrational numbers but might still struggle with the properties of $\pi$ and its reciprocal.
