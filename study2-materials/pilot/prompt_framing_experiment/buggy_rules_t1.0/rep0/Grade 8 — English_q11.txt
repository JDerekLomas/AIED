Step 1: Specific procedural steps
1. **Semantic Decoding**: Translate the prompt (appearance, history, community) from English to internal conceptual framework (often Hindi).
2. **Knowledge Retrieval**: Access a specific mental schema for a local landmark (Banyan/Peepal tree) and associated cultural narratives.
3. **Syntactic Mapping**: Formulate sentences using "There is," "It has," or "People worship," requiring subject-verb agreement.
4. **Lexical Selection**: Recall specific English adjectives for physical description (gnarled, shade, thick, ancient) and abstract nouns (blessings, gathering, tradition).
5. **Structural Assembly**: Maintain paragraph cohesion over 5-6 sentences while adhering to the three-part prompt constraint.
6. **Orthographic Execution**: Apply correct spelling and punctuation (capitalization/periods).

Step 2: Systematic "Buggy Rules"
1. **L1 Interference (Transliteration)**: Applying Hindi syntax to English (e.g., "My village in one old tree is" instead of "There is an old tree in my village").
2. **The "One-Line" Bug**: Providing a single descriptive sentence because the effort of English composition exhausts cognitive load, ignoring the 5-6 sentence requirement.
3. **Aspect Neglect**: Addressing appearance but omitting history or community importance due to limited vocabulary for abstract concepts.
4. **Tense Inconsistency**: Switching between present (appearance) and past (history) incorrectly (e.g., "It was very big" when it is still alive).
5. **Copy-Prompt Strategy**: Reusing words from the prompt (appearance, history) as the only content words because the student doesn't know synonyms.

Step 3: Student Population Analysis
Grade 8 students in this demographic often possess "Functional Literacy" but struggle with "Expressive Fluency." In an open-ended format (recall) vs. multiple choice (recognition), the lack of scaffolding is a significant barrier.
