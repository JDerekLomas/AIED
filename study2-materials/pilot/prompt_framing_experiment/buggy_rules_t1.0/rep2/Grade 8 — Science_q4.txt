4.  **Functional Application**: Deduce that melting gold/silver requires the maximum heat, thus requiring the outermost zone.
5.  **Language Encoding**: Formulate the response in English, correctly using technical terms ("outermost," "non-luminous," or "complete combustion") as required by the rubric.

Step 2: Buggy rules/Systematic errors
1.  **Luminous Bias**: Associating "brightest" or "most visible" (middle zone) with "hottest." Students often pick the luminous yellow zone because it is the most salient part of a flame.
2.  **Color-Heat Inversion**: Misinterpreting the color blue as "cool" (based on household water taps) and yellow/red as "hot."
3.  **Terminology Confusion**: Swapping "luminous" and "non-luminous" or "complete" and "incomplete" combustion.
4.  **English Translation Bug**: Students may know the concept in Hindi (बाहरी क्षेत्र) but fail to retrieve the specific English term "outermost" or "non-luminous" under test pressure.
5.  **Reasoning Omission**: Providing the correct zone but failing to provide the "reason" (highest temperature), leading to a 0 score if the rubric is strictly applied for the full 1 mark.

Step 3: Population Considerations
1.  **Recall vs. Recognition**: This is a short-answer question, not multiple choice. There are no cues to help narrow down the zones.
2.  **Language Barrier**: For Hindi-medium students in Grade 8, articulating the "reason" in English is a significant hurdle, even if the concept is understood.
3.  **Rote Learning Reliance**: Students often memorize the "what" but forget the "why," or vice versa.

Step 4: Holistic Analysis
While this is a standard textbook question, the combination of two-part requirements (Zone + Reason), the counter-intuitive nature of the blue zone being the hottest, and the English language proficiency barrier for economically weaker students suggests a high error rate.
