Step 2: Systematic "Buggy Rules" and Errors
1. Over-generalization/Category Collapse: Students provide the category ("Bacteria") but forget the specific name, or vice versa, treating the two-part prompt as a single-answer requirement.
2. Phonetic Approximation Bug: Due to Hindi-medium backgrounds, students may produce "Lactobasillis" or "Lacto-baccilus." If the rubric is strictly "Lactobacillus," these are marked wrong.
3. Retrieval Interference: Confusing the microorganism with the process (Fermentation) or the acid involved (Lactic Acid).
4. Language Barrier Bug: Misinterpreting "category" as a general descriptor (e.g., "useful" or "small") rather than a biological classification.

Step 3: Population Considerations
1. Recall vs. Recognition: The open-ended format is significantly harder for ESL/EWS students than a multiple-choice format.
2. High stakes for 0.5 marks: The low point value often leads to "economical" answers where students provide only one part of the required two-part answer.
3. Medium of Instruction: In Indian government schools, "Lactobacillus" is often taught as a rote-memorized fact, but the spelling and the distinction of its "category" remain high-frequency error zones.
