Step 1: Specific procedural steps
1.  Decoding/Translation: Correctly translate the academic English terms "significance" and "subcontinent" into conceptual schemas (likely mapping to Hindi terms like *mahatva* and *bharatiya upmahadveep*).
2.  Retrieval: Accessing long-term memory for physical or cultural geography facts related to the Himalayas (e.g., climate barrier, source of rivers, defense).
3.  Filtering: Selecting exactly "two" distinct points as required by the prompt structure, despite the rubric seemingly only rewarding one point for the maximum score (potential mismatch between prompt and rubric).
4.  Formulation: Encoding the conceptual points into written English sentences that are "plausible" and "distinct."
5.  Verification: Reviewing the output to ensure it answers the "impact/role" aspect of the question.

Step 2: Systematic procedural errors ("Buggy Rules")
1.  Attribute Substitution: Substituting "importance" with a mere description (e.g., "The Himalayas are very tall mountains") instead of an impact/significance.
2.  Partial Recall Error: Providing two related sub-points of the same category (e.g., naming two specific rivers) rather than "distinct" thematic points.
3.  Term-Confusion: Confusing "Himalayas" with general "mountains" or specific peaks (Everest), leading to irrelevant facts (e.g., "People like to climb it").
4.  Language Interference: Constructing sentences using Hindi syntax with English vocabulary that may result in unintelligible phrasing to a rigid grader.
5.  Omission of Linkage: Stating a fact (e.g., "There is snow") without stating the significance (e.g., "It provides water for perennial rivers").

Open-ended questions are significantly harder for these students than multiple-choice because their active English vocabulary is much smaller than their passive vocabulary. The transition from Grade 5-6 (descriptive) to Grade 7 (analytical/significance-based) is a frequent point of failure.
