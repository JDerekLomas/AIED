Step 1: Specific procedural steps
1. **Decode Lexical Triggers**: Translate English academic vocabulary ("equality," "guaranteed," "Constitution") into functional mental concepts (likely requiring translation into Hindi counterparts like *samanata*).
2. **Retrieve Constitutional Framework**: Recall the formal principle (e.g., Article 14 or the idea that "all are equal before the law").
3. **Map Abstract to Concrete**: Generalize the legal concept to identify a relevant real-world scenario (e.g., voting queues, mid-day meals, or public water access).
4. **Synthesize/Express**: Construct a written response in English (the medium of the test) that matches the logical structure of either an explanation or an example.

Step 2: Common "Buggy Rules" and Cognitive Errors
- **Literal Substitution Bug**: Students substitute "equality" with "same treatment for all," missing nuances of equity or specific legal protections.
- **Example Misalignment**: Providing a general example of "kindness" or "fairness" rather than a specifically *civic* or *constitutional* right (e.g., "helping a friend" instead of "equal right to vote").
- **Linguistic Scaffolding Failure**: Correct conceptual understanding exists, but the student lacks the English syntax to "Explain," leading to sentence fragments that don't meet the rubric's "correct explanation" threshold.
- **Partial Recall/Concept Narrowing**: Defining equality solely as "no untouchability" (a common memorized specific) without recognizing it as the broader concept asked for.
