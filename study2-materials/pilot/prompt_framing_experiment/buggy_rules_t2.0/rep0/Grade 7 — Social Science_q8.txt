Step 2: Systematic "Buggy Rules" (Procedural Errors)
1. Semantic Overgeneralization: Listing general rural activities (e.g., "going to school" or "cooking") instead of "occupations."
2. Keyword-Trigger Response: Seeing "Rajasthan" and "Rural" and describing a physical feature (e.g., "desert" or "camels") rather than an occupation.
3. Language Switching Failure: Being able to identify the concept in Hindi (Pashupalan) but lacking the specific English lexicon (Animal Husbandry), leading to an omitted answer or an ungradeable code-mix.
4. Descriptive Incompleteness: Providing the list but failing the second half of the procedural prompt (describing one in brief).
5. Concept Conflation: Listing an urban occupation mentioned in another chapter (e.g., "Industry") due to lack of local contextual differentiation.

Step 3: Target Population Analysis
Students from Hindi-medium backgrounds and economically weaker sections often have high conceptual knowledge of agriculture but low English literacy. Because this is an open-ended recall item (not a multiple-choice recognition item), the cognitive load is significantly higher. The "one-point" rubric for a multi-step task (list + describe) provides a low margin for error.

Step 4: Holistic Estimate
While the subject matter is culturally familiar, the requirement to name *two* occupations *and* describe one *in English* acts as a significant barrier for Grade 7 students with limited proficiency.
