Step 1: Procedural steps:
1. Lexical and semantic decoding: Correctly interpreting the phrase "maximum value... exact number of times" as a request for the Highest Common Factor (HCF).
2. Prime Factorization: Breaking down 75 into $3 \times 5 \times 5$ and 69 into $3 \times 23$.
3. Commonality Identification: Identifying common factors from the two sets.
4. Calculation: Multiplying common factors to find the HCF.
5. Unit Attachment: Expressing the final answer in 'kg'.

Step 2: Buggy rules/Systematic errors:
- Keywords trap: Seeing "maximum" and "total" and defaulting to Addition or Least Common Multiple (LCM) due to confusion between "Greatest" and "Least".
- Division bugs: Difficulty factoring 69 because 23 is a prime number that many students do not recognize quickly; they may assume 69 is prime or try to divide by small primes (2, 5) and fail.
- Factor Isolation: Failing to realize that the "weight" being sought is the common divisor, not the sum or difference.
- HCF vs. LCM Confusion: Executing the procedure for LCM ($3 \times 5 \times 5 \times 23$) instead of HCF.

Step 3: Population context:
Grade 6 students from economically weaker sections often face language barriers with English word problems. In Hindi-medium contexts, the technical vocabulary for HCF (*Mahattam Samapavartak*) is distinct; translating "maximum weight that can measure" into the HCF procedure requires a high level of conceptual abstraction. The open-ended nature of the problem offers no cues or distractors to narrow down the operation.
