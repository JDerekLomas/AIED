Step 2: Systematic "Buggy Rules"
1. **Mis-mapping Context (The "Geography Bug"):** Attributing factors of Indian nationalism (e.g., British salt tax, Gandhi) to Europe because the term "nationalist sentiment" triggers the most recent/strongest memory schema.
2. **The "Recognition-only Bug":** Student can identify the term "French Revolution" if seen in a list but cannot produce it via free recall.
3. **Keyword Overgeneralization:** Using "Liberty" or "Equality" as a generic answer for any history question without connecting it to European growth.
4. **The "Single-Factor Constraint":** Providing only one factor despite the prompt asking for two, or failing to "explain" because the maximum score is 1 (low incentive/confusion on depth).
5. **Syntax-Semantic Gap:** Knowing the concept in Hindi but being unable to translate "common language" or "culture" into a written English sentence.

Step 3: Population Considerations
The Grade 8 level is an inflection point where vocabulary becomes abstract. For students from Hindi-medium backgrounds, the linguistic barrier for "sentiment" and "contributed" is high. In EWS (Economically Weaker Section) contexts, rote memorization of "points" is common, but open-ended recall without prompts often leads to high omission rates.

Step 4: Holistic Analysis
The task requires high-level retrieval and linguistic encoding for a very low reward (1 mark). The mismatch between the "identify two" instruction and "explain one" rubric increases cognitive load. The likelihood of context-switching errors (Indian vs. European nationalism) is high for this demographic.
