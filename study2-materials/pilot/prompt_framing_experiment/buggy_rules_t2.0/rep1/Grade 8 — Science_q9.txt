Step 2: Systematic Buggy Rules
1. "Natural is Better" Bug: Students often systematically equate "organic/manure" with "better/stronger growth" regardless of the timeframe, leading them to predict Pot A will grow faster because manure is "healthy" and fertilizer is "harmful."
2. "Label-Preference" Bug: Associating "Gobar khaad" with traditional wisdom/familiarity, leading to an affective bias where the familiar substance is assigned the "positive" outcome (faster growth).
3. "Growth = Health" Bug: Failure to distinguish between *rate* of growth (speed) and *quality* of growth (sustainability/soil health).
4. "Linguistic Transfer" Bug: Students may understand the concept but lack the English syntax to express "comparative initial velocity," resulting in vague answers that don't meet rubric specificity.

Step 3: Population Considerations
1. English Proficiency: Many students may struggle to parse the nuances of "urea" vs. "manure" or explain the "why" in a second language.
2. Conceptual Prior: Indian curriculum often emphasizes the long-term benefits of manure and the environmental hazards of fertilizers. Without a specific focus on the *solubility* of Urea, students are cognitively primed to choose the "better" option (Manure) for all positive growth indicators.
3. Rubric Rigidity: The rubric requires Pot B to be identified as "faster," which contradicts common-sense classroom messaging about the superiority of organic farming.
