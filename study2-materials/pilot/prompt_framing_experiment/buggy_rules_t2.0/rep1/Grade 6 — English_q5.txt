Step 1: Specific procedural steps
1. **Decode the Prompt:** Parse the question syntax ("most important thing") and connect the English abstract concept of "importance" to the Hindi concept of "zaroori" or "mahatvapurna."
2. **Global Retrieval/Text Access:** Re-read or recall the poem's stanzas to identify value-based declarations.
3. **Thematic Abstraction:** Filter descriptive details to identify the "moral" or central claim (friendship) rather than concrete objects mentioned in the text.
4. **Synthesis:** Map specific poetic phrases (e.g., "a friend is like a treasure") to the general concept required by the rubric (value of friendship).
5. **Language Encoding:** Construct an English sentence that conveys "agreement" with the theme as per rubric requirements.

Step 2: Buggy Rules (Systematic Procedural Errors)
1. **Literalist/Concrete Bug:** Students search for the first physical noun mentioned or the most "exciting" concrete image rather than an abstract theme (e.g., naming a specific object from the poem instead of "friendship").
2. **Frequency Heuristic Bug:** Assuming the word repeated most often is the "most important thing," regardless of its semantic role.
3. **Personal Preference Overload:** Answering what is important to *the student* (personal opinion) rather than what is important to *the poet*.
4. **Keyword Matching Bug:** Copying a line that contains the word "thing" or "important" from the text, even if that line doesn't answer the question.
5. **Syntax Fragmentation:** Inability to transition from a mental Hindi concept ("Dosti") to a formal English written sentence, resulting in single-word answers that might not satisfy examiners looking for "agreement with themes."

Step 3: Target Population Analysis
Grade 6 students in EWS/Hindi-medium contexts often struggle with the transition from literal comprehension to thematic inference in a second language. The "open-ended" format removes the scaffolding provided by multiple-choice options. While the rubric is generous (accepting themes without evidence), the procedural hurdle of extracting an abstract theme from a second-language text and articulating it remains high.
