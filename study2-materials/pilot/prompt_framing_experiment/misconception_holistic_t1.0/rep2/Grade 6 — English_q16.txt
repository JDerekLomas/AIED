Step 2: List common misconceptions.
A common misconception in linguistic-mathematical logic for these students is "subset-superset confusion." A student might describe "Rainy days" instead of naming the season "Monsoon" or "Rainy Season." Because the rubric specifically awards the mark for *naming* the season, failing to use the specific noun would lead to an incorrect answer despite writing a paragraph. Another misconception is regarding the duration of a "season" versus a "weather event."

Step 3: Factors making the item easier.
The context is highly familiar and culturally relevant (Indian seasons are distinct). The rubric is extremely lenient; although the prompt asks for 6-8 lines, the student earns the full mark just by naming the season. For an EWS student with limited English, writing "I like Summer" is significantly easier than producing a 6-line paragraph.

Step 4: Factors making it harder.
The English language barrier is the primary obstacle. A student might know their favorite season in Hindi (e.g., "Sardi" or "Garmi") but struggle to recall or spell the English equivalent ("Winter" or "Summer"). The open-ended nature and the long instruction (6-8 lines) may intimidate a student with low English proficiency, causing them to skip the question entirely, unaware that naming the season alone would satisfy the rubric.

Step 5: Holistic estimate.
Given that the rubric only requires naming a season—a vocabulary word taught in early primary grades—most Grade 6 students, even from Hindi-medium/EWS backgrounds, will be able to identify at least one English word for a season (Summer, Winter, or Rainy).
