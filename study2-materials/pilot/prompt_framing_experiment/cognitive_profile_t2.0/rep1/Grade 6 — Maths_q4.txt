### Cognitive Difficulty Profile Analysis

**Concept Familiarity: Familiar**
The concept of LCM is a staple of the Grade 6 curriculum in India (NCERT/State Boards). While foundational, it is often taught through rote procedural methods. Students from Hindi-medium backgrounds recognize it as *Laghuttam Samapavartak (La.Sa.)*, though they frequently encounter the English abbreviation (L.C.M.) in exams.

**Computational Complexity: 2-3 Steps**
Using the standard common division (ladder) method, students must perform a series of prime factorizations and a final multiplication. For $\{12, 15, 20\}$, this involves 4-5 division steps and the final product $2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 5$.

**Common Error Traps: Minor**
The numbers 12, 15, and 20 are "highly composite," which helps, but common traps include:
*   Confusing LCM with HCF (Greatest Common Factor).
*   Arithmetic errors in the final multiplication.
*   Incomplete division (e.g., stopping when one number is still divisible).

**Format Demand: Open-ended**
This requires full recall of the algorithm. Since the rubric is "all or nothing" for 1 mark, students do not benefit from partial credit for correct prime factorization if the final calculation fails.

**Number Friendliness: Easy**
These are "benchmark numbers" frequently used in classroom examples. They are small, well-known in multiplication tables (up to 20), and lead to a clean integer result (60).
