**Step 2: List the most common misconceptions.**
*   **Fruit/Veg Sufficiency:** Many students believe fruits and vegetables are "ultimate" healthy foods; they may think his diet is perfect as is. (Leads to incorrect answer).
*   **Protein Sources:** Students might name meat or pulses instead of identifying what milk specifically provides (calcium/growth support). (Leads to incorrect answer).
*   **Confusing Diet with Calories:** Thinking "junk food" is the only thing ever "missing" or "wrong" in a diet. (Leads to incorrect answer).

**Step 3: Factors making the item easier.**
*   **Context:** Food and milk are extremely familiar daily life concepts for students in India, even in economically weaker sections where government milk programs (like Mid-Day Meals) often exist.
*   **Low Linguistic Barrier:** While the English is slightly awkward ("has ate"), the core keywords ("fruits," "vegetables," "no milk") are basic vocabulary.

**Step 4: Factors making it harder.**
*   **The "Double" Question:** The item asks "What is missing?" AND "Prepare a diet chart." However, the rubric only awards a point for the identification. A student might get overwhelmed by the second task and skip the first, or vice-versa.
*   **English Proficiency:** For Hindi-medium students, phrasing a full "diet chart" in English is a significant barrier, even if they know the answer is "milk/calcium."
*   **Open-ended:** There are no options to nudge the student toward the correct category.

The primary goal (identifying the missing component) is highly accessible because the prompt explicitly mentions "no milk."
