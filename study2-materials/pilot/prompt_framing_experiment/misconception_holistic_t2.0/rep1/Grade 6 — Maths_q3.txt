Step 1: Mathematical concepts and skills required:
*   Place value (up to thousands place).
*   Comparing numbers with different digits (3-digit vs. 4-digit numbers).
*   Understanding "ascending order" (small to large).
*   Numerical sequencing and scanning/matching.

Step 2: Common misconceptions:
*   "Longer is larger": Students may focus only on the leading digits (3, 5, 7, 4) and miss that 736 has fewer digits, mistakenly placing 736 last because 7 is larger than 3, 4, or 5. This would lead to an incorrect answer.
*   Misunderstanding "ascending": Reversing the order (descending). This would lead to an incorrect answer.
*   Vocabulary confusion: Many students in this context struggle with the terms "ascending" and "descending" in English, though they know the concept in Hindi (Aarohi Kram).

Step 3: Factors that make it easier:
*   Simple numbers: The numbers are distinct; there are no close values like 3367 and 3376 to cause "typo" errors.
*   Varying lengths: The presence of a 3-digit number (736) among 4-digit numbers provides a clear "smallest" starting point for those who understand place value.
*   Familiar task: Number sequencing is a core drill in primary grades.

Step 4: Factors that make it harder:
*   Open-ended format: No multiple-choice options to trigger memory.
*   Language barrier: If the student doesn't know what "ascending" means in English, they cannot complete the task despite having the mathematical skill.
*   Rubric quirk: The rubric only awards 0.5 marks, suggesting a low-stakes item that might be part of a longer list, which can lead to careless errors.
