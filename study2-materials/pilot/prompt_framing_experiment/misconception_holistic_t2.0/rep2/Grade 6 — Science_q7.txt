**Step 2: Common misconceptions.**
*   **The "Vegetarian" Misconception:** Students might believe "fruits and vegetables" represent a "complete" healthy diet because they are often messaged as "good." They may not realize that a missing food group (Proteins/Fats/Calcium) constitutes a deficiency. (Could lead to incorrect answers).
*   **Source of Nutrients:** Students may know they need "calcium" but fail to associate it specifically with milk in a written context. (Could lead to incorrect answers).
*   **Functional Understanding:** Many students may define a "balanced diet" as simply "eating enough" rather than "variety." (Could lead to incorrect answers).

**Step 3: Factors that make the item easier.**
*   **Cultural Context:** In India, milk is highly emphasized in government school Mid-Day Meal schemes and at home as a "complete food" for children.
*   **Simple Prompt:** The question tells the student what Kartik *has* eaten, making the exclusion easier to spot through process of elimination if they know basic food groups.

**Step 4: Factors that make it harder.**
*   **Language Barrier:** The phrase "has ate" (grammatical error in the source) and "missing in his diet" may be difficult for Hindi-medium students with limited English.
*   **Multi-part/Open-ended:** The rubric only awards points for identifying what is missing, but the prompt asks students to "Prepare diet chart." Students may spend too much time drawing the chart and forget to explicitly name the missing component, or vice versa.
*   **Cognitive Load:** Asking for a full chart for a 1-point question is high effort for low reward, which can lead to incomplete responses.

**Step 5: Estimate of correct proportion.**
Given the strong cultural emphasis on milk in the Indian context, most students will recognize it as the missing element.
