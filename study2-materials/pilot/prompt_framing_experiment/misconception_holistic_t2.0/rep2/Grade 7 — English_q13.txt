Step 2: Common misconceptions include:
- Phonetic vs. Spelling rule: Students may rely on the letter 'e' in 'elephant' (vowel) but might struggle if they perceive 'tiger' or 'zoo' differently. However, in this case, the spelling matches the sound.
- Over-generalization of "the": Students often use "the" for everything if they are unsure, or omit articles entirely because Hindi (the primary language) does not have equivalent grammatical markers for articles.
- Writing conventions: Students might correctly identify the articles but fail the rubric requirement to "rewrite the whole sentence" due to poor writing speed or attention to detail. This specific requirement is a major barrier for ESL students.

Step 3: Factors making it easier: The vocabulary is extremely simple ("elephant," "tiger," "zoo") and is taught in early primary years. The vowel-sound rule for 'an elephant' is the most commonly practiced example in Indian textbooks.

Step 4: Factors making it harder: 
- Language interference: Hindi speakers often omit articles because they feel redundant.
- Strict rubric: Requiring the *entire* sentence to be rewritten without transcription errors is difficult for students with limited English literacy. 
- Compound item: A student must get 3 out of 3 placements correct AND the sentence structure to earn 1 mark. A mistake in even one article results in a score of 0.
